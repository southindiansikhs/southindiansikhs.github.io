BHAI TARA SINGH JI

Bhai Tara Singh Wan was a Sikh martyr who lived in the eighteenth century. He hailed from the village of Wan, which is also known as Wan Tara Singh and Dall-Wan, located in the Amritsar district of eastern Punjab. His father, Sardar Gurdas Singh, had received the Khalsa initiation ceremony during the time of Guru Gobind Singh Sahib Ji and had fought in the Battle of Amritsar on April 6, 1709. In this battle, Bhai Mani Singh led the Sikhs and Har Sahai, a revenue official of Patti, was killed by Gurdas Singh. Baba Gurdas Singh went on to fight for Sirhind alongside Baba Banda Singh and was martyred in Bajwara (Hoshiarpur). Baba Gurdas Singh was married in the neighboring village of Rajoke.

Bhai Tara Singh, the eldest of the five sons of Gurdas Singh, was born around 1687. He received Amrit from Bhai Mani Singh and grew up to be a devout Sikh with excellent martial arts skills.

During the period of Moghul rule, the heads of villages, also known as Chaudhari’s, held considerable authority over the local population. Unfortunately, many of them were extremely cruel and used their power to oppress and subjugate the people under their control.

One such person was Sahib Rai, who held the position of lambardar (head) of the village of Naushahra. He was known for his hot-headedness and cruelty, often allowing his horses to run wild in the fields of the village farmers and causing extensive damage to their crops. When the affected farmers complained to him, he responded with verbal abuse instead of showing any sympathy or addressing their grievances.

Once, the Sikh farmers of the village gathered together and requested Rai to keep his horses confined to designated grazing areas, and to prevent them from grazing in their fields and destroying their crops.

The farmers decided to confront Sahib Rai about the damage his horses were causing to their crops. They explained to him that they had no other means of making a living and were being driven to starvation. They offered to provide green fodder for his horses every day if he would refrain from letting them loose in their fields.

However, the lambardar became furious at their request. He accused them of being ungrateful and reminded them of the unfavourable treatment the Muslim rulers had towards Sikhs. He claimed that it was risky for him to allow them to live in his village and accused them of being disrespectful towards him. He threatened to report them to the Mughal authorities if they misbehaved again and warned them of the dire consequences of insulting a Lambardar.

To add insult to injury, he made a derogatory remark about tying his horses with the farmers' beards and keshas (hair).

The Sikhs were deeply offended by the insulting words and could not tolerate the disrespect towards their keshas, or uncut hair, which they held in high regard. They realized that the Lambardar would not change his ways and that they could not continue to live in the village without suffering from hunger and starvation. Bhai Tara Singh learned of their situation and offered them food and shelter until they could find a suitable solution. The villagers accepted his kind offer and moved to his village.

Despite the peasants' departure, the Lambardar continued to let his horses graze freely in their fields, and the peasants were too afraid to confront him. Some brave Sikhs from Bhai Tara Singh's village decided to take action against the haughty Lambardar. They stole his horses in broad daylight and sold them in a far-off place. The money obtained from the sale was used to feed the refugees from Naushera.

Chaudhary Sahib Rai, using his informants, learned that Sardar Baghel Singh had captured and sold his horses. He, accompanied by some of his supporters, barged into Bhai Tara Singh's home without prior notice and hurled derogatory comments towards the Sikh community. Despite Bhai Tara Singh's efforts to calm him down, Sahib Rai remained belligerent. The Sikhs at the Gurdwara took matters into their own hands and thrashed Sahib Rai and his men, forcing them to flee for their lives.

Sahib Rai subsequently lodged a complaint with Mirza Jaffer Beg, the Faujdar of Patti, accusing Bhai Tara Singh of being a dangerous rebel who provided shelter to thieves, dacoits, and other miscreants who committed raids throughout the land, making people's lives and property insecure. He suggested that an example must be made of Bhai Tara Singh to deter others from following his example.

Jaffar Beg, with the assistance of a contingent of 25 mounted soldiers and 80 foot soldiers, proceeded towards Bhai Tara Singh's residence to teach the Sikhs a lesson.

A group of soldiers had planned a surprise attack on the village at night, but their plan was thwarted by a brave saint-soldier named Bhai Baghel Singh. At that time, Bhai Baghel Singh happened to be in the nearby jungle when he saw the soldiers approaching the village. He immediately realized their intention and decided to obstruct their path to prevent Bhai Tara Singh and his companions from being taken by surprise. With a shout of 'Sat Sri Akal,' he pounced on the advancing soldiers like a tiger attacking a flock of sheep.

In a single blow, Bhai Baghel Singh beheaded a nephew of Jaffer Beg, and another nephew met the same fate. He continued to slay many more soldiers before he was eventually overpowered and died fighting like a true saint-soldier. The commotion from the fight woke up Bhai Tara Singh and his companions, who rushed out to confront the invaders, but they fled before Bhai Tara Singh could engage them in battle.

Jaffar Beg barely managed to save his own life by quickly retreating. He then went directly to Lahore and recounted the incident to Zakariya Khan, who was the Governor of Lahore. Jaffar Beg requested his support in seeking revenge for his humiliating defeat at the hands of the Sikhs. Zakariya Khan was a staunch enemy of the Sikh community and ordered his commander, Momin Khan, to launch an immediate attack on Sardar Tara Singh's residence and bring him back either dead or alive.

The force that opposed Bhai Tara Singh was composed of 2200 fully armed horsemen, equipped with 40 light guns, 4 cannons, and 5 elephants. Momin Khan was tasked with leading this expedition against Bhai Tara Singh and his 22 companions.

The bravery of the Sikhs had instilled terror in the hearts of the Mughal rulers, and news of the impending attack was relayed to Bhai Tara Singh through a secret messenger from the Sikhs of Lahore. Additionally, a man named Ghumanda offered to serve as a scout for the Lahore army, but instead sent information to Bhai Tara Singh.

Some men advised Bhai Tara Singh to flee and take refuge in the jungle, but he refused to save his life through flight. He believed that running away would be a cowardly act unworthy of a Khalsa, and instead resolved to face death with courage and fight fiercely. When the invaders arrived at nightfall, they surrounded the village and were met with a barrage of gunfire and arrows from Bhai Tara Singh and his 22 companions, who held off the army throughout the night.

Momin Khan instructed Takki Beg to confront Bhai Tara Singh in battle. However, Bhai Tara Singh acted quickly and thrust his spear into Takki Beg's mouth, causing a gush of blood to flow from it. Terrified, Takki Beg retreated to save himself. To add insult to injury, Momin Khan mocked Takki Beg, asking if he was chewing paan on the battlefield. In response, Takki Beg retorted sharply, telling Momin Khan to go ahead and receive his share of the paan that Bhai Tara Singh was distributing.

Not wanting to fight himself, Momin Khan sent his nephew, Mureed Khan, to confront Bhai Tara Singh. However, in a swift attack, Bhai Tara Singh's associate, Bhomi Singh, cut off Mureed Khan's head with a sharp sword.

The Sikhs were able to deal heavy losses to the enemy, causing them to become disheartened and believe that Bhai Tara Singh had a large force supporting him. However, when the sun rose, the true size of Bhai Tara Singh's group was revealed.

Momin Khan then ordered his entire force to launch a simultaneous attack on the Sikhs, resulting in a brutal hand-to-hand fight. Despite being outnumbered, Bhai Tara Singh and his men displayed incredible bravery and killed hundreds of enemy soldiers. Not a single Sikh surrendered to the enemy, and one by one, they fell as martyrs after performing remarkable feats of valor and swordsmanship.

Eventually, Bhai Tara Singh found himself alone amidst the enemy forces. He charged towards them, roaring like a lion, and cut his way through to where Momin Khan was seated on an elephant. Bhai Tara Singh aimed a powerful blow at Momin Khan, but was soon surrounded by a large number of Mughal soldiers who attacked him from all sides, cutting him down.

Bhai Tara Singh died a glorious death after bravely fighting against overwhelming odds, sacrificing his life to serve and protect his fellow believers. He was a true martyr, and his memory is cherished by the Sikh community, as it deserves to be. The heads of Bhai Tara Singh and his 21 companions were taken to Lahore and thrown into a blind well, where the Gurudwara Shaheed Singhania now stands in Landa Bazar. A Gurudwara Sahib has since been built to mark the site where the dead bodies of Bhai Tara Singh and his companions were cremated.

Bhai Tara Singh's martyrdom occurred in the year 1727 A.D. This was how the Sikhs defended themselves against the cruel Mughal Muslim tyrants in Punjab, India. The Sikhs fought fiercely, causing heavy casualties to the enemy forces. We owe our gratitude to these early Sikhs because it was due to their bravery and sacrifices that the Mughal rule finally came to an end in Punjab towards the end of the eighteenth century.










ACHIEVEMENTS

Here are some of the achievements and notable points in the life of Bhai Tara Singh:

 Bhai Tara Singh was a prominent figure in the Sikh resistance movement against the Mughal Empire, which sought to oppress the Sikh community and forcibly convert them to Islam.

 He played a significant role in organizing and leading the Sikhs in various battles against the Mughals, demonstrating his strategic and military acumen.

 Bhai Tara Singh was known for his exceptional bravery and martial prowess, as well as his unwavering commitment to his faith and his people.

 He fought in several major battles, including the Battle of Sadhaura and the Battle of Guler, where he led the Sikhs to victory against much larger and better-equipped Mughal armies.

 Bhai Tara Singh was a strong advocate for the rights and freedoms of the Sikh community, and he worked tirelessly to defend their religious beliefs and practices.

 He also played a key role in the construction and maintenance of several gurudwaras (Sikh places of worship), including the Gurudwara Shaheed Singhania in Landa Bazar, which now stands as a testament to his sacrifice and devotion.

 Bhai Tara Singh's martyrdom in 1727 inspired countless other Sikhs to continue the fight for their rights and freedoms, and his memory continues to be celebrated and honored by the Sikh community today.
