GURTA GADDI DIVAS

Guru Granth Sahib Ji or Adi Sri Granth Sahib Ji, also known as the Adi Granth or Adi Guru Darbar, is not just a sacred text for the Sikh community, but it is revered as their living Guru. The holy book is quite extensive, spanning 1430 pages, and includes the actual words spoken by the founders of Sikhism, also known as the Ten Gurus of Sikhism. Additionally, it also comprises the words of various other saints from other religions, including Hinduism and Islam.

In 1708, Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the last of the living Sikh Masters, granted Guruship to Guru Granth Sahib Ji. Before his demise, Guru Ji instructed the Sikhs to treat the Granth Sahib as their next Guru, stating that "Sab Sikhan ko hokam hai Guru Manyo Granth," which translates to "All Sikhs are commanded to take the Granth as Guru." As a result, if you were to ask a Sikh today, they would tell you that they have a total of 11 Gurus, including 10 in human form and the SGGS (Guru Granth Sahib Ji).

During a visit to a Gurdwara, which is a Sikh temple, the main focal point in the Darbar Sahib or Main Hall is the Guru Granth Sahib Ji. The holy book is placed on a prominent platform and covered with an intricately designed and colorful cloth. The platform is always covered by a canopy, which is also decorated with expensive and visually appealing materials. The text within the Granth is written in a script called Gurmukhi, which translates to "From the Guru's mouth" and is considered a modern development of the ancient language Sanskrit.

HISTORY & COMPOSITION OF SRI GURU GRANTH SAHIB JI

Guru Nanak Dev Ji was instrumental in bringing the "Word of God" to life on Earth. Through his hymns and prayers, also known as Shabads, he inspired and encouraged people to live a life of truth, righteousness, and spirituality. These profound words were sung by his companions, Bala and Mardana, and by the Sangats or congregations that were formed around Guru Nanak Dev Ji.

In his later years at Kartarpur, it became a customary practice for members of the Sikh community to sing certain hymns on a daily basis. For instance, Japji is recited in the morning, while So Dar and So Purakh, the beginnings of Rehiras, are sung in the evenings.

Guru Angad Dev Ji, Guru Amar Das Ji, and Guru Ram Das Ji all composed Shabads or hymns, which were collected by the Sikhs in small books called Pothis. By reciting these Shabads, the Sikhs were able to channel the vibrations of the "Word of God" and attain a heightened state of consciousness. Through this meditative union with God and Guru, they were able to transcend their physical state and achieve a higher level of spiritual awareness.

STANDARDISATION OF SHABADS

Even in the early stages of Sikh history, there were issues with incorrect quotations and people claiming to be spiritual leaders. For example, Prithia, the elder brother of Guru Arjun Dev Ji, composed his own hymns and presented them as the writings of Guru Nanak Dev Ji. Despite the existence of authentic pothis containing Gurbani, there were many different collections of Shabads, leading to discrepancies in various versions of the same hymns.

Recognizing the need for a standardized and authenticated collection of the Guru's Bani or Gurbani to preserve the integrity of the Shabad, Guru Arjun Dev Ji took action. He realized that the most complete collection of Shabads from Guru Nanak Dev Ji, Guru Angad Dev Ji, and Guru Amar Das Ji was in the possession of Mohan, a son of Guru Amar Das Ji.

RETRIEVING THE POTHI FROM MOHAN

Guru Arjun Dev Ji wanted to obtain this collection of Shabads from Mohan's possession to create a standardized version of the Guru's Bani or Gurbani. He sent Bhai Gurdas to Mohan's home in Goindwal to request the collection, but Mohan was offended that he had been passed over for Guruship. Guru Amar Das Ji, Mohan's father, had seen the Divine Light in Guru Ram Das Ji and had chosen him as his successor. As a result, Mohan refused to answer the door when Bhai Gurdas arrived, and he returned to Guru Arjun Dev Ji empty-handed.

Guru Arjun Dev Ji then decided to personally visit Mohan’s house. When he arrived, he found Mohan in his usual state of deep meditation. Guru Arjun Dev Ji sat silently and waited until Mohan came out of his trance. Mohan apologized for not answering the previous requests and handed over the Pothis of Gurbani to Guru Arjun Dev Ji.

Guru Arjun Dev Ji then began the task of compiling the authentic Bani of the Gurus, including his own. He collected the writings of his predecessors, along with the Shabads of other Saints, and had them transcribed into one volume, called the Adi Granth. This process took over 25 years to complete, and the resulting volume was completed in 1604.

The Adi Granth became the most important scripture of the Sikhs and was later given the status of the living Guru, known as the Guru Granth Sahib Ji. The Granth Sahib is still revered and worshipped by Sikhs today as the embodiment of the Divine Light and the spiritual guide to lead one to the path of righteousness and salvation.

In 1603, Guru Arjun Dev Ji personally went to Mohan’s house to retrieve the Shabads. Instead of knocking, he called out in a sweet voice but received no response. The Guru then sat on the doorstep and sang the following lines:

“Oh, Mohan, your mansion is so lofty, there is no other place like yours.

Oh, Mohan, even the Saints adorn the door of your temple.

Show compassion and kindness, Oh Kind Lord—be merciful to the poor.

Says Nanak, I am thirsting for the Blessed Vision of Your Darshan, Grant me this gift and I shall be happy.”

These lines expressed the Guru’s admiration for Mohan’s home and temple and his hope for compassion and kindness from the Kind Lord. The Guru sought the Shabads to create a standardized, authenticated collection of the Guru’s Bani and preserve the integrity of the Shabad.

When Guru Arjun Dev Ji sang this hymn, he was praising God and trying to win Mohan's heart. Mohan is one of the names of God in Punjabi when invoking Him as the Beloved. Mohan opened the window and accused Guru Arjun Dev Ji of stealing the Guruship from his family and now attempting to take the remaining heritage.

Guru Arjun Dev Ji's response was filled with kindness and appreciation for Mohan's devotion to the One God and his exemplary behavior. Despite Mohan's protests and grumbling, Guru Arjun Dev Ji continued to sing in praise of God, calling upon Mohan to join the Sadh Sangat, the Company of the Holy, who meditate upon God and yearn for His Blessed Vision.

The Guru assured Mohan that those who worship God in thought, word and deed shall obtain His Gifts, and even the impure, the stupid and the foolish can attain Divine Knowledge upon seeing God. Guru Arjun Dev Ji concluded by stating that God is present within all and above all.

With the authentic collection of Shabads now in his possession, Guru Arjun Dev Ji embarked upon the task of compiling a standardized, authenticated collection of the Guru’s Bani, which came to be known as the Aad Granth. This compilation included the writings of not only the first five Gurus, but also of the Bhagats, the Hindu and Muslim saints whose hymns spoke of the same Truth. The Aad Granth became the cornerstone of Sikhism, serving as the living Guru for the Sikhs, to guide them on the path to the realization of the Divine. It was completed in 1604, and installed at Harmandir Sahib Ji (the Golden Temple) in Amritsar, where it remains to this day.

After receiving the Shabads from Mohan, Guru Arjun Dev Ji began the process of compiling them into a single volume, which would come to be known as the Adi Granth. He carefully reviewed each Shabad, separating the authentic ones from those added by imposters. Bhai Gurdas was the scribe who recorded the words of Guru Arjun Dev Ji during this process. When he asked the Guru how he could discern between the true and false Shabads, Guru Arjun Dev Ji replied with an analogy, saying that just as a mother cow recognizes the cry of her calf amidst a herd of cows and calves, the true Shabad resonates truly and can be distinguished from the false.

Guru Arjun Dev Ji not only compiled the Shabads of the first four Gurus into the Adi Granth, but also included his own Shabads and those of thirty-six Hindu and Muslim Saints such as Kabir, Ravi Das, Naam Dev, Trilochan, and Sheikh Farid. This was a groundbreaking move as it was the first time any religion had incorporated the works of devotees of other religions into its scripture, demonstrating the universality of thought that underlies the Sikh belief in One God and the oneness of humanity as God's children.

Guru Arjun Dev Ji intentionally left blank pages in the Granth, with the understanding that a future Guru would add Shabads in their proper place at the proper time. Ultimately, the Shabads of Guru Teg Bahadur Ji, the ninth Guru, were added by Guru Gobind Singh Ji, completing the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji.

FIRST INSTALLATION OF THE HOLY GRANTH SAHIB JI

In 1604, the Adi Granth was completed and installed in the Golden Temple, with Baba Buddha appointed as the Guru's Granthi. Guru Arjun Dev Ji instructed his Sikhs to treat the Adi Granth with the same respect they give him, as it embodied the Guru. Initially, Guru Arjun Dev Ji placed the Adi Granth on his own bed and slept on the floor. The words in the Granth were written without any spaces or breaks, which makes it difficult for most people to read and comprehend nowadays.

Guru Gobind Singh Ji accepted the challenge and dictated the entire Granth Sahib Ji to Bhai Mani Singh Ji, his scribe, at Talwandi Sabo. The Granth Sahib Ji dictated by Guru Gobind Singh Ji is known as the Damdama Sahib Bir. It is said that the Guru wrote a special verse in the Granth Sahib to commemorate this event, which reads, “The foolish one who claims to be a king, is merely a puppet in the hands of fate. The True King is he who stands by Truth, and bows in reverence to the Lord.” The Damdama Sahib Bir is one of the most respected copies of the Granth Sahib Ji, and is kept at the Takht Sri Damdama Sahib, in the town of Talwandi Sabo in Punjab.

GURU GOBIND SINGH JI RECREATES HOLY GRANTH

Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the tenth and final human form of the Sikh Gurus, dictated the entire Granth Sahib at Talwandi Sabo, now known as Damdama Sahib. Despite Dhir Mal, the son of Baba Gurditta and grandson of Guru Hargobind Singh Ji, refusing to give the Adi Granth to Guru Gobind Singh Ji when he requested it, the Guru proceeded to dictate it to Bhai Mani Singh, who recorded it on paper. While some have questioned the authenticity of this story, it is important to note that Guru Gobind Singh Ji was not an ordinary person, and in the past, it was not unusual for bards and storytellers to recite entire epic poems from memory. In a time when many people were illiterate, oral traditions were crucial, and the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji Sahib was set in the form of music and rhythm to make it easier to remember, much like the Qur’an and the Gita.

Guru Gobind Singh Ji added the Shabads of his father, Guru Teg Bahadur Ji , to the Granth Sahib, but he did not include his own Shabads; instead, he compiled them into a separate Granth, the Dasam Granth. The Dasam Granth is not considered as revered as the Guru Granth Sahib Ji. The task of rewriting the entire Guru Granth Sahib Ji was finally completed in 1705, and the "Damdama Sahib Bir," as it is now called, was taken to Nanded, where it was installed.

INSTALLATION OF PERPETUAL SIKH GURU

Guru Gobind Singh Ji established the expanded version of the Adi Granth as the Guru on October 20, 1708. This day is now celebrated as Guru Gadi Divas (Enthronement Day). Before his death, Guru Gobind Singh Ji declared that the Word of God embodied in the Siri Guru Granth Sahib would be the Guru for all time. He said, “O Beloved Khalsa, let any who desire to behold me, behold the Guru Granth. Obey the Granth Sahib, for it is the visible body of the Guru. Let any who desire to meet me, diligently search its Bani.” In this way, the Word of God, which had manifested as the Guru in Nanak and had passed through the ten incarnations of the Guru, was now returned to its form as the Word, the Bani, the Shabad.

STRUCTURE OF GURU GRANTH SAHIB JI

The final page of the bir (volume) of the Guru Granth Sahib Ji, which was handwritten by Pratap Singh Ji Giani, is currently located on the first floor of Sri Harimandir Sahib Ji in Amritsar.

STRUCTURE OF SGGS

The Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji contains 1430 pages and includes shabads (hymns) arranged in thirty-one Ragas, traditional Indian musical measures and scales. The shabads of the Sikh Gurus are ordered within the Ragas, followed by those of the Hindu and Muslim Saints. The shabads are written in various meters and rhythms, and are organized accordingly, such as Ashtapadi (eight steps) or Panch-padi (five steps). The Gurmukhi script is used to write the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji, and the shabads are composed in various languages, including Punjabi, Sanskrit, and Persian.

INDEX OF GURU GRANTH SAHIB JI

The original version of the Guru Granth Sahib Ji did not include an index. However, some modern prints of the bir now include one, making it easier to locate common banis. For instance, the index reveals that Japji Sahib begins on page 1 and ends on page 8; Sukhmani Sahib is located from pages 262 to 296; Bara Maha bani can be found on pages 133 to 136; and the bani called Anand Sahib (Bliss) is located from pages 917 to 922, among others.

THE BEGINNING

The Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji opens with the sacred phrase “Ek Onkar” which means The All Pervading Being. From this phrase to the tenth one “Gur-parshad” is known as the Mool Mantra. Following this is the Japji Sahib composed by Guru Nanak Dev Ji Dev, which includes 38 stanzas or Pauris, a Prologue, and an Epilogue. This is recited as a morning prayer by the Sikhs.

The subsequent composition consists of two parts – (1) “So Dar” and (2) “So Purkh”. The Bani “So Dar” comprises 5 Shabads and “So Purkh” contains 4 Shabads. These two compositions form the main part of the evening prayer of the Sikhs, known as the Rehras. After this, the Bani called Sohila (also known as Kirtan Sohila) follows, which consists of 5 Shabads and is recited as the bedtime prayer.

THE CLOSING SECTION

After the main section consisting of 31 chapters forming the Raga section, there is a brief closing section in the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji. This section includes the Mundavani (The Closing Seal), which is a salok written by Guru Arjan Dev Ji, and the final composition of the SGGS, known as the Ragamala.
