ARPINDER KAUR

Arpinder Kaur, a 28-year-old flight instructor based in San Antonio, Texas, has become the first turbaned Sikh woman to be hired as a commercial airline pilot in the United States. With support from advocacy groups for Sikhs, flying can now be another opportunity for young Sikhs. Kaur's hiring by American Airlines Corporation (AMR) in March 2008 as a First Officer followed the resolution of the issue of wearing her dastaar, with the help of the Sikh Coalition. She filed a grievance for accommodation of her religious article of faith based on American Airlines’ allowance of “regulation approved hats”. An agreement was reached that is consistent with state and federal anti-discrimination law. In June 2008, she completed her pilot training program and is now flying Embraer Jets for American Eagle, the largest regional airline system in the world, which is part of AMR and is based out of the Dallas-Fort Worth International Airport.

Kaur's dream of becoming a pilot began when, at the age of 15, during her first flight from India to the US, she asked a stewardess on her British Airways flight if she could sit in the cockpit. The pilots allowed her to sit and observe for an hour, and that was all it took for her to realize that being a pilot was what she wanted to do. Despite the hardships, including earning a 4-year degree in Information Systems and her mother’s belief that it was too dangerous for a girl to be a pilot, Kaur chose to follow her passion while using it as a means of supporting her family. Kaur said it was the love and support of her husband, Pritpal Singh, that pushed her forward on the path toward becoming a pilot. Kulbir Singh Sandhu, a captain with AMR, mentored her throughout her aviation career.

Harinder Singh, executive director of the Sikh Research Institute (SikhRI) in San Antonio, Texas, said, “This is a great day for the Sikhs in America. Religious accommodation, not assimilation, is what the founders of this great nation envisioned, and we are thrilled that American Airlines celebrates the rich religious and cultural diversity of all American populations.”

WOMEN IN AVIATION

In the U.S., only a little over six percent of commercial pilots are women, as reported by the Federal Aviation Administration's 2015 Active Civil Airmen Statistics. This makes female pilots a rare sight, and Arpinder Kaur's achievement as the first female Sikh pilot in the U.S. is like feathers on an elephant. She is also the first Sikh pilot of any gender to wear a turban on the flight deck. American Eagle/Envoy, the airline she works for, deserves commendation for evaluating her request and recognizing her religious right to wear her turban, or dastaar as it is known in the Sikh community.

Setting this precedent has brought Arpinder into the spotlight, where she has received well-deserved press coverage, acknowledging her achievement as a woman and as a Sikh pilot in the U.S. She even represented Sikh Americans in the 2015 Rose Parade in Pasadena, California, on their first-ever float commemorating over 125 years of Sikh history in America.

Arpinder feels a sense of responsibility as the first turban-wearing Sikh pilot. It is important to her to help other Sikhs realize they can take on any challenge and hold any position in the U.S. with or without their turban. More importantly, she feels responsible for teaching young girls the importance of following their dreams, regardless of gender roles. "My main thing is to tell them, you are able to do what boys are able to do," says Arpinder. "We have so many strengths. We're capable of so many things. I tell them to pursue those dreams, because you're able to become what you want to become."

ACHIEVEMENTS

Fortunately, Arpinder took her own advice and is now a Captain with Envoy on the E145. She finds it gratifying to witness the smiles on passengers' faces during the holidays when they reach their destinations safely. Moreover, she takes immense pride in her sons, Insaf and Jivat, writing about her in their school journals or drawing pictures of her with wings on her shirt. She is grateful to have a family, a career she has always aspired to, and the freedom to practice her religion as she sees fit.

As a Sikh woman, Arpinder chooses to wear the dastaar. She believes that Sikhism, which lacks gender pronouns in their literature, unites men and women as followers of the religion. The turban could be seen as a perfect metaphor for Arpinder's pursuit of her aviation dreams. As a pilot, the unifying factor is to bring people home safely and the passion for flying, which has no gender constraints.

Arpinder states, "This turban is a symbol of my career path. That's why I am so grateful to [Envoy] for granting me that accommodation, allowing me to spread this message to the community and telling girls that anything is achievable."
