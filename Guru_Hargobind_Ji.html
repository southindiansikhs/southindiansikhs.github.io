GURU HARGOBIND SINGH JI

Guru Hargobind Singh ji, who is revered as one of the ten Gurus of the Sikh religion, was born on June 19, 1595. He became the Guru at the tender age of eleven, after his father, Guru Arjan Dev Ji, was executed by the Mughal emperor Jahangir.

To safeguard the Sikh community and in response to his father's execution, Guru Hargobind Singh ji introduced the process of militarization to Sikhism. He symbolized this by wearing two swords, which represented the dual concepts of Miri and Piri, i.e., temporal power and spiritual authority. In Amritsar, he constructed the Akal Takht, which is located in front of the Harmandir Sahib. The Akal Takht is regarded as the highest seat of earthly authority of the Khalsa, the collective body of the Sikhs, to this day.



BIOGRAPHY

Guru Hargobind Singh Ji was born into a Sodhi Khatri family in the village of Guru Ki Wadali, located 7 kilometers west of Amritsar. He was the only son of Guru Arjan Singh Ji, who was the fifth Sikh Guru. As a child, he contracted smallpox but survived. According to orthodox Sikh hagiographies, his uncle Prithi Chand attempted to poison him, and he also survived a cobra attack.

Guru Hargobind Singh Ji received religious education from Bhai Gurdas and trained in swordsmanship and archery with Baba Budda. In 1606, Guru Arjan Singh Ji, five days before his death, appointed Guru Hargobind Singh Ji as his successor and instructed him to establish a military tradition to protect the Sikh people. Shortly after the succession ceremony, Guru Arjan Dev Ji was arrested, tortured, and killed on the orders of Mughal Emperor Jahangir.

To honour his father's wishes, Guru Hargobind Singh Ji put on two swords, representing his spiritual and temporal authority, respectively. He also surrounded himself with armed Sikhs for protection, as advised by his father. The number 52 held special significance for him, and his retinue consisted of 52 armed men. This marked the beginning of the military tradition in the Sikh faith.

Guru Hargobind Singh Ji had three wives: Damodari, Nanaki, and Marvahi, and children from all three. His eldest sons from the first wife died during his lifetime, while his son from Mata Nanaki, Guru Tegh Bahadur Singh Ji, became the ninth Sikh Guru. The Guru himself was a skilled martial artist and encouraged physical fitness among his followers, preparing them for combat.

He had his own Darbar, and he armed and trained some of his devoted followers. His army, known as the Risaldari, consisted of 300 horsemen and 60 musketeers, and he owned 700 horses.

After leading the Sikh community for several years, Guru Hargobind Singh Ji nominated his grandson, Har Rai, as his successor and the seventh Guru. Guru Hargobind Singh Ji passed away in 1644 in Kiratpur Sahib, a town located on the banks of the Sutlej river. He was cremated on the banks of the same river, where the Gurdwara Patalpuri Sahib now stands.



















LEGACY

The main highlights of Guru Ji's life are as follows:

• He introduced martial arts and weapons training and created a standing military force to defend the Sikh community after his father's martyrdom.

• He carried two swords, representing Miri and Piri.

• He constructed the Akal Takht in 1608, which is now one of the five Takhts (Seats of Power) of the Sikh religion.

• He founded the city of Kiratpur in District Rupnagar (formerly known as Ropar), Punjab.

• He was held in the fort of Gwalior for one year, ostensibly to pray for the recovery of the ill Emperor Jahangir (the Guru had willingly gone to the fort). When Jahangir ordered his release, he refused to leave unless 52 imprisoned Hindu Rajas were also freed. He cleverly earned their freedom by turning the Emperor's own words against him. To mark this occasion, Sikhs celebrate Bandi Chorr Divas to celebrate his release and return to Amritsar.

• He was the first Guru to engage in warfare, fighting and winning four defensive battles against Mughal forces.

• The city of Hargobindpur, in the Majha region of Punjab, is named after him, which he won over from the Mughals after defeating them in a battle.


ARMINNG AND MARTIAL TRAINING

Guru Arjan Dev Ji, a peaceful and saintly figure, was subjected to extreme torture during his captivity. Despite the ordeal, he concentrated and relied on God for guidance to save the nascent Sikh Sangat from annihilation. The only solution that came to him was to use arms to defend the Sangat. He pondered over the problem continuously until he finally concluded that the militarization of Sikhism had become necessary. Therefore, Guru Arjan Dev Ji sent a Sikh disciple to his young son, eleven-year-old HarGobind, to nominate him as the Guru of the Sikhs. He gave him the final instruction to sit fully armed on his throne and maintain an army to the best of his capacity.

Guru Hargobind Singh Ji had a great talent for managing state affairs and his Darbar (court) was known for its grandeur. He began to arm and train some of his devoted followers, and with the acquisition of seven hundred horses, his Risaldari (army) grew to three hundred horsemen and sixty gunners. He also recruited five hundred infantrymen from the Majha region of Punjab. Guru HarGobind Ji constructed a fortress in Amritsar called Lohgarh (Fortress of Steel). He had his own flag and a war-drum that was beaten twice a day. Those who had worked to have Guru Arjan executed now focused their efforts on convincing Jahangir that the Lohgarh fort, the Akal Takht, and the growing Risaldari army were all intended to facilitate Guru HarGobind Ji's revenge for his father's wrongful death.

AKAL TAKHT

In 1606, Guru Har Gobind Ji constructed the Akal Takht (God's throne) in front of Harmandar Sahib Ji. He sat on a raised platform of twelve feet, dressed in princely attire. Harmandir Sahib Ji was the seat of his spiritual authority, and the Akal Takht was the seat of his temporal (worldly) authority. This marked the beginning of Sikh militarization, with symbols of sovereignty such as the umbrella and the Kalgi added to the traditional symbols of sainthood. As a king would, Guru Har Gobind Singh Ji administered justice, granting honors and imposing punishments. The Akal Takht was the first Takht in Sikh history. According to Cunningham, "The genial disposition of the martial apostle led him to rejoice in the companionship of a camp, in the dangers of war, and in the excitements of the chase."

RELATION WITH THE EMPEROR

Those who harbored ill will towards the growing Sikh community were alarmed by the rapid growth of the Sikhs under the guidance of Guru Hargobind Ji. They joined hands with the rulers of Lahore and traveled to Delhi to voice their complaints against Guru Hargobind Singh Ji to the power brokers in the Mughal court, and even to Jahangir himself. They told Jahangir that Guru Hargobind Sahib Ji was gathering an army and amassing arms with the intention of avenging his father's death. They advised him to suppress Guru Ji and the growing Sikh community immediately.

Jahangir, however, decided to summon the Guru to Delhi to assess his character and aims. Rather than the expected confrontation and arrest of the Guru, a surprising thing happened when both the Emperor and his powerful wife were taken by Guru Ji's charm, grace, and godliness. A friendship and mutual respect soon followed, and Guru Hargobind Singh Ji even went on hunts with the Emperor on his grand shikars. On one remarkable occasion, the young Guru saved the life of the Emperor, whom he could have easily hated for the death of his father, by jumping between a lion and the Mughal ruler.

Chandu Shah and his associates, who had plotted to harm Guru Ji, grew fearful of his developing friendship with the Emperor Jahangir. To prevent this, they used an illness of the Emperor to have court astrologers predict that only the prayers of a holy man at a shrine in Gwalior Fort would lead to the Emperor's recovery. Jahangir, influenced by superstition and his court astrologers, ordered the Guru to be imprisoned at the Gwalior Fort, despite his growing friendship with the Emperor. The Guru's Sikhs were worried about his safety, but he himself was not concerned about his release. Eventually, Pir Hazrat Mian Mir reminded Jahangir of the Guru's confinement, and he ordered the Guru's release. However, the Guru refused to leave unless the fifty-two Prince who had been imprisoned at the fort were also released.

WARS WITH MUGHALS

Guru Hargobind Singh Ji had several reasons for arming his followers. The external and internal situations were changing, and the Guru's policies needed to adjust accordingly. The development of Sikhism had mostly occurred during the tolerant reign of Akbar, who had even supported the Gurus in various ways. However, the execution of Guru Arjan Dev Ji and imprisonment of Guru Hargobind by Jahangir showed that harsher times were coming, and peaceful organization was no longer enough. Both Gurus understood that the Sikh community could not be protected without the aid of arms. Guru Hargobind Singh Ji had 800 horses in his stable, and his Risaldari consisted of 300 horsemen and 56 matchlock-men for his personal safety.

Risaldari consisted of 300 horsemen and 56 matchlock-men for his personal safety. Jahangir was unable to tolerate the armed policy of Guru Hargobind and subsequently imprisoned him. However, reports from across the country revealed that people were against the throne due to the Guru's popularity and the unjustified martyrdom of the fifth Guru. Many people were following Sikhism, and there was a possibility of a coup if the Guru was not released immediately. At the time, there were 52 Hindu kings in the Gwalior prison, and Jahangir's policies against the local majority people were oppressive in nature. Therefore, the situation compelled him to order the release of Guru Hargobind in order to save the throne.

During the reign of Shah Jahan, relations between the Mughal Empire and the Sikhs became bitter once again. Shah Jahan was intolerant and destroyed the Sikh baoli at Lahore. Quarrels that began over hawks or horses between Mughal officials and the Sikhs escalated into large-scale uprisings that led to the deaths of thousands on both sides. Battles were fought at Amritsar, Kartarpur, and other places. Guru Hargobind and his devoted soldiers defeated the Imperial troops near Amritsar. Although the Guru was attacked again by a provincial detachment, he and his soldiers routed the attackers and slayed their leaders. Guru Hargobind personally led his soldiers against the troops of the empire and fearlessly opposed and overcame the provincial governors and personal enemies.

PEOPLE HOSTILE TOWORDS YOUNG GURU JI

When Guru Hargobind Singh Ji took over as the leader of the Sikhs, he faced hostility from many individuals. One of them was his uncle, Priti Mal, who was the brother of Guru Arjan Dev Ji. Priti Mal had previously attempted to kill Guru Hargobind Singh Ji when he was a child, by setting a deadly snake upon him. Despite his failed attempt, Priti Mal continued to conspire against Guru Hargobind Singh Ji and complained about him to Emperor Jahangir. Additionally, Chandu Shah, who had played a key role in getting Guru Arjan Dev Ji arrested and killed, also directed his animosity towards Guru Hargobind Singh Ji . Another person who was hostile towards the Sikh Gurus was Shaikh Ahmad Sirhindi, and he too incited Jahangir against Guru Hargobind Singh Ji . Jahangir himself was worried that Guru Hargobind Singh Ji might seek revenge for his father's arrest, torture, and execution.




SIKHISM

Guru Hargobind Singh Ji sent his followers to distant regions like Bengal and Bihar to spread the teachings of Sikhism. He permitted the Udasis to propagate Sikhism, but did not accept them as part of the Sikh community. Bhai Gurdas mentions in his Var the names of Nawal and Nihala, two Sabharwal Khatris who established their business in Bihar and inspired many locals to adopt Sikhism. In his personal life, Guru Hargobind Singh Ji remained committed to the principles of Guru Nanak Dev Ji, whose successor he was and whose teachings he spread throughout the world.




SAMARTH RAMDAS JI AND GURU HARGOBIND SINGH JI

According to Sikh tradition, there is an old Punjabi manuscript called Panjah Sakhian, which states that Samarth Ramdas met Guru Hargobind Singh Ji in Srinagar, located in the Garhval hills. This meeting is also mentioned in a Marathi source, Ramdas Swami's Bakhar, written in 1793 by Hanumant Swami. It is believed that the meeting took place in the early 1630s, during Samarth Ramdas's pilgrimage travels in the north and Guru Hargobind Singh Ji 's journey to Nanakmata in the east.

As they came face to face with each other, Guru Hargobind Singh Ji had just returned from a hunting excursion and was fully armed while riding a horse. Ramdas asked him about the type of sadhu he was, given that he had heard that Guru Hargobind Singh Ji occupied the Gaddi of Guru Nanak Singh Ji Singh Ji . In response, Guru Hargobind Singh Ji described himself as internally a hermit and externally a prince. He further explained that arms were meant for the protection of the poor and destruction of tyrants. He also stated that Guru Nanak Dev Ji Singh Ji had not renounced the world but had renounced Maya.
