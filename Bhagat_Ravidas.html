BHAGAT RAVIDAS JI

Bhagat Ravidas Ji was a poet, devotee, and a Gurmukh, born on Maghshudhi 14 Pooranmashi in Smt.1456, which is equivalent to January 30, 1399 in Kashi. He believed in monotheism and was a follower of Gurmat. He greatly admired Kabir Ji and Namdev Ji. Many of Bhagat Ravidas Ji's devotional hymns can be found in the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji, where they are placed under the Bani Bhagatan ki. Ravidasis consider Bhagat Ravidas Ji as their Guru, and they have a separate sect from the mainstream Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs. Bhagat Ravidas Ji was a tanner by profession.

Sikhs hold the teachings of Bhagat Ravidas Ji in high regard and consider him to be one of their spiritual leaders. His teachings share many similarities with those of Bhagat Kabir Ji, Guru Nanak Dev Ji, and the Bhatts. All of these spiritual leaders are revered as Gurus by Sikhs and their teachings are included in the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji. Sikhism emphasizes the importance of respecting and learning from the wisdom of many spiritual teachers who share similar beliefs about God.

EARLY LIFE

Ravidas' parents were prosperous leather traders and lived near the city of Varanasi. When he was just five days old, Bhagat Ramanand visited their home and blessed the child. As Ravidas grew up, his father urged him to join the family business, but Ravidas was more interested in pursuing spiritual and humanitarian values. He used the money his father gave him for the business to help saints and the needy. His father was greatly upset by this and eventually expelled Ravidas from the house. Despite being married at the time, Ravidas didn't hold any grudges against his father and instead left the house peacefully with his wife. They lived in a makeshift hut and Ravidas started a small shoe repair shop by the roadside.

DEVOTION TO THE LORD

Despite being banished from his house, Bhagat Ravidas Ji did not abandon his devotion to God. He built a temple with clay walls and a thatched roof, and installed an idol made of hide inside. His intense devotion and universal love drew hundreds of people from all castes to join him in worship. This, however, led to envy among the Brahmin priests, who raised the issue in the court of the Muslim Nawab of Kashi. The Nawab, who was known for his fairness, decided to put the matter to a miraculous test.

During the miraculous test conducted by the Muslim Nawab of Kashi, Bhagat Ravidas recited one of his hymns (Gauri Purbi P.346) in deep meditation and prayed to Almighty to dispel his doubts. The Almighty answered his prayer, and his devotion was acclaimed as a triumph in the test. Overjoyed with gratitude, he sang his hymn (Asa P.1606), "Thou art sandal and I am the poor castor-plant, dwelling close to thee. From a mean tree I have become sublime and Thine fragrance, exquisite fragrance, now, abides in me."

During the early fourteenth century, Muslim dominion had firmly established in India. Bhagat Ramanand (1366-1467 AD) settled in Kashi/Benares and observed that a significant number of Hindus had converted to Islam and embraced it as their religion. As an orthodox follower of Ram, Ramanand was drawn towards the Islamic faith's emphasis on the oneness of God and the equality of all humans, regardless of their class. The Muslims, except for the ruling feudal elite, disregarded the caste system and mingled across all levels of society, be it living, eating, religious ceremonies, or marriages.

Bhagat Ramanand was deeply troubled by the growing number of conversions of low-caste Hindus into Islam, which he witnessed after settling in Kashi/Benares during the 14th century when Muslim rule was well-established in India. Despite being an orthodox devotee of Ram, he was impressed by the Islamic belief in One God and the equality of men. He abandoned his Shivaite austere practices taught to him by his Guru Ramanuj and embraced the worship of Universal Brotherhood. He welcomed low-caste Hindus and even invited Muslims to worship with him and become his followers. Kabir, a Muslim weaver, Sain, a barber, and Dhanna, a cultivator, were among his most notable disciples, each of whom became a Guru (teacher) with their own followers.

Legend has it that a wealthy man once tried to tempt Bhagat Ravidas with the promise of riches, giving him a philosopher's stone that could turn any object into gold with just a touch. Despite Ravidas's refusal of the gift, the man left the stone hanging from the ceiling of Ravidas's home. When the man returned a year later, he found that the stone was still in the same position. He then proclaimed to the world the unshakable godliness of Bhagat Ravidas. This incident is often seen as a test of Ravidas's resistance to worldly desires, but some versions of the story suggest that it was actually a ploy by the Brahmin priests to discredit him, which ended up backfiring and proving Ravidas's worth.

Ravidas's devotion and love for humanity without regard to caste spread widely, and even a noble woman, Maharani Jhally of Chitaur, was captivated by his teachings. She became his disciple and abandoned her luxurious lifestyle, despite the disapproval of the Brahmin priests and her husband, the Maharana. When the Maharana invited Ravidas to Chitaur and asked him to participate in an oblation, the Brahmin priests refused to eat while a cobbler was seated in the same column of rows. Ravidas voluntarily moved away, but miraculously every person distributing food appeared to look just like Bhagat Ravidas to the Brahmins, leading to the Maharana becoming an ardent devotee. Ravidas stayed in Chitaur for a long time and it is said that even Mira Bai became his disciple during that period. According to some accounts, Ravidas died at the age of almost 120 years in Benares. This episode is considered to be an ecclesiastic test to judge Bhagat Ravidas's endurance towards worldly love. However, some accounts associate this to the devious manipulation of the Brahmin priests to discredit Ravidas, which ultimately resulted in his triumph.

Guru Arjan Dev Ji made the decision to include the works of Ravidas in the Shri Guru Granth Sahib Ji. In the Sikh holy book, Ravidas is referred to as Bhagat Ravidas and there are 41 of his verses included, mostly in clear Hindi. Ravidas's poetry is full of passionate love for God, the Universe, Nature, the Guru, and the Name. His use of sarcasm and pique reveals his intimacy with God.

UNTOUCHABILITY

Bhagat Ravidas was a trailblazer in motivating his followers to challenge discrimination based on caste. His charismatic deeds made him an important figure in the world, and his followers, who refer to themselves as Ravidasis, continue to use the term "Guru" to refer to him across India today. He inspired his followers to reject the label of "untouchables" that had been imposed on them by others, and through his own actions, he demonstrated that he was closer to God than those who deemed him to be of a "low caste". Ravidas was also one of the first to openly criticize and attack the Hindu caste system.

THE LIFE AND TEACHINGS OF BHAGAT RAVIDAS JI

Today, Bhagat Ravidas is a highly respected Hindu reformer. BJP Scheduled Caste Morcha president Bangaru Laxman, in the Organizer (6-8-1995), accused Congress leader Sitaram Kesri of disrespecting Dalit saints like Ravidas, Satyakam Jabali, Sadhna Kasai, Banka Mahar, Dhanna Chamar, and others who protected Hindus against foreign attacks, by bracketing Dalits with minorities as "victims of Hindu oppression."
