KHUSHWANT SINGH

Khushwant Singh, born as Khushal Singh on 2 February 1915, was a renowned Indian author, lawyer, diplomat, journalist, and politician. He gained inspiration from his personal experience during the 1947 Partition of India, which led him to write his most well-known novel, Train to Pakistan in 1956, which was adapted into a film in 1998.

Singh was born in Punjab and received his education from Modern School in New Delhi, St. Stephen's College, and Government College in Lahore. He later studied at King's College London and received an LL.B. degree from the University of London. He was admitted to the bar at the London Inner Temple and worked as a lawyer at the Lahore High Court for eight years. Upon India's independence from the British Empire in 1947, he joined the Indian Foreign Service. In 1951, he was appointed as a journalist at All India Radio and later moved to the Department of Mass Communications of UNESCO in Paris in 1956. These experiences ultimately led him to pursue a literary career.

As a writer, Singh was widely known for his sharp secularism, humor, sarcasm, and his love for poetry. His writings often included comparisons of the social and behavioural characteristics of Westerners and Indians, with a touch of acid wit. Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, he served as the editor of various literary and news magazines, as well as two newspapers. Additionally, between 1980 and 1986, he served as a Member of Parliament in the Rajya Sabha, which is the upper house of the Parliament of India.

Khushwant Singh received the Padma Bhushan award in 1974 for his contributions to Indian literature, but he returned the award in 1984 to protest against Operation Blue Star, a military operation carried out by the Indian Army in Amritsar. In 2007, he was honoured with the Padma Vibhushan, the second-highest civilian award in India, in recognition of his significant contributions to Indian literature, journalism, and public life.

EARLY LIFE

Khushwant Singh was born in Hadali, Khushab District, Punjab (now in Pakistan), into a Sikh family. He was the younger son of Sir Sobha Singh, a prominent builder in Lutyens' Delhi, and Veeran Bai. However, births and deaths were not recorded at that time, so his father simply made up the date of 2 February 1915 for his enrollment at Modern School, New Delhi. Khushwant's grandmother, Lakshmi Devi, asserted that he was born in August, so he later adopted 15 August as his birthday. His uncle, Sardar Ujjal Singh (1895–1983), had previously served as Governor of Punjab and Tamil Nadu.

Khushwant's birth name, given by his grandmother, was Khushal Singh, meaning "Prosperous Lion." He was called by the pet name "Shalee." However, at school, his name earned him ridicule from other boys who mocked him with the expression "Shalee Shoolee, Bagh dee Moolee," meaning "This shalee or shoolee is the radish of some garden." He chose the name Khushwant to rhyme with his elder brother's name, Bhagwant, but he later admitted that the new name was "self-manufactured and meaningless." He later discovered that there was a Hindu physician with the same name, and the number of Khushwants subsequently increased.

Khushwant Singh enrolled in Delhi Modern School in 1920 and continued his studies there until 1930. It was during this time that he met his future wife, Kanwal Malik, who was one year his junior. From 1930 to 1932, he studied Intermediate of Arts at St. Stephen's College in Delhi. He then pursued higher education at Government College, Lahore, obtaining his BA degree in 1934 with a "third-class degree." In 1938, he went to King's College London to study law and was awarded an LL.B. degree from the University of London. He was subsequently called to the bar at the London Inner Temple.

CAREER

Khushwant Singh began his professional career as a lawyer in 1939, practicing in Lahore at the Chamber of Manzur Qadir and Ijaz Husain Batalvi. He worked in Lahore Court for eight years, where he collaborated with some of his best friends and admirers, including Akhtar Aly Kureshy, Advocate, and Raja Muhammad Arif, Advocate. After India gained independence in 1947, he joined the Indian Foreign Service. He began his service as an Information Officer of the Government of India in Toronto, Canada. Later, he served as Press Attaché and Public Officer for the Indian High Commission in London and Ottawa for four years. In 1951, he became a journalist with All India Radio. From 1954 to 1956, he worked in the Department of Mass Communication at UNESCO in Paris. In 1956, he turned his attention to editorial services. He founded and edited Yojana, a government journal in India from 1951 to 1953, as well as The Illustrated Weekly of India, a newsweekly, and The National Herald. He was also appointed as the editor of Hindustan Times on the recommendation of Indira Gandhi.

During his time as editor, The Illustrated Weekly became India's leading newsweekly, with its circulation increasing from 65,000 to 400,000. However, nine years later, just a week before he was due to retire, Singh was asked to leave "immediately" by the management. A new editor was appointed the same day. After Singh's departure, the weekly suffered a significant decline in readership. In 2016, Khushwant Singh was posthumously recognized in the Limca Book of Records as a tribute.

POLITICS

Khushwant Singh served as a member of Rajya Sabha, the upper house of the Indian parliament, from 1980 to 1986. He received the Padma Bhushan award in 1974 for his contributions to his country, but returned it in 1984 as a form of protest against the Indian Army's siege of the Golden Temple. In 2007, he was awarded the Padma Vibhushan by the Indian government.

As a public figure, Singh was accused of showing favoritism towards the ruling Congress party, particularly during the time of Indira Gandhi's leadership. When Gandhi declared a nationwide emergency, he publicly supported it and was mockingly referred to as an "establishment liberal".

Singh's belief in the Indian political system was shaken by the anti-Sikh riots that took place after the assassination of Indira Gandhi, in which some high-ranking Congress politicians were alleged to be involved. However, he continued to remain optimistic about the potential of Indian democracy and worked with the Citizen's Justice Committee, which was founded by H. S. Phoolka, a senior advocate of the Delhi High Court.

Singh was a strong advocate for developing diplomatic relations with Israel, even at a time when India did not want to offend Arab nations where many Indians were employed. He visited Israel in the 1970s and was impressed by the country's progress.

HONOURS AND AWARDS

Khushwant Singh was the recipient of numerous awards throughout his life, including:

• Rockefeller Grant in 1966

• Padma Bhushan by the Government of India in 1974 (which he returned in 1984 in protest against the government's siege of the Golden Temple, Amritsar)

• Honest Man of the Year by Sulabh International in 2000

• Punjab Rattan Award by the Government of Punjab in 2006

• Padma Vibhushan by the Government of India in 2007

• Sahitya Akademi Fellowship by the Sahitya Academy of India in 2010

• All-India Minorities Forum Annual Fellowship Award by Uttar Pradesh Chief Minister Akhilesh Yadav in 2012

• Lifetime achievement award by Tata Literature Live! The Mumbai Litfest in 2013

• Fellow of King's College London in January 2014

• The Grove Press Award for the best fiction.
