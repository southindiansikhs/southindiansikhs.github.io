KARAM SINGH

Subedar and Honorary Captain Karam Singh PVC was an Indian soldier who was awarded the Param Vir Chakra (PVC), India's highest award for gallantry. Singh began his military service in 1941, participating in the Burma Campaign of World War II. During the Battle of the Admin Box in 1944, he demonstrated outstanding bravery and was recognized with the Military Medal. Singh also fought in the Indo-Pakistani War of 1947, where he played a crucial role in safeguarding a forward post at Richhmar Gali, located south of Tithwal. For his heroic actions, he was awarded the PVC. Singh was also selected as one of the five soldiers to hoist the Indian flag for the first time after independence in 1947. He eventually rose to the rank of subedar and was granted the honorary rank of captain before his retirement in September 1969.


EARLY LIFE

Karam Singh was born on September 15, 1915 in Sehna village, which is located in the Barnala district of Punjab, British India. His father, Uttam Singh, was a farmer. Although Singh had initially planned to pursue farming, he was inspired by the stories of World War I veterans from his village and decided to join the army. After completing his primary schooling in his village, he joined the Army in 1941.

MILITARY CAREER

On 15 September 1941, Karam Singh enlisted in the 1st battalion of the Sikh Regiment. He was awarded the Military Medal for his bravery and valor in the Battle of the Admin Box during the Burma Campaign of World War II. As a young and decorated sepoy, he gained the admiration and respect of his fellow soldiers in his battalion. In 1947, he was among the five soldiers handpicked by the then Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru to hoist the Indian flag for the first time after independence.

WAR OF 1947

After India gained independence in 1947, a conflict broke out between India and Pakistan over the princely state of Kashmir. The Pashtun tribal militias of Pakistan crossed the border into the state, occupying several villages, including Tithwal, which was strategically important for India due to its location on the Line of Control in the Kupwara Sector.

On 23 May 1948, the Indian Army captured Tithwal from the Pakistani troops. However, the Pakistanis quickly launched a counter-attack to recapture the area. The Indian troops, unable to withstand the attack, withdrew to the Tithwal ridge, where they prepared to regain their positions at the right moment.

As the battle at Tithwal continued for months, the Pakistanis grew desperate and launched a massive attack on 13 October, hoping to drive the Indians from their positions. Their primary objective was to capture Richhmar Gali, located south of Tithwal, and the Nastachur Pass, east of Tithwal.

During the fierce battle at Richhmar Gali on the night of 13 October, Lance Naik Karam Singh was commanding a 1 SIKH forward post. Despite being outnumbered ten-to-one by the Pakistani troops, Singh and his men repelled their attacks multiple times. With their ammunition running out, Singh ordered his men to join the main company, knowing that reinforcement was impossible under Pakistani shelling. With the help of another soldier, he brought two injured men along, though he himself was wounded.

Under heavy Pakistani fire, Singh moved from position to position, boosting the morale of his men and intermittently throwing grenades. Even after being wounded twice on both hands, he refused evacuation and continued to hold the first line of trenches.

During the fifth wave of attacks, two Pakistani soldiers closed in on Singh's position. Singh jumped out of his trench and killed them with his bayonet, greatly demoralizing the Pakistanis. Singh and his men then successfully repelled three more enemy attacks before the Pakistani troops finally retreated, unable to capture their position.

PARAM VIR CHAKRA

On 21 June 1950, Karam Singh was awarded the Param Vir Chakra, India's highest military decoration. The citation for his award stated that Lance Naik Singh showed extraordinary courage during the Battle of Tithwal in Jammu and Kashmir in 1948. On 13 October 1948, when the enemy launched a brigade attack to retake Richmar Gali and bypass Tithwal, Singh was commanding a section at Richmar Gali. Despite heavy shelling and accurate enemy fire, he went from bunker to bunker, providing assistance to the wounded and urging his men to fight. The enemy launched eight separate attacks that day, and in one such attack, they managed to obtain a foothold in the platoon locality. Severely wounded, Singh led a counter-attack with a few men and evicted the enemy after close quarter combat, accounting for many enemy dead by using his bayonet. The citation praised Singh's leadership and indomitable spirit.

Singh's statue at Param Yodha Sthal, National War Memorial, New Delhi

Singh was later promoted to the rank of JCO jemadar (later naib subedar), then to subedar, and finally to subedar-major. He received an honorary commission in the rank of captain on 26 January 1969, and retired from the army in September of the same year. Singh was also one of the five soldiers who had the opportunity to raise the Indian flag for the first time on Independence Day, which is a day of great respect in India. On this day, people show respect to the Indian flag and sing the national anthem.

OTHER HONOURS

During the 1980s, the Ministry of Shipping, under the Government of India, operated the Shipping Corporation of India (SCI). As a tribute to the PVC recipients, SCI named fifteen of its crude oil tankers after them. One such tanker, the MT Lance Naik Karam Singh, PVC, was delivered to SCI on 30 July 1984 and served for 25 years before being decommissioned. In addition, the government constructed a memorial in Sangrur's District Administrative Complex to honor Lance Naik Karam Singh's memory.
