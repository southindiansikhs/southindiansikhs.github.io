CAPTAIN MOHAN SINGH

General Mohan Singh was an officer in the Indian military who played a significant role in the Indian Independence Movement. He is best known for his leadership and organization of the First Indian National Army in South East Asia during World War II. After India gained independence, Mohan Singh became a Member of Parliament in the Rajya Sabha (Upper House) of the Indian Parliament and served in public life. He passed away in 1989.





EARLY LIFE

Mohan Singh was born and brought up in Badiana, a district near Sialkot, which is now in Pakistan. He was born into a Sikh family as the only son of Tara Singh and Hukam Kaur. Sadly, his father passed away just two months before his birth, and his mother moved to her parents' home in Badiana, where Mohan Singh was raised.









MILITARY CAREER

After completing his high school education, Mohan Singh joined the 14th Punjab Regiment of the Indian Army in 1927. Following his recruit training at Hrozpur, he was posted to the 2nd Battalion of the Regiment, which was serving in the North-West Frontier Province at the time. In 1931, he was selected as a potential officer and received six months' training at Kitchener College in Nowgong, Madhya Pradesh, followed by another two and a half years at the Indian Military Academy in Dehra Dun. After completing his training, he received his commission in 1934 and was posted to a British unit, the 2nd Border Regiment, for a year. He was then posted to the 1st Battalion of his former 14th Punjab Regiment, which was stationed at Jhelum at the time. In 1939, World War II broke out.

By the time the war broke out, Mohan Singh had been promoted to captain, and his battalion was selected for operational service in the Far East. They were still undergoing intensive training in Secunderabad when Mohan Singh got married to Jasvant Kaur, the sister of one of his fellow officers, in December 1940. On March 4, 1941, he left for Malaya with his unit.

SECOND WORLD WAR

In December 1941, Japan entered the war by launching a surprise attack on the American airbase at Pearl Harbor, Hawaii, and subsequently took over the entire South East Asia in a matter of weeks. In October of the same year, the Japanese Imperial General Headquarters established the Fujiwara Kikan (also known as the F-kikan) in Bangkok, with the mission of gathering intelligence and making contact with the Indian independence movement, the overseas Chinese, and the Malayan Sultanate in order to encourage cooperation and friendship with Japan. The F-kikan was headed by Major Fujiwara Iwaichi, chief of intelligence of the 15th army, and had a staff of five commissioned officers and two Hindi-speaking interpreters. Their initial contact was with Giani Pritam Singh, a leader of an organization, who introduced them to Mohan Singh. Major Fujihara and Pritam Singh asked Mohan Singh to form an Indian Army consisting of captured Indian soldiers. Though initially hesitant, Mohan Singh ultimately agreed to the proposal. Fujihara handed over around 40,000 Indian soldiers, who had surrendered to him, to Mohan Singh, which marked the first step towards the formation of the INA.

MALAYAN CAMPAIGN

During World War II, the British forces in the northern part of the Malaya Peninsula, including Captain Mohan Singh's battalion, 1/14 Punjab Regiment, were retreating towards the south as they were outmatched by the Japanese forces at Jitra. Mohan Singh himself was captured by Japanese troops after several days in the jungle and was taken to Alor Star to meet with Major Fujiwara and Giani Pritam Singh at a joint office of the F-Kikan and the IIL. Fujiwara was known for his commitment to the values that his office was meant to convey to the expatriate nationalist leaders, and he found acceptance among them. He was later self-described as "Lawrence of the Indian National Army," after Lawrence of Arabia.

INDIAN NATIONAL ARMY

After being taken captive by Japanese troops, Mohan Singh was convinced by Major Fujiwara to betray his oath to the British Crown and unite with the Japanese mission for the greater motive of Indian freedom. Fujiwara convinced him with the promise that he would be treated as an ally and friend, rather than as a prisoner of war. In December 1941, Mohan Singh met with the Japanese commanding general and was convinced of the feasibility of raising an armed Indian unit. Singh, Pritam Singh, and Fujiwara began contacting Indians in the British Indian Army in South-East Asia, and also began recruiting from amongst those captured by the Japanese in Malaya. All Indian prisoners of war and stragglers were placed under his charge and he was asked to restore order in the town of Alor Star. Thus, the nucleus of what came to be the Indian National Army was born.

Kuala Lumpur fell on 11 January 1942 with 3,500 Indian prisoners of war, and Singapore fell on 15 February with 85,000 British troops, of whom 45,000 were Indians. Mohan Singh asked for volunteers who would form the Azad Hind Fauj (Free India Army) to fight for the liberation of India from British rule. A large number of men came forward to join, and by 1 September 1942, when the new set-up came into being, the strength of volunteers had reached 40,000. Mohan Singh, now designated as a general, was to command it. During a conference held at Bangkok during 15–23 June 1942, the Indian Independence League under the leadership of Rash Behari Bose had been inaugurated. Through one of the 35 resolutions passed by the conference, Mohan Singh was appointed commander-in-chief of the "Army of Liberation for India," i.e. the Indian National Army.

POST 1947

Mohan Singh's vision for Indian liberation was ultimately realized on August 15, 1947, when India gained independence, but it was also accompanied by the partition of the country into India and Pakistan. Due to this, Mohan Singh was forced to leave his home in what became Pakistan and became a homeless refugee in India. He was eventually allotted some land in the village of Jugiana near Ludhiana where he permanently settled. Mohan Singh then became involved in politics and joined the Indian National Congress. He served as a legislator in the Punjab for a period of time and was later elected to the Rajya Sabha, the upper house of the Indian Parliament, for two terms. Both inside and outside of Parliament, he worked tirelessly to have the members of his Azad Hind Fauj recognized as "freedom fighters" who fought for the liberation of their nation.





ACHIEVEMENTS

The achievements of Captain Mohan Singh:

 Captain Mohan Singh was a captain in the British Indian Army, who later became a major in the Indian National Army.
 He played a key role in the formation of the Indian National Army (INA) during World War II, which aimed to fight against British rule in India.
 He persuaded Indian prisoners of war and stragglers to join the INA, which ultimately grew to a strength of 40,000 volunteers.
 Mohan Singh was appointed as the first commander-in-chief of the Indian National Army.
 After India gained independence from British rule in 1947, Mohan Singh entered politics and joined the Indian National Congress.
 He served as a legislator in the Punjab and was elected to the Rajya Sabha, the upper house of the Indian Parliament, for two terms.
 He fought for the recognition of the members of the Azad Hind Fauj as "freedom fighters" in the cause of India's liberation.
 Mohan Singh received the Padma Bhushan, India's third-highest civilian award, in 1954 for his contribution to India's freedom struggle.
