BHAGAT FARID JI

There are 134 hymns in the Guru Granth Sahib Ji that are credited to Sheik Farid. Some Sikh scholars believe that these hymns were actually written by Farid Shakar Ganj, a follower of the Sufi Qutbuddin Bakhtiyar Kaki who lived between 1173 and 1265 AD (or 569-664 AH). It is said that Sheikh Brahm (also known as "Farid Sani" or "Farid the 2nd"), who was the tenth person to hold Farid Shakar Ganj’s position, was also met twice by Guru Nanak Dev Ji.

However, Max Arthur McAuliffe, a well-known scholar of Sikh Lore, states that the hymns attributed to Farid were actually written by the latter Farid. Some scholars believe that the hymns were written by different Sufis from the Pak Pattan center who all used the name Farid as it was customary in those times to choose the most suitable devotee of the leader to take their place shortly before their death.

SHAKAR GANJ / SHEIKH FARID (1173-1265 A.D)

Baba Farid of Pak Pattan, originally named Farid-ud-Din Masaud, was given the name "Shakar Ganj" by his mother due to a miraculous incident. As a young boy, his mother used to hide sugar crystals under his prayer-carpet as a reward for completing his prayers. One day, when his mother was away, he prayed for an extended period of time and found a larger supply of sugar under his carpet as a result. He shared the sugar with his friends and told his mother about the incident upon her return. She realized that it was a gift from God and gave him the surname "Shakar Ganj", which means "treasury of sugar". Baba Farid is primarily known by this name and is revered for his devotion and spiritual teachings.







GREAT DEAL WRITTEN ABOUT FARID THE 1ST

There is a substantial amount of information available on the original Shaikh Farid. The Jawahir-i-Faridi, a text on his life, provides two different genealogies for Farid, who later became known as Farid Shakar Ganj. One genealogy relates to his spiritual lineage, while the other pertains to his family lineage.

Farid received his spiritual position from his priest Khwaja Qutub-ul-din Bakhtiyar Ushi of Dihli, whose spiritual lineage can be traced back in an unbroken line to the Prophet of Makka. On the other hand, Farid's family lineage has been traced back to Hazrat Amir-ul-Mumanin Umr-bin-ul Khitab Qureshi Makki Faruqi, who was the second Khalifa of the Muslims.

THE ROBBER BECOMES BLIND

Nizam-ul-Din Auliya, who was a disciple of Farid, tells a story about a robber who went to the house of Farid's mother to steal. As he began his theft, he suddenly lost his sight. Realizing that there must be a saint or miracle-worker present, he made a vow that if his sight was restored, he would give up stealing and become a good Muslim. Hearing his vow, Miriam (Farid's mother) prayed for him and his sight was miraculously restored.

The robber went back to Miriam's house the next morning with an offering of milk and expressed his desire for himself, his wife, and children to become Muslims. Miriam fulfilled his wish and they all became devout Muslims. When Miriam asked for his name, he said it was Chawa. His shrine, along with others in the area, has since become a place of pilgrimage.

FARID JI'S BIRTH

When Farid's mother was pregnant with him, she spent all her time in prayer. He was born in Kothiwal on the first day of Ramzan, the most sacred month in the Muslim religion, in A.H. 569 (1173). On that night, the sky was dark and cloudy, and the moon, which marks the beginning of Ramzan, was not visible in the sky. As a result, the devotees did not know when to start their fast (as the start of Ramzan is marked by the sighting of the new moon).

Then a holy man arrived and announced that a wonderful son had been born to Jamal-ul-Sulaiman. He said that if the infant suckled, it meant that the time for fasting had not yet begun, but if he refused the breast, then all good Muslims should fast. Farid did not suckle, so it was apparent that the fast had begun. During the entire month of Ramzan, it is said that the infant only took milk at night in the Muslim fashion and fasted during the day.

HOT WATER FOR HIS MASTER

According to a legend passed down by disciples of Sheikh Farid, there is an important episode from the time when he was serving his spiritual mentor Khawaja Qutubudin Bakhtiyar Kaki. Sheikh Farid used to arrange hot water for his master's ablutions. One very wet and cold, stormy night, he realized he had no way to light a fire, as everything around was either cold and humid or wet. So he left the sanctuary in search of fire, and kept walking through the dark rainy night, becoming quite wet and starting to shiver, but he did not give up his search. Finally, he perceived a light in the distance and started walking towards it. He eventually arrived at the door of a building and knocked on it, and a voice asked who it was.

After Sheikh Farid revealed his name, a woman answered the door and asked him why he had arrived at a brothel. Sheikh Farid explained that he was led there by the light of a lamp and was looking for a way to light a fire to heat water for his master's ablutions. The woman unabashedly told him that she would not give him the fire for free and asked if he had any way to pay her. He replied that he did not have any money. The woman then said that if he needed fire, he must give her what she asked for.

Sheikh Farid asked for her price, and she replied that she would like one of his "eyeballs" as payment. Sheikh Farid then asked for a knife and unhesitatingly pulled out his eyeball and gave it to the woman. In return, she gave him some burning wood to take away.

Sheikh Farid returned to the sanctuary and resumed his duties, as before. The next morning, when his master was giving a discourse to an audience, Sheikh Farid appeared with a bandage covering his bleeding eye. Khawaja Qutubudin Bakhtiyar Kaki asked him why he had a bandage on his eye. Sheikh Farid replied in Punjabi, "Akh Aa Gayee Ay," which means his eye was infected and oozing. His master smiled and said, "You are right, open your bandage now." In Punjabi, "Akh Aa Gayee Ay" also means "the eye has come."

Sheikh Farid had a bandage over one of his eyes, but when he removed it and rubbed his eye, he discovered that his missing eyeball had miraculously returned. His master then remarked that the new eyeball was slightly smaller than the original one that God had given him, and he was unable to compete with God, so he had obtained a smaller one. It is believed that whoever succeeds Sheikh Farid spiritually will also have one eye smaller than the other.

FAMILY OF FARID THE 2ND

Shaikh Brahm had two sons, one of whom was named Shaikh Taj-ul-Din Mahmud, who was a renowned saint. The other son was named Shaikh Munawwar Shah Shahid. Shaikh Brahm had several disciples, including Shaikh Salim Chishti Fatahpuri, who was a holy man that prophesied the birth of Akbar's son. Other disciples included Shaikh Ahmadi of Chunian, Baba Ahmad Lanak of Dipalpur, Maulvi Jalal-ul-Din of Shaikhbad, Shah Abdul Fatah of Ghazipur, and Haji Niamat Ulla of Shaikhupur, among others.

Shaikh Brahm passed away on the 21st of Rajab, in the year 960 A.H. (1552 A.D.), after a spiritual reign of 42 years. According to Kaulasat-ul-Tawarikh, he was buried in Sarhind.

THOUGHTS ON FARID'S BANI

O Raven, you have searched my skeleton, and eaten all my flesh. But please do not touch these eyes as I hope to behold my Beloved. (91)

In one of his writings, Farid ji compared human desires to a bird, saying, "O Shaykh Fareed, you can attain the Lord today if you can control your bird-like desires which keep your mind restless. ||1||Pause||" Therefore, the mention of a raven or crow in the particular line of Bani is a metaphor for the desires that arise within the mind.

QUOTES

Baba Farid was born on the first day of the holy month of Ramadan in the year 1173 CE, in the town of Kothiwal in Punjab. His given name was Farid-ud-Din Masaud, and he later earned the moniker "Shakar Ganj." However, he is most commonly known and revered as Baba Farid of Pak Pattan. Baba Sheikh Farid was born during a tumultuous period in Punjab's history. At the time of his birth, Punjab was undergoing a series of invasions and pillages by various conquerors such as Tamarlane (Taimur the Lame), Halaku (son of Chengez Khan), Mohammed Ghouri, and Mahmud Ghazanvi.







VIOLENT RULERS IN INDIA

During the period in which India was ruled by the Slave Dynasty under Qutb-Ud-Din Aibak, the official languages were Turkish and Persian. Sultan Balban was the head of the dynasty at that time. Prior to this period, several centuries ago, the sword of Islam had spread throughout the Western regions of the Indian subcontinent, including Iran, Afghanistan, and Central Asia.

In India, Qutb-Ud-Din Aibak was successful in establishing a lineage of rulers who governed a significant portion of the country from Delhi for several decades. Following this, Sufi saints from Arabia and other regions arrived to spread their message of love for Allah. Notable among these Sufi saints was Khwaja Qutub-Ud-Din Bakhtiar Kaki, a Syed of the Jaffri Hussaini tribe, who was widely known and revered.

BABA FARID'S MEETING

Khwaja Bakhtiar Kaki was born around 1150 CE and received his education from Abu Hafiz, a renowned scholar from Ush. He later went to Ajmer and became a disciple of Khwaja Moin-ud-Din Chishti. After spending some time there, he traveled to Delhi where he met Baba Farid and became his disciple. Even the Emperor Sultan Shams-ud-Din Iltutmish was counted among his disciples.

Khwaja Bakhtiar Kaki passed away in the year 1235 CE and was laid to rest in Delhi. His tomb is highly revered by both Hindus and Muslims. His descendants are known as Chishtis, and they hail from his priestly tribe. This information is recorded in Makhazan-ul-Tawarikh.

GENEALOGY OF BABA JI

According to the genealogy recorded in the Jawahir-e-Faridi (The gems of Farid), which is preserved at the shrine of Pak Pattan, Baba Sheikh Farid ji was descended from Farrukh Shah, who was the king of Kabul and other states were subject to him, including the kings of Ghazni. Baba Farid ji's great-grandfather was the son of Farrukh Shah, but he was killed by Halaku, the grandson of Chengez Khan, during his invasion of Kabul. Halaku killed several princes and learned men, including several of Baba Farid’s ancestors. In the aftermath of these events, Baba Farid’s grandfather, Shaikh Shaib, abandoned their country and took refuge in the Punjab in CE 1125.

MOVED TO PUNJAB

The Qazi of Kasur, who was aware of Shaikh Shaib's high position in Kabul, received him and his family with great respect and hospitality. Later, Shaikh Shaib moved to Multan, where he thought he would be less exposed to worldly influences and the temptations of ambition. He settled in Kothiwal, now called Chawali Mushaikh, near Dipalpur. There, he founded a private college for religious education and gained much attention. His eldest son, Jamal-ud-din, married Bibi Miriam, the daughter of Syed Muhammad Abdula Shah, a descendant of Ali. Bibi Miriam gave birth to three sons - Khwaja Aziz-ud-din, Farid-ud-Din Masaud (Baba Farid), and Khwaja Najib-ud-din - and one daughter, Khatun Jamila.

BECOMES HAJJI AND OBTAINS SCHOLARSHIP

At the age of 16, Baba Farid went to Hajj and stayed with Abdul Rahim Ansari, where an unkempt faqir predicted his future greatness upon hearing him speak Punjabi. Upon returning to Punjab, he was sent to Delhi to learn theology under Khwaja Qutub-ud-Din Bakhtiar Kaki. However, as Baba Farid was found to be deficient in scholarship, he was sent to the shrine of Abdul Shakur of Sarsa, near Delhi to complete his education. During this time, Baba Farid repeated a verse expressing his desire to walk in God's way and become like holy men.

After gaining a high reputation in Delhi, Baba Farid became tired of the attention and went to Hansi, where he stayed for some time. When Khwaja Qutub-ud-Din Bakhtiar Kaki passed away, Baba Farid returned to Delhi and assumed his spiritual guide's mantle.

MOVED TO PAK PATTAN

After leaving Delhi, Baba Farid entrusted the college he had founded to Jamal-ud-Din of Hansi and proceeded to Ajodhan, which is now known as Pak Pattan. The town's name was changed due to a canal that passed nearby, where visitors to Baba Farid would wash their hands and feet. Sheikh Farid turned Pak Pattan into a great center of Sufi thought, attracting people from all over India and the Middle East. Despite his education in Arabic, Persian, and other languages, he always used Punjabi, the language spoken by common people, and wrote his couplets in Punjabi or Persian script. Baba Farid refused money but accepted gifts of food and other items for the public kitchen. During a return trip to Delhi, he was warmly welcomed by Emperor Nasir-ud-Din Balban, who introduced him to his family. Baba Farid eventually married the emperor's daughter, Hazabra, but only after Balban agreed not to give any costly gifts. Baba Farid distributed all of the jewelry and other gifts he received to the poor.

THE FATHER OF PUNJABI LITERATURE

Baba Sheikh Farid Shakarganj is rightfully considered as the pioneer of Punjabi literature, which makes it older than the literature written in Hindi, Urdu, and other languages. It was Baba Farid's use of Punjabi that inspired writers like Tulsidas, Mira Bai, and others to write religious literature in Hindi. Thus, Baba Sheikh Farid Shakarganj's contribution to Punjabi language and literature is invaluable, and his legacy continues to inspire generations of Punjabi writers and poets.
