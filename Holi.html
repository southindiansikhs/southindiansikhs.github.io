HOLA MAHALLA

Hola Mahalla is a Sikh festival celebrated on the first day of the lunar month of Chet, which typically falls in March. This festival follows the Hindu festival of Holi by one day, as per the tradition established by Guru Gobind Singh Ji. "Hola" is the masculine form of the feminine sounding "Holi".

The term "Mohalla" originates from the Arabic root hal (alighting, descending) and is a Punjabi word that refers to a structured procession resembling an army column. However, unlike Holi, where people playfully sprinkle colored powder, dry or mixed in water, on each other, Guru Gobind Singh Ji established Hola Mohalla as an occasion for Sikhs to display their martial prowess in simulated battles.

Together, the phrase "Hola Mohalla" means "mock fight." During this festival, processions are organized in the form of army columns accompanied by war-drums, standard-bearers, and move to a designated location or travel in a parade from one gurdwara to another. This tradition began in the time of Guru Gobind Singh Ji, who hosted the first such mock battle event in Anandpur in February 1701.

BACKGROUND

The foothills of the Shivalik range in Ropar district in the northeastern region of Punjab, particularly around the historic towns of Anandpur Sahib and Kiratpur Sahib, have been hosting Hola Mohalla since 1701. In recent times, the Indian government has recognized it as a national festival. The mock battles, which were directly overseen by Guru Gobind Singh, took place on the riverbed of Charan Ganga with the prominent Hindu temple of Mata Naina Devi in the Shivalik hills serving as the backdrop.

The annual festival, which takes place at Anandpur Sahib in Punjab and is now celebrated at other Gurdwaras around the world, was initiated by the tenth Sikh Guru as a congregation of Sikhs for military training and simulated battles. The festival is held on the day following the Holi festival at Anandpur Sahib and serves as a reminder of the values of courage and readiness for defense, which were particularly important to the Tenth Guru, as he was defending the Sikhs against attacks from the Mughal Empire and the hill kings at that time.

3 DAYS OF CELEBRATIONS

During the three-day Hola Mohalla festival, a range of activities are conducted including mock battles, exhibitions, and weapon displays. This is followed by competitions in music and poetry, as well as kirtan performances. Participants showcase their bravery by performing daring feats such as Gatka, which involves mock encounters with real weapons, as well as tent pegging, bareback horse-riding, standing erect on two speeding horses, and other challenging acts.

There are several Darbars where the Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji is present and religious lectures and kirtan take place. On the final day, a lengthy procession led by Panj Pyaras begins at Takhat Keshgarh Sahib, one of the five major Sikh religious seats. The procession passes through several significant Gurdwaras like Qila Anandgarh, Lohgarh Sahib, and Mata Jitoji before concluding at Takhat Keshgarh.

When visiting Anandpur Sahib during the festival, visitors can partake in the langars, which are voluntary community kitchens organized by local people as a form of sewa (community service). The villagers living nearby provide the raw materials such as wheat flour, rice, vegetables, milk, and sugar. Women volunteers cook the food while others help with cleaning utensils and performing other necessary tasks. Traditional cuisine is served to the pilgrims, who sit in rows on the ground (pangat) to eat.

THE FESTIVAL OF HOLA MAHALLA

A person who is enlightened does not have any particular identity. Their values are universal and aligned with the timeless state of existence. This timeless state of existence can be referred to by different names such as Akal Purukh, God, Raam, Rahim, Hari, Parmaatma, and so on, but all these names point to the same ultimate truth. Worshiping a name is meaningless unless it becomes a personal experience.

In recent times, developing nations like India have recognized the importance of tourism for generating employment and foreign exchange. However, recent research has highlighted the negative impacts, including erosion of cultural identity, materialism, rising crime, social conflicts, overcrowding, and environmental degradation. These negative effects have sometimes been counterproductive and led to strong opposition, particularly in the case of sex tourism prevalent in underdeveloped countries like Thailand and India. Profit-seeking adults often exploit innocent children from third-world nations. The only solution to mitigate these issues is to strictly adhere to the development of community and religious tourism and related sectors.

This paper aims to explore the potential and impact of community/religious tourism and its ability to thrive and develop. The focus is on the Sikh community's celebration of Hola Mahalla at Anandpur Sahib, Punjab, which coincides with the Indian festival of Holi. The study suggests that community-focused tourism, such as Hola Mahalla, can generate economic benefits while preserving the unique Sikh cultural heritage and promoting partnership with others. The paper provides a brief history of the Hola Mahalla festival, which has been recognized as a State festival by the Government of Punjab. It also examines the significance of community tourism and its impact on the economic and socio-cultural environment for both the host community and tourism development.

STREET IN ANANDPUR DURING FESTIVITIES

Hola Mahalla is an annual festival celebrated by the Sikh community on the first day of the lunar month of Chet, which typically falls in March, following the Hindu festival of Holi. "Hola" is the masculine form of the feminine noun "Holi". "Mahalia," on the other hand, is a Punjabi word derived from the Arabic root "hal" meaning "alighting" or "descending." It refers to an organized procession that takes the form of an army column accompanied by war drums and standard-bearers and proceeds to a specific location or moves in procession from one Gurdwara (Sikh place of worship) to another.

This tradition began during the time of Guru Gobind Singh Ji (1666-1708), who initiated the first march at Anandpur on Chet vadi 1, 1757 Bk (22nd February, 1701). Unlike the playful colored powders sprinkled during Holi, Hola Mahalla was an occasion for Sikhs to showcase their martial skills in simulated battles. This was likely done to prevent a more serious struggle against the imperial power following the Battle of Ninnohgarh in 1700. Hola Mahalla became an annual event held in an open ground near Holgarh, a fort located across the rivulet Charan Ganga, northwest of Anandpur Sahib Ji.

The popularity of Hola Mahalla festival can be gauged by the fact that among the five Sikh public holidays requested by the Khalsa Diwan of Lahore in 1889, the government approved only two - Hola Mahalla and the birth anniversary of Guru Nanak Dev Ji. Currently, Hola Mahalla is the biggest festival celebrated at Anandpur. It is worth mentioning here a brief overview of the town and the participants of this festival.

ANANDPUR

Anandpur is located in the Ropar District of Punjab, on one of the lower spurs of the Shivalik Hills, and is easily accessible by road and rail. The town is considered a significant pilgrimage center for Sikhs, and is known as Anandpur Sahib as a mark of reverence. It was at Anandpur Sahib that Guru Gobind Singh Ji gave birth to the Khalsa and baptized the original Panj Piare on Baisakhi in 1699. He also established the order of Saint-Soldiers, who were holy warriors dedicated to defending not only Sikhs, but also their fellow countrymen and their religious rights, in the tradition of Guru Tegh Bahadur, one of India's greatest Martyrs, who gave his life defending Hinduism on behalf of the Pandits of Kashmir.

At the behest of Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the Khalsa order would now be recognized by five distinct symbols, collectively known as the Five K's, which are kes (uncut hair), kangha (a comb), kachcha (a pair of shorts), kara (a steel bracelet), and kirpan (a sword). This was to ensure that they could be easily identified by anyone under attack. Additionally, Sikhs were instructed to adhere to the highest ethical standards and to remain prepared to fight against oppression and injustice.

HOLLA MAHALLA FESTIVAL

Anandpur Sahib, having been the residence of the last two human Gurus of the Sikhs for over 20 years, was witness to many significant events of Sikh history, including the Hola Mahalla festival which continues to be celebrated annually. Although the festival has lost much of its original military significance, a large number of Sikhs still gather at Anandpur Sahib on this day, and an impressive and colorful procession is held. The Nihangs, with their traditional attire, lead the procession and showcase their skills in various war-like sports, including the use of arms, horsemanship, and tent-pegging.

WARLIKE SPORTS OF NIHANG

The Nihang Singh Jis, originally known as Akalis, are lovingly referred to as the "Guru's Knights" or the "Guru's Beloved." They still retain the martial atmosphere and heroic style that was developed during the lifetime of Guru Gobind Singh Ji. The Nihang Singh Jis form a unique group among the Sikhs and are easily identifiable by their loose, dark blue clothing and their large, pointed turbans adorned with steel quoits, Khalsa insignia, and rosaries. They are always armed and are often seen mounted, carrying a heavy array of weapons such as swords, daggers, spears, rifles, shotguns, and pistols.

The term "Nihang" has possible origins from the Persian word for "alligator" or "sword", or from the Sanskrit word for "fearless" or "carefree". In the former sense, it may refer to the daring courage displayed by members of the order in battle. In the writings of Guru Gobind Singh Ji, specifically in Var Sri Bhagauti Ji 47, it is used to describe swordsmen warriors who lead the vanguard. Regardless of its exact origin, the word Nihang signifies the characteristic qualities of the clan, including fearlessness, readiness for action, and non-attachment to worldly possessions. During the eighteenth century, one of the confederate armies of the Dal Khalsa was led by Naina Singh Ji, the chief of the Nishanvalia misl, whose style of wearing a tightly tied tall turban with a dumala became popular among the Sikhs and those who adopted this style were called Akali Nihangs.

The Nihangs gained importance and a sense of self-discipline due to their privilege of convening at Akal Takht general assemblies of the Khalsa, despite their small numbers and political authority. During the time of Maharaja Ranjit Singh Ji (1780-1839), the Akali Nihangs maintained their independent existence and their leader, Phoola Singh Ji Nihang, as the custodian of the Akal Takht, was the voice of religious and moral conscience of the state. He even had the authority to censure and chastise the sovereign himself.

Presently, the Nihangs are divided into several groups, each with their own cantonment, but they are loosely organized into two forces or Dals - Buddha Dal and Taruna Dal. These names were initially given to the two sections into which the Khalsa army was divided in 1733. Buddha Dal has its cantonment at Talvandi Sabo in Bhatinda District, while the main cantonment of the Taruna Dal Nihangs is at Baba Bakala in Amritsar District.

The Hola Mahalla festival, which lasts for a week, concludes at Gurdwara Holgarh Sahib located one and a half kilometers northwest of the town, across the Charan Ganga rivulet. The Gurdwara stands on the site of Holgarh Fort. In the spring of 1701, Guru Gobind Singh Ji introduced the celebration of Hola on the day after the Hindu festival of Holi. Unlike the playful sprinkling of colors during Holi, Hola was turned into an occasion for demonstrating skills in simulated battle, which is now carried out by the Nihangs.

Every year in March, thousands of Nihangs gather at Anandpur Sahib to celebrate Hola Mahalla. They organize military skill tournaments and engage in mock battles. The most impressive event during this festival is a grand procession of Nihangs on horseback, elephants, and foot, showcasing their proficiency in using various traditional and modern weapons. What sets Hola Mahalla apart from other festivals is the Nihangs' commitment to preserving its original form and content, which has been strictly observed by the Akalis for over three centuries.

The Nihangs' display of martial arts during the Hola Mahalla festival offers visitors and tourists a glimpse into their skills and traditions. Given the festival's rich historical, socio-religious, and military importance, it has the potential to raise awareness about Sikh heritage and promote sustainable community tourism.
