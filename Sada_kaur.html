SARDARNI SADA KAUR JI

Sardarni Sada Kaur, who was the mother-in-law of Maharaja Ranjit Singh, is considered one of the most remarkable women in the history of Punjab. She passed away in December 1832, while being held as a prisoner in Amritsar. Prince Nau Nihal Singh, son of Maharaja Kharak Singh, performed her funeral rites, and Maharaja Ranjit Singh came to Amritsar to express his condolences. Sada Kaur played a crucial role in Punjab politics for about thirty years and was Ranjit Singh's main support system that helped him ascend to the pinnacle of power.

Sada Kaur was the daughter of Sardar Dasaundha Singh Dhariwal and was born in 1762. She married Gurbakhsh Singh, the tall and handsome son of Sardar Jai Singh, Chief of the Kanahiya Misl, and had a daughter named Mehtab Kaur in 1782. Unfortunately, Sada Kaur was widowed at the young age of 22 when her husband was killed defending against an attack by Jassa Singh Ramgarhia, sponsored by Mahan Singh Sukkarchakkia in February 1785.

During those days, alliances between Sikh Sardars were made and broken to gain or hold territories of erstwhile friends. The Kanahiyas, Sukkarchakkias, and Bhangis were the dominant forces in northern Punjab, but a dispute arose in 1784 over sharing the booty of a crore of rupees from Jammu, which was ransacked by Mahan Singh Sukkarchakkia. Jai Singh Kanahiya, who was the conqueror of the formidable fort of Kangra, demanded a share, but Mahan Singh refused. Consequently, Jassa Singh Ramgarhia was offered help by Mahan Singh to recover his territories from Jai Singh after a quarrel between them. Jassa Singh Ramgarhia had been driven out of his territories in October 1778, jointly by the Ahluwalias, Bhangis, and Kanahiyas, and was living in Malwa, south of Satluj.

In February 1785, Jassa Singh launched his attack and Jai Singh stayed at Batala, sending his 26-year-old son, Gurbakhsh Singh, to meet the enemy at Rampura near Batala. Unfortunately, Gurbakhsh was fatally wounded by a gunshot during the encounter.

After the tragic death of her husband, Sada Kaur's father-in-law agreed to return Jassa Singh's estates to him. However, this event caused Jai Singh to rapidly decline in his fortunes. Despite the difficult circumstances, Sada Kaur, who was born during a prosperous time for her family, could not accept the shattering of her dreams. In 1786, she met Raj Kaur, the wife of Mahan Singh, at Jwalamukhi. Sada Kaur decided to put aside her bitterness and proposed a marriage between her beautiful infant daughter, Mehtab Kaur, and Raj Kaur's 6-year-old son, Ranjit Singh. Ranjit Singh had previously lost his left eye due to smallpox. Raj Kaur accepted the proposal, which paved the way for reconciliation between the two families - the Kanahiyas and the Sukkarchakkias.

In 1790, Mahan Singh became ill and on his deathbed, he gave the hand of his only son, Ranjit Singh, to Jai Singh Kanahiya, who would become the boy's grandfather-in-law. When Ranjit Singh ascended the throne after his father's death, he was only 10 years old. His mother, Raj Kaur, and his would-be mother-in-law, Sada Kaur, both widows, took it upon themselves to run the affairs of his state, with the assistance of Mahan Singh's Diwan, Lakhpat Rai. In 1795, Mehtab Kaur was married to Ranjit Singh. The same year, Jai Singh, who was 80 years old, bequeathed Batala, Mukerian, and a few other places to Sada Kaur, his daughter-in-law. Additionally, he gave Hajipur and Sohian to his wife, Raj Kaur, who was the mother of Nidhan Singh, 7, and Bhag Singh, 2.

In her confidence, Sada Kaur maintained her own army to defend her interests, rule with dignity, and eventually rise in power. In July of 1799, with Sada Kaur's encouragement, young Ranjit Singh decided to capture Lahore. The combined forces of the Sukkarchakkias and the Kanahiyas entered the city on July 6th and evacuated the fort from Chet Singh Bhangi on July 7th.

Ranjit Singh's wife, Mehtab Kaur, was not as interested in the glitter of gold and galloping of horses as she was in the love of budding youth and the self-respect of a daughter of a brave and independent lady. She did not appreciate her husband's harsh words, who was not only a king but also overly ambitious in acquiring more and more. Moreover, he had taken another wife, Raj Kaur, sister of Bhagwan Singh and daughter of Khazan Singh of Nakai Misl. Mehtab Kaur was unhappy and spent much time in her mother's home at Batala. Maharaja used to visit her there from time to time.

In 1801, Maharaja's second wife, Rani Raj Kaur, gave birth to a son, Kharak Singh, which caused Sada Kaur to see one of her dreams slipping away. Kharak Singh would be the heir apparent. Sada Kaur tried to bring Mehtab Kaur closer to Ranjit Singh but was not successful.

In 1802, Ranjit Singh married Moran, a beautiful Muslim dancing girl from Multan. Despite being a secular prince, he considered himself the Maharaja of the Sikhs and his government as Sarkar-i-Khalsa. Moran retained her religion, but there was concern about the future of the Sikh kingdom if she were to bear a son.

In 1803, Ranjit Singh annexed all the territories of his second wife's brother, Bhagwan Singh Nakai, which threatened Sada Kaur's own territories. She continued to try and bring Ranjit Singh closer to her daughter, Mehtab Kaur.

Sada Kaur was overjoyed in 1804 when Mehtab Kaur gave birth to a son, named Ishar Singh, but unfortunately, the baby passed away before he was a year and a half old.

In 1807, twin sons were born to Mehtab Kaur and were named Sher Singh and Tara Singh. However, some people spread false rumours that she had given birth to a girl and that the male babies were actually acquired from "menial" workers - one a carpenter and the other a weaver. Maharaja Ranjit Singh believed these rumours and refused to acknowledge the twins as his own sons. Sada Kaur was extremely upset with this development.

In 1808, the Kumbh Mela was held at Hardwar after 12 years, and Maharani Mehtab Kaur went there for pilgrimage along with her mother. This was the first time that the Hardwar Kumbh was held under the supervision of the British, and it was managed by Charles Metcalfe, a young assistant in the Delhi Residency.

According to Bhagat Singh's History of the Sikh Misals, Sada Kaur met Samru Begum during the Kumbh Mela. As a gesture of sisterhood, they exchanged scarves (dupattas), which was similar to men exchanging turbans to pledge brotherhood. However, this act was seen by some as an attempt by Sada Kaur to gain power with the help of others.

During the same Kumbh Mela, a British spy named Mr. Mathew was also present. Mathew had been authorized by the Fort William Council in Calcutta on February 29, 1808, to operate as a tourist and gather military information in the territories of Sikh chieftains, particularly Maharaja Ranjit Singh. When Mehtab Kaur was returning from the Kumbh Mela, Mathew joined her caravan and made acquaintances with the staff of the Maharani to gather references and make his way through Punjab more smoothly.

On May 4, 1808, Captain Mathew wrote a letter from Batala to his contact officer, Adjutant-General Major General G. H. Fagan. In the letter, he mentioned that Sada Kaur desired an alliance with and protection from the British. She wished to replace Ranjit Singh as the ruler, and was willing to pay the British six annas from every rupee of the net revenue of the state. He added that he was confident that if it were in the interest of the English to possess the country during Sada Kaur's life or now, it could be done without trouble or bloodshed by acting in accordance with her measures. Sada Kaur might have shared her dreams with the English spy, and her grievances against Ranjit Singh, who had not yet acknowledged her grandsons, might have surfaced during the meeting.

In the same month, Mathew had a meeting with Ranjit Singh. The Maharaja was smart enough to understand that Mathew was not an ordinary traveller and must have received information regarding his identity. Ranjit Singh cleverly allowed Mathew to hear a comment he made to one of his aides, implying that the English would face the consequences if they tried to implement any evil plans. However, he also asked Mathew to convey a private communication (khareeta) from him to the Governor-General. When the khareeta arrived in Fort William, Calcutta on July 6, 1808, Captain Mathew was recalled.

Later that year, Charles Metcalfe was sent on a mission to negotiate an alliance between the British and the Sikhs, purportedly against a potential French invasion of India, either alone or with Turkey or Persia. However, the negotiations were stalled over the boundary dispute between the two powers, specifically, whether it should be the river Jamuna or the river Sutlaj.

In November 1808, the negotiations between Ranjit Singh and the British reached a point where war seemed imminent. At this critical moment, Sada Kaur offered Charles Metcalfe the use of her fortress, Atal-garh, on the condition that her possessions, which had been seized by the Maharaja, were returned to her. However, Metcalfe regretfully declined her offer, and was unable to negotiate with her further.

Two years later, in 1810, Maharani Mehtab Kaur passed away. Surprisingly, the Maharaja did not attend her cremation or any other ceremonies. It was only after being persuaded by Diwan Mohkam Chand that Ranjit Singh went to Sada Kaur's residence and participated in the ceremonies for his deceased wife.

According to Cunningham's account in "History of the Sikhs" (p. 158), "Sada Kaur realized that she could not gain any power through her grandchildren, and in 1810, the disappointed woman reached out to English authorities, accusing her son-in-law of usurping her rights and preparing for war with his new allies. While her message was received with some attention, she was unable to organize an insurrection and eventually came to terms with her situation."

In February 1812, Prince Kharak Singh married Chand Kaur, the daughter of Sardar Jaimal Singh Kanahiya. An intelligence report sent to the British on February 7 from Ranjit Singh's deorhi indicated that the Maharaja was missing his mother-in-law and her grandchildren.

In February 1812, Prince Kharak Singh got married to Chand Kaur, the daughter of Sardar Jaimal Singh Kanahiya. According to an intelligence report sent to the British on February 7, Maharaja Ranjit Singh expressed his regret over the absence of his mother-in-law, Sada Kaur, and her grandchildren from the wedding celebrations due to their differing opinions. The report states that the wedding was carried out splendidly, spending approximately two lakhs of rupees, meeting the Maharaja's expectations.

Afterward, Sada Kaur arranged the marriage of her grandson, Sher Singh, and adopted a more conciliatory approach towards the Maharaja. The wedding of Sher Singh took place in December of that year, but it did not receive the same level of grandeur as Prince Kharak Singh's wedding.

On August 25, 1814, an intelligence report was sent to the British stating that after the failure of the Kashmir campaign, Ranjit Singh began to reminisce and appreciate the advice and assistance of his mother-in-law, which had helped him to rise to power in his earlier years.

On September 17, 1814, Sada Kaur received a positive update that Ranjit Singh had informed her grandsons, Sher Singh and Tara Singh, that they would be receiving Jagirs soon. This news came after the Noble Sarkar had a private meeting with Diwan Muhkam Chand and Sada Kaur, where he expressed his disappointment over the failure of the Kashmir campaign and his gratitude for their support. In response, Sada Kaur and Muhkam Chand assured him that they were willing to make any sacrifices necessary to support him, including their lives and property.

In 1820, instead of granting fresh jagirs to Sada Kaur's grandsons, Ranjit Singh sent her a firman asking her to allocate half of her estate for their maintenance. Sada Kaur, who was in his camp at the time, had no choice but to sign the deed. However, soon after signing, she managed to escape from the camp hidden in a covered palanquin. One of her old Kanahiya family retainers, Vasakha Singh, secretly informed Ranjit Singh, who then sent Desa Singh with a contingent to retrieve her. She was brought back and confined first in Lahore, and later in Amritsar.

Not satisfied with just her confinement, Ranjit Singh ordered a division of the army to march and seize all of her wealth and territory. After a few weeks of resistance, one of her female attendants who was in charge of her principal stronghold, Attul-gurh, surrendered and the sequestering of her wealth and territory was completed.

This sad saga of Sardarni Sada Kaur ended with her death in 1832, while still under close confinement by Ranjit Singh, who had risen to great heights with her support and never looked back.
