BABA DEEP SINGH JI

Baba Deep Singh Shahid is one of the most revered martyrs in Sikh history. He founded the Shahid Misl (group) and was the first leader of the Damdami Taksal (Damdama school of learning), a 300-year-old religious school for Sikhs established by the last Sikh prophet, Guru Gobind Singh Ji. He was known for his exceptional strength and bravery.











EARLY LIFE

Shaheed Baba Deep Singh Ji was born on January 20, 1682, in the village of Pahuwind, in the district of Amritsar. His father's name was Bhai Bhagtu Ji. At the age of 12, Baba Deep Singh Ji accompanied his parents to Anandpur Sahib to meet Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the tenth Sikh guru. They stayed at Sri Anandpur Sahib for several days and served the Sangat. When his parents were ready to return to their village, Guru Gobind Singh Ji requested Baba Deep Singh Ji to stay with him. He humbly accepted Guru Ji's command and began serving him.

THE MISSIONS OF BABA DEEP SINGH JI

In the late 17th and early 18th centuries, Baba Deep Singh Ji was summoned by Guru Gobind Singh Ji to work with Bhai Mani Singh Ji on the final text of Sri Guru Granth Sahib at Damdama Sahib. Guru Ji recited the entire Granth Sahib to them while they wrote out the text. After its completion, Baba Deep Singh Ji spent several years hand writing four additional copies of the holy scriptures, each distributed to different Sikh holy places. He also inscribed another copy of the Sri Guru Granth Sahib in Arabic script which was sent to the Middle East.

In 1706, Guru Gobind Singh Ji left for the Deccan with Bahadur Shah, placing Baba Deep Singh Ji in charge of Damdama Sahib while sending Bhai Mani Singh Ji to head the Sangat at Harimander Sahib in Amritsar. Baba Deep Singh Ji spent many years at Damdama Sahib preaching Sikh values and teachings, doing service for the community, and distributing gutkas to the Sikh community.

Around 1709, Baba Deep Singh Ji joined Banda Singh Bahadur, the Jathedar appointed by Guru Gobind Singh before his passing, in the fight for the freedom of Punjab. They fought together in the battle at Sirhind, where Guru Ji's younger sons were killed. Despite being significantly outnumbered by the Muslim army, the Sikh army, with Baba Deep Singh Ji's help, was able to defeat the Muslim forces and kill Wazir Khan, ending his tyranny. Baba Deep Singh Ji's bravery and dedication to the Sikh cause have made him a highly respected and honored figure in Sikh history.

In 1732, Baba Deep Singh came to the aid of Sardar Ala Singh who was under siege in Barnala by Manjh and Bhatti Rajputs, in collaboration with the faujdar of Jalandhar and the nawab of Malerkotla. The following year, when the Mughal governor of Lahore offered the Sikhs a nawabship and a jagir, Baba Deep Singh and his jatha joined Nawab Kapur Singh at Amritsar to form a joint Sikh force, the Dal Khalsa. For administrative purposes, the Dal Khalsa was divided into the Buddha Dal and the Taruna Dal, with the latter being further split into five jathas. Baba Deep Singh was given command of one of these jathas, which in 1748 were redesignated as misls. His misl came to be known as the Shahid misl, and he achieved numerous victories for the Sikhs as its leader. The Shahid misl's sphere of influence was south of the River Sutlej, and Baba Deep Singh's headquarters remained at Talvandi Sabo. The tower in which he lived still stands next to the Takht Sri Damdama Sahib and is known as Burj Baba Deep Singh Shahid.

In 1755-56, at the invitation of Mughlani Begum, Ahmed Shah Abdali invaded India for the fourth time. On his return journey, Abdali's soldiers carried enormous stores of gold, silver, and other valuables looted from the towns of Mathura, Bindraban, Agra, and Delhi. Thousands of unmarried girls and married women, from both the Hindu and Muslim communities, were being forcibly taken against their wishes to serve as maidens and slaves of Abdali, and to be auctioned in an open market. They were herded together in bullock carts and bound to prevent their escape en route.

When the leaders of the Khalsa Panth (Misaldars) came to know of this caravan passing through the Punjab, they decided to intervene with force, to free these girls and women and save their honor and that of the country. The cries for help of these unfortunate women fell on deaf ears, and nobody dared to rescue them until the caravan arrived near Kurukshetra. Baba Deep Singh's Jatha (army) was assigned duty near the river Markanda. When he and his brave companions heard the wailings of the helpless children and women, they stormed out of the surrounding jungles (forests) and pounced upon Abdali's caravan like lightning bolts from an angry sky. While some of them attacked Abdali's soldiers, others captured and drove away many bullock carts laden with the children, women, and looted valuables, taking them to the safety of their nearby jungle hideouts.

With little thought for their own safety or lives, the Sikhs rescued about 300 women and young girls, as well as 100 boys, from the clutches of Abdali. The freed boys, girls, and married women, both Muslim and Hindu, were safely escorted to their homes by the Sikh soldiers, men whose moral character was of the highest order even in those difficult days.

The Rajput and Maratha Khatris had failed to mount any attempt to rescue the prisoners. But the saints-soldiers of Guru Gobind Singh were made of other stuff. Ahmed Shah himself wondered at the Sikhs' daring. How could their Gurus take sparrows and turn them into hawks and eagles? Did naming a man a lion turn him into one?

SUPREME SACRIFICE

During the invasion of India by Ahmad Shah Durrani, Punjab was taken over by his Afghan rule and his son, Taimur Shah, was appointed as the viceroy of Lahore, with Jahan Khan, an experienced general, as his deputy. In May 1757, Jahan Khan attacked Amritsar, destroyed the Sikh fort of Ram Rauni, and defiled the sacred pool. Despite being 75 years old, Baba Deep Singh Ji still possessed the strength of a young warrior. He gathered a large group of Sikhs and led them towards Sri Harimander Sahib. As they reached the village of Tarn Taran, which was about ten miles from Amritsar, their numbers had grown to about five thousand. At this point, Baba Ji drew a line on the ground with his sword and asked only those who were willing to fight and die to cross the line.

All of the assembled Sikhs crossed the line eagerly. Baba Deep Singh Ji then recited a shabad, which translates to: "Those who wish to play the game of love (to follow the Sikh path), come to me with your head in your palm. If you wish your feet to travel this path, don't delay in accepting to give your head."





ACHIEVEMENTS

Baba Deep Singh is considered one of the most important figures in Sikh history due to his many achievements. Here are some of his accomplishments in detail:

Military Leader: Baba Deep Singh was a skilled military leader and played a key role in many battles fought by the Sikhs against their enemies. He was the leader of the Shaheedi misl, one of the 12 Sikh Misls, which had its sphere of influence south of the River Sutlej. He achieved numerous victories for the Sikhs, and his headquarters remained at Talvandi Sabo.

Defender of Sikhism: Baba Deep Singh was a staunch defender of Sikhism and worked tirelessly to protect the Sikh community and their religious sites. In 1732, he went to the rescue of Sardar Ala Singh, who had been besieged in Barnala by Manjh and Bhatti Rajputs in collaboration with the faujdar of Jalandhar and the nawab of Malerkotla. In May 1757, when Jahan Khan invaded Amritsar and razed the Sikh fortress of Ram Rauni, Baba Deep Singh rallied a large group of Sikhs and advanced towards Sri Harimander Sahib to protect it.

Formation of Dal Khalsa: In 1733, Baba Deep Singh and his jatha joined Nawab Kapur Singh at Amritsar to form a joint Sikh force, the Dal Khalsa, which was soon divided for administrative convenience into the Buddha Dal and the Taruna Dal, the latter being further split into five jathas.

Establishment of Damdama Sahib: Baba Deep Singh played a key role in the establishment of Takht Sri Damdama Sahib at Talvandi Sabo. He is credited with overseeing the compilation of the Sikh holy scripture, the Guru Granth Sahib, at this site.

Rescue of Captured Women: During Ahmad Shah Durrani's invasion of India, his soldiers carried enormous stores of gold, silver, and other valuables looted from the towns of Mathura, Bindraban, Agra, and Delhi. Thousands of unmarried girls and married women, from both Hindu and Muslim communities, were being forcibly taken against their wishes, to serve as maidens and slaves of Abdali, and to be auctioned in open market. Baba Deep Singh and his brave companions rescued about 300 women and young girls as well as 100 boys from the clutches of Abdali.

Overall, Baba Deep Singh was a fearless warrior, a devoted Sikh, and a great leader who made significant contributions to the Sikh community. His legacy continues to inspire Sikhs around the world today.
