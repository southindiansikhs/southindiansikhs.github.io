JASSA SINGH RAMGARIAH JI

Jassa Singh Ramgarhia was a significant leader of the Sikh community during the era of the Sikh Confederacy. He is known for founding the Ramgarhia Misl, which was one of the prominent Sikh Misls.

EARLY LIFE

Jassa Singh Ramgarhia was born in 1723 to a Sikh family with the surname Sagoo. His birthplace is a matter of debate among different sources. He was of Tarkhan origin and originally known as Jassa Singh Thokar. After his father Bhagwan Singh passed away, Jassa Singh became the head of the family. He had four brothers, and he rose to lead the Sikh misl, which later became known as Ramgarhia.

Jassa Singh built a fort called Ram Rauni and a watchtower called Ramgarhia Bunga in Amritsar. He joined forces with Adina Beg, who appointed him as a commander. However, he later switched sides and rebuilt the damaged fort after Beg's siege. The fort was renamed Ramgarh, and Jassa Singh took on his new name.

Jassa Singh's policies were different from those of other misl leaders. The size of his territory made other Sikh Misls jealous, and they did not want him to become too powerful. At the peak of his power, Jassa Singh's territory included several regions in Bari Doab such as Batala, Kaldnaur, Dinanagar, Sri Hargobindpur, Shahpur Kandi, Gurdaspur, Qadian, Ghuman, Matteval, and in Jalandhar Doab, Urmur Tanda, Sarih, Miani, Garhdivala and Zahura. In addition, he received tribute from Kangra, Nurpur, Mandi and Chamba amounting to two lakh rupees.

MILITARY CAREER

In 1758, after the death of Adina Beg, Jassa Singh Ramgarhia collaborated with Jai Singh Kanhaiya to capture Sri Hargobindpur and Miani. He established his headquarters at Sri Hargobindpur.

In 1763, he sacked Kasur along with Jai Singh Kanhaiya, Hari Singh Dhillon, the Ramgarhias and Kanhaiyas shared all their booty equally. However, on this occasion, he tried to keep all the booty for himself. As a result, his relationship with Jai Singh Kanhaiya deteriorated.

In 1765, Ahmad Shah Durrani invaded India for the seventh time in the winter of 1764-1765. During this campaign, he was constantly harassed by Sikhs. Qazi Nur Muhammad, who was present in the Afghan army, describes the number of engagements between Sikhs and Afghans. A battle was fought on the western bank of the Satluj opposite Rupar. It was morning, and the Afghan army had hardly gone 3 km from the western bank of the Satluj when they were attacked by the Sikhs. The Afghans immediately stopped marching and got into a regular formation of battle. Ahmad Shah Durrani was in the center with 6,000 choice soldiers. Shah Vali Khan, Jahan Khan, Shah Pasand Khan, Anzala Khan, and others at the head of 12,000 troops were on the right. Nasir Khan with 12,000 Baluchis was on the left. The Dal Khalsa also organized themselves in a regular battle army. Jassa Singh Ahluwalia fearlessly stood like a mountain in the center. Close by him was Jassa Singh Thokah, looking like a lion in stature. The Qazi says that Ramgarhia has his own flag and war drum.

In 1767, Ahmad Shah Durrani invaded India for the eighth time. While Ahamed Shah was crossing the river Beas, his passage was obstructed by Jassa Singh Ahluwalia and Jassa Singh Ramgarhia. A fierce contest took place in which Ahluwalia was severely wounded. He retired to Cis-Satluj areas. Ramgarhia, who had succeeded Hari Singh Dhillon as a leader of Taruna Dal, took command of the Dal Khalsa.

In 1770, he led a plundering expedition into the hills. He defeated Raja Ghamand Chand, the most powerful hill Raja, in the battle of Talwara on the banks of the river Beas. Raja Ghamand Chand and other Rajas of the hills became tributary to him. He realized tribute of about two lakhs of rupees from Kangra hill states.

A conflict between Jai Singh Kanhaiya and Jassa Singh Ramgarhia developed, and the Bhangi Misl sardars also developed differences with Jai Singh Kanhaiya. A big battle was fought between Jai Singh, Charat Singh, and Jassa Singh Ahluwalia on one side and Bhangis, Ramgarhias, and their associates on the other side. The Bhangi side lost the battle.

In 1775, Jassa Singh Ahluwalia was going to Achal near Batala when he was attacked by Jassa Singh Ramgarhia's brothers Khushal Singh, Tara Singh, and Mali Singh. Jassa Singh Ahluwalia was taken prisoner. Jassa Singh Ramgarhia apologized for the misbehavior of his brothers and honorably returned Ahluwalia with gifts. However, the differences between the two increased. Jassa Singh Ahluwalia took an oath that he would drive the Ramgarhias out of the country.

In 1758, following the death of Adina Beg, Jassa Singh Ahluwalia allied with Jai Singh Kanhaiya to capture Sri Hargobindpur and Miani, establishing his headquarters at Sri Hargobindpur.

In 1763, he, along with Jai Singh Kanhaiya and Hari Singh Dhillon, sacked Kasur, sharing the booty equally. However, Jassa Singh Ahluwalia's attempt to keep all the booty for himself damaged his relationship with Jai Singh Kanhaiya.

In 1765, Ahmad Shah Durrani invaded India for the seventh time in the winter of 1764-1765, with the Sikhs constantly harassing his army. Qazi Nur Muhammad, present in the Afghan army, described the numerous engagements between the Sikhs and Afghans. A battle was fought on the western bank of the Satluj opposite Rupar, in which the Afghans were attacked by the Sikhs as they marched. The Afghans immediately formed a regular battle formation, with Ahmad Shah Durrani in the center with 6,000 elite soldiers. Shah Vali Khan, Jahan Khan, Shah Pasand Khan, Anzala Khan, and others, at the head of 12,000 troops, were on the right, and Nasir Khan, with 12,000 Baluchis, was on the left. The Dal Khalsa also organized themselves into a regular battle army. Jassa Singh Ahluwalia fearlessly stood in the center, with Jassa Singh Thokah close by him, looking like a lion in stature. The Qazi notes that Ramgarhia had his own flag and war drum.

In 1767, Ahmad Shah Durrani invaded India for the eighth time. As he was crossing the river Beas, his passage was obstructed by Jassa Singh Ahluwalia and Jassa Singh Ramgarhia, leading to a fierce contest in which Ahluwalia was severely wounded. He retired to Cis-Satluj areas, and Ramgarhia, who had succeeded Hari Singh Dhillon as the leader of Taruna Dal, took command of the Dal Khalsa.

In 1770, Jassa Singh Ahluwalia led a plundering expedition into the hills, defeating Raja Ghamand Chand, the most powerful hill Raja, in the Battle of Talwara on the banks of the river Beas. Raja Ghamand Chand and other Rajas of the hills became tributaries to him, and he collected tribute of about two lakhs of rupees from the Kangra hill states.

A conflict arose between Jai Singh Kanhaiya and Jassa Singh Ramgarhia, which caused tension between their respective misls. In addition, the Bhangi Misl sardars also had issues with Jai Singh Kanhaiya. As a result, a major battle occurred between Jai Singh Kanhaiya, Charat Singh, and Jassa Singh Ahluwalia on one side, and the Bhangi, Ramgarhia, and their allies on the other side. Ultimately, the Bhangi side lost the battle.

In 1775, Jassa Singh Ahluwalia was attacked by Jassa Singh Ramgarhia's brothers, Khushal Singh, Tara Singh, and Mali Singh, while on his way to Achal near Batala. Jassa Singh Ahluwalia was taken prisoner, but Jassa Singh Ramgarhia apologized for his brothers' actions and released him with gifts. However, this incident only furthered the tension between the two misls, and Jassa Singh Ahluwalia took an oath to drive the Ramgarhias out of the country.

In 1778, Jai Singh Kanhaiya and Haqiqat Singh Kanhaiya, with the support of Jassa Singh Ahluwalia, attacked Jassa Singh Ramgarhia and exiled him to the desert of Hansi and Hisar. He then established his headquarters in Tosham.

In March 1783, Jassa Singh Ramgarhia learned that the Dal Khalsa, led by Jassa Singh Ahluwalia, was marching towards Delhi. He joined them on March 10th, and on the following day, the Dal Khalsa entered the royal palaces in the Red Fort. In the Diwan-e-Am, Jassa Singh Ahluwalia was crowned Badshah Singh of Delhi by the Sikhs. However, Ramgarhia challenged Ahluwalia's claim to the throne and demanded that he step down immediately. Both sides drew their swords and were about to attack each other when Jassa Singh Ahluwalia renounced the honor. Ramgarhia then took the throne of Mughal emperor Aurangzeb, who had ordered the execution of the 9th guru, Teg Bahadur Ji, and brought it on elephants to the Golden Temple in Amritsar. The throne, known as Ramgarhia Bunga, is still present at the Golden Temple today.

In January 1784, Jassa Singh Ramgarhia and Karam Singh Nirmala crossed the Jamuna River and raided several areas, including Sarsawa, Naula Village, Merat, Saharanpur, Muzaffarnagar, and others. Zabita Khan dispatched Qutabi Ranghar and Nahar Singh Gujjar with a contingent of troops to stop the Sikhs from entering his territories. Zabita vakil visited the Sikh camp, where Jassa Singh Ramgarhia demanded Rs. 50,000 in exchange for sparing Zabita Khan's territory. Zabita Khan paid Rs. 10,000 and agreed to pay the same amount as an annual tribute to Jassa Singh. The Sikhs returned home after crossing the Jamuna at Barari Ghat, and Jassa Singh Ramgarhia deposited his booty in Tosham.

In the beginning of January 1785, Jassa Singh Ramgarhia, along with Baghel Singh and Gurdit Singh of Ladwa, crossed the Jamuna river and proceeded to plunder the villages and towns of Sadaat-e-Bara. Zabita Khan did not leave his fort of Ghausgarh. They crossed the Ganga river and entered Rohilakhand. On January 13, the villages of Barsi and Mahmudpur were destroyed. The following day, they sacked Chandausi, a great center of about 2000 bankers, rich merchants, and jewelers. They obtained booty worth a crore of rupees in two days and nights. Jassa Singh then turned his attention towards Punjab, where sharp differences had arisen between Jai Singh Kanhaiya and Maha Singh over the booty of Jammu. Maha Singh invited Jassa Singh Ramgarhia and Sansar Chand Katoch, both of whom were enemies of Jai Singh Kanhaiya. Jassa Singh hurried from Tosham at the head of his whole force and stopped at Jagraon, where Maha Singh's agents met him to form a plan of action. A Kanhaiya force tried to stop the Ramgarhias but failed, and a hard battle was fought near Achal. Jai Singh's heir apparent, Gurbaksh Singh Kanhaiya, was killed in the engagement. Jassa Singh Ramgarhia recovered all his lost territories and established his headquarters at Batala.





DEATH AND LEGACY

Jassa Singh Ramgarhia passed away in 1803 at the age of 80. An impressive equestrian statue of Sardar Jassa Singh Ramgharia has been erected in Amritsar, Punjab.
