BIBI BAGHEL KAUR JI

A Hindu newlywed bride was travelling back to her in-laws' village along with her husband and the wedding procession. On the way, a group of Mughal soldiers abducted her and looted her dowry. The unarmed groom and his companions were beaten and forced to flee. They approached the Muslim chief of the area for help, but he did not take any action and callously remarked, "What does it matter if our soldiers enjoy her for a few days? I shall return her to you once I find her." The husband was disheartened and decided to seek help from the nearby forest where the Sikhs were located.

During those times, Ahmad Shah Abdali repeatedly invaded India, and the Mughal Empire in Delhi, as well as the governor of Lahore, had become weak. Abdali looted Indian cities and forcibly took beautiful Hindu women with him. However, when he was returning to Afghanistan, the Sikhs attacked his army and recovered the loot he was taking with him. They also rescued the women whom Abdali had taken by force. The Sikhs fought a guerrilla war and retreated into the forest before they could be captured.

The groom, who was disappointed with the Muslim chief's inaction, met some Sikhs in the forest. They comforted him and initiated him into the Sikh faith. He was given the name Teja Singh. One night, a group of Sikhs, including Teja Singh, attacked the same group of plunderers and gave them a taste of their own medicine. Teja Singh's wife, who was in a wretched state, was also rescued from them. Though she wanted to end her life, she was convinced to keep living and was also initiated into the Sikh faith. She was now known as Baghel Kaur, and she wore a turban instead of a scarf on her head. She always carried a long sword with her. Many women like her lived in the wilderness near the Kahnuwan pond, in the company of the Sikhs.

In the wilderness, Baghel Kaur and her group met a few more Sikhs who were known to Teja Singh. They devised a plan to attack a patrolling party of Muslim soldiers and seize their horses and weapons for the new members. Upon reaching the village, they discovered that the soldiers were armed but asleep. Baghel Kaur and her companions took some guns and two horses from the soldiers and left the village before they awoke. They only killed those soldiers who resisted them. Baghel Kaur and her party returned safely and met their companions who were eagerly waiting for them. All of them left the Kahnuwan pool area (District Gurdaspur). They had to cross a dense forest and thorny bushes on the banks of the Bias River.

Mir Mannu, the governor of Lahore, was initially sympathetic towards the Sikhs due to the influence of his minister Kaura Mal. However, after Kaura Mal's death, Mir Mannu turned his attention towards eliminating the Sikhs. He was a tyrant who wanted to force the Sikhs to convert to Islam, and he used every possible punishment to subjugate them. The Sikhs had left their villages and taken refuge in thick forests. At that time, they used to say, "Mir Mannu is our sickle and we are his blades of grass. As he cuts us, we grow two hundred times stronger."

Abdali consulted with Mir Mannu and sent a challenge to the Sikhs to come out of the forest and fight face to face. Nawab Kapur Singh accepted the challenge, and the next day, four thousand Sikhs, along with a few hundred Sikh ladies, including Baghel Kaur, divided themselves into two parties and rode on their horses fully armed with swords and spears. They faced 10,000 Pathan forces, and although 500 Sikhs were martyred, the Pathans suffered a heavy loss.

On the second day, Baghel Kaur and a few other ladies fought so bravely and courageously that their deeds would be remembered forever. In the evening, the Pathan army had to retreat, but in the confusion that followed, Baghel Kaur and four other ladies became separated from the Sikh forces. They eventually reached a small village, cooked their food, and slept on the ground.

The dense and thorny bushes that surrounded them provided a protective fortress for the Sikhs, deterring the Mughal soldiers from entering. Within the forest, they had cleared some areas and set up tents. They relied on rations they could bring in, meat from hunted animals, and foraged edibles. After a long journey, they reached their comrades led by Nawab Kapur Singh, who encouraged them to fight against aggression for the sake of justice.

They took turns watching over their horses and weapons, rising before dawn for morning prayer before setting off. One day, they encountered a patrolling party of fifty enemy soldiers, with five approaching Baghel Kaur's party, unaware that they were facing a fierce adversary. The soldiers planned to capture and marry them, but Baghel Kaur swiftly cut the sword of the first soldier with her own sword, while her companion injured him with hers. Another soldier then attacked Baghel Kaur with his spear, but her friend deflected his attack with her sword and injured him. The wounded soldiers retreated to seek help from their party.

Meanwhile, Baghel Kaur and her companions fled to the thick forest to meet their comrades. The Pathan soldiers pursued them, but a Sikh watchman alerted the others in the forest about the approaching enemy. The Sikhs quickly emerged and defeated the Pathans within a few minutes, though three Sikhs also lost their lives in the battle. The Sikhs urged Baghel Kaur and her companions to remain in the village, but the courageous ladies refused, opting to stay and fight alongside their fellow Sikhs.

Mir Mannu, a notorious bigot, was responsible for the massacre of numerous Sikhs. He even offered a reward of twenty-five rupees per Sikh head. In total, he killed over thirty thousand Sikhs and ordered the capture of any Sikh women found in his territory to be forced to convert to Islam. Baghel Kaur was determined to save a few remaining ladies who were unable to leave the village due to their young children. Disguising herself one night, Baghel Kaur returned to her village and rescued the three Sikh women who were hiding in the house of a Muslim friend. She convinced them to accompany her early the next morning and flee to the thick forest on the other side of the River Beas.

After taking a short two-hour nap, Baghel Kaur and her three companions, along with two children, left the village at 4am. However, four soldiers who were sleeping outside the village noticed them and followed them to the riverbank. Baghel Kaur instructed two of the ladies to cross the river with their children, while she and the third lady faced the soldiers. She quickly thrust her spear into the chests of the first two soldiers who approached her before they could attack. However, when her companion tried to attack the third soldier, she was injured in the arm by his spear.

Baghel Kaur gave her horse to her injured friend and asked her to cross the river immediately. She then took the horse of the injured soldier and fought the remaining two soldiers with bravery and fearlessness. Both Baghel Kaur and the soldiers were injured and bleeding. Despite this, she gathered the courage to cross the river on her horse in the blink of an eye. Once all four ladies and two children had crossed, they quickly rode away and soon were out of sight of the chasing soldiers. After traveling a long distance, they reached their destination and met a group of Sikhs.

The plight of Sikh ladies who were detained in Mir Mannu's camps was miserable. They were tortured and kept thirsty and hungry as they refused to convert to Islam. Each of them was given a small millstone to grind a fixed quantity of wheat. It was also ordered that their children be taken away from them. One soldier threw a child into the air, and another killed the child with his spear before the child could hit the ground. The dead bodies of these children were then cut into pieces and used to garland the ladies. Pieces of the children's flesh were even thrust into the mouths of their mothers.

Despite all the atrocities and torture inflicted upon them, not a single one of the Sikh ladies cried or gave in to the pressure of converting to Islam. One particular incident even left Mir Mannu, the cruel ruler, stunned. He had visited the camp where these ladies were being held and witnessed a horrific scene where a soldier threw a child up in the air and another soldier killed him with his spear before he could touch the ground. The dead child was then cut into pieces and garlanded around the necks of the mothers, while pieces of his flesh were forced into their mouths. This brutal act failed to break the resolve of these brave women, who refused to embrace Islam.

After witnessing this incident, Mir Mannu was visibly shaken and returned to his palace in silence. Later, he went for hunting with only four soldiers. During the hunt, Mir Mannu's horse became scared and ran wildly, causing Mir Mannu to fall and get his feet entangled in the stirrups. His cries further frightened the horse, and it continued running, dragging Mir Mannu along. As a result of this accident, Mir Mannu suffered severe injuries and died in the forest.

After Mir Mannu's sudden death, the Sikhs gained courage and began to settle back into their villages. In an act of bravery, Baghel Kaur led a group of Sikhs to attack the Lahore camp at midnight, killing 25 unprepared Muslim soldiers and freeing captive ladies, whom they escorted to a safer location. However, Mir Mannu's queen invited Ahmad Shah to help capture the Sikhs. During this time, Baghel Kaur was living in her village with her four-year-old son and husband. She asked her husband to take the child and flee to the forest, while she set out to rescue the ladies who were being forcibly taken to the camp by Muslim soldiers. She managed to injure two soldiers with her spear, but was caught by their companions and became a captive along with the other ladies in the camp.

The soldiers in the camp provided every lady with a piece of bread, but some injured and hungry ladies lay half-dead on the ground, while their children cried for food. Baghel Kaur selflessly gave her own piece of bread to the crying children and went hungry herself. The soldiers whipped, insulted, and taunted the ladies, hoping to force them to embrace Islam and escape their torment. Baghel Kaur spoke out against the mistreatment, but was ordered to grind wheat for the entire night without rest.

At midnight, the commander of the camp summoned Baghel Kaur, but she refused to comply. A drunken soldier grabbed her by the wrist and pulled her along. Baghel Kaur bravely struck the soldier and took his sword from his belt, injuring him in the process. The other ladies came to her aid and the soldier fled. In the morning, all the ladies were gathered together, and the commander offered to release anyone who agreed to marry a soldier of their choice and convert to Islam. Baghel Kaur stood up and declared that none of them would renounce their religion, and they would rather die than live as cowards. Her bold words left the commander speechless.

She was then taken to a pillar to be tied up and whipped to death. On the way, she pushed the soldier escorting her and seized his sword. This caused the other soldiers in the camp to surround her, resulting in many of the captive ladies being killed. Baghel Kaur fought bravely, but ultimately she was killed by the armed soldiers surrounding her. The next night, about 8000 Sikhs attacked the camp, killed the commander, and liberated the captive ladies.
