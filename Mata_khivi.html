MATA KHIVI JI

Mata Khivi was born in 1506 to Karan Devi and Bhai Devi Chand Khatri. Her father, a shopkeeper and moneylender, was a popular man in the neighborhood and she inherited his finest attributes of generosity and congenial spirit. At the age of 13, she was married to Bhai Lehna, and they remained married for 20 years before he became the second Guru of the Sikhs.

Historical records indicate that Mata Khivi had four children. Her eldest, Dasu, was born in 1524, followed by Bibi Amro in 1532, Bibi Anokhi in 1535, and son Datu in 1537. As the wife of one of the wealthiest men in town, Mata Khivi lived a life of luxury and enjoyed great respect. However, her life took a turn when she came under the influence of Mai Bhirai, who introduced her to the teachings of Guru Nanak Dev Ji.

Around the same time, Bhai Lehna, Mata Khivi's husband, also became interested in the Guru's teachings through Bhai Jodha, one of Guru Nanak's earliest disciples. In 1532, shortly after the birth of their first daughter Amro, Bhai Lehna set out on his annual pilgrimage and stopped at Kartarpur to meet the Guru. After listening to Nanak speak, Bhai Lehna begged to stay and become his disciple. He had finally found the truth he had been seeking and dedicated himself to serving his master with the utmost devotion, doing even the most menial tasks like sweeping the visitor's quarters, washing their clothes, and helping in the fields.

As Bhai Lehna's knowledge and understanding of the new teachings grew, so did the Guru's affection and approval of his disciple. However, this created a problem for the Guru's sons, who increasingly grew jealous of Bhai Lehna and made no effort to conceal their dislike. Dealing with this kind of stress and strain would have been very difficult for Bhai Lehna's wife, Mata Khivi. Unfortunately, there are no records of her thoughts or feelings or how she handled the situation. It is likely that had she behaved foolishly during this time, someone would have recorded it.

A YOUNG FAMILY

At the time, Bhai Lehna was 28 years old and had a wife and two young children. The Guru he had chosen spoke of the equality of women and advocated a normal family life as the best way to attain salvation. After serving the Guru for some time, he was sent back to Khadoor to see his family. He was instructed to take his time and to spend it spreading the word of the new faith to all he met.

Bhai Lehna carried out his task well, and Guru Nanak Dev Ji was pleased with the reports he heard of him. The reports were so good that Guru Nanak Dev Ji visited his village twice to see him and reinforce his work with his own preaching. Mata Khivi also learned from her husband and embraced the new faith wholeheartedly. However, the women in the village taunted her, saying that her husband was becoming an important holy man and would soon forsake her. She knew she had nothing to worry about and gave birth to two more children during that period of time.

After Guru Nanak Dev Ji passed away, Guru Angad Dev Ji, who was previously known as Bhai Lehna, felt the need to prepare himself for the work ahead. To do so, he moved into Mai Bharai's house to pray and meditate, and he requested to be left alone, except for the supply of milk that he received from her.

When Bhai Lehna became the second Guru of the Sikhs, as Guru Angad Dev Ji, Mata Khivi's life became very busy. People would come to her house to see their Guru, and she had to adjust to this new purposeful coming and going. Prior to this, Mata Khivi was accustomed to a busy social life, but this was different. In addition, Sikh teaching emphasized the importance of earning a living through one's own labor.

MANAGING THE LANGAR

Mata Khivi was deeply committed to the Sikh teachings and took the responsibility of managing every aspect of the langar very seriously. She ensured that only the best ingredients were used and everyone was treated with utmost respect, without any distinction of caste, creed or colour. Khadoor, being situated at the riverbank, served many travellers who would stop by the langar before or after crossing the river, which played a crucial role in the spread of Sikhism. The langar became a unique and integral part of Sikhism, and the credit for keeping it alive goes to Mata Khivi. While Guru Angad Dev Ji spread the teachings of Sikhism in congregations (Sangat), Mata Khivi spread it through the community kitchen where sweet dishes were also served to everyone. The expenses of the kitchen were met through the offerings made by the Sikhs.

Mata Khivi's dedication to Sikhism and hospitality has been admired and emulated by many over the centuries, and has become an integral part of Sikh culture. Despite taking on the double responsibility of managing her household and the community kitchen, she did so with a cheerful demeanour. Guru Angad Dev Ji, the second Guru of the Sikhs, did not rely on offerings from his followers and instead earned a living by making strings out of coarse grass. Mata Khivi played a crucial role in establishing and strengthening the Sikh community through her tireless efforts in managing the langar and ensuring that only the best ingredients were used, and everyone was treated with respect and courtesy, regardless of their caste, creed, or colour. Her many virtues, including her efficiency, good nature, and beauty, have been praised in Sikh history. Mata Khivi is the only one of the Guru's wives to be named in the Guru Granth Sahib, where she is described as a "good person," "an affectionate mother," and "one who provides shelter and protection to others."

Mata Khivi's contributions went beyond her work in the community kitchen. She also created a loving environment for everyone she interacted with. She and Guru Angad Dev Ji had a deep fondness for their children, and they showered their love and affection not just on their own offspring, but on all the children in the community. Their dedication and devotion set a beautiful example for all who witnessed it.

POPULARISING GURMUKHI

Guru Angad Dev Ji took great pleasure in spending time with the children and taught them a simplified version of the Punjabi script that was easier for illiterate people to learn. This new script, which he created, came to be known as the Gurmukhi script and is credited with popularizing the alphabet used to write the Guru Granth Sahib Ji.

Every day, a special time was set aside for teaching the children and enjoying their cleverness. Afterwards, the Guru and Mata Khivi would watch the children play and even observe wrestling matches together. From these games, the Guru would draw lessons for his congregation. With the help of Bhai Bala and other disciples, Guru Angad Dev Ji wrote the first biography of Guru Nanak Dev Ji, which became the first published prose in the Punjabi language.

AFTER GURU ANGAD DEV JI

Mata Khivi lived for thirty years after her husband's passing. Despite her loss, she remained dedicated to serving the community and continued to stay connected with the Guru's house. During these years, she had the honor of meeting the five Gurus who succeeded her husband.

Mata Khivi passed away in Khadoor in 1582, and her cremation was attended by the fifth Guru, Guru Arjan Sahib himself. She was a devoted, noble, and wise lady known for her sweet tongue and temper. Her legacy of establishing the tradition of the community kitchen continues to be cherished by the Sikhs, and every Sikh should be proud of her and acknowledge her significant contribution to their community.

When Guru Angad Sahib Ji chose Amar Das as the third Guru, Mata Khivi accepted the decision willingly and did not raise any questions about the rights of her sons. When Guru Angad Dev Ji asked her, "I know you approve of my decision in selecting Amar Das as the next Guru, but what about your sons?" She replied, "They are proud of being Guru's sons, be kind to them, and show them the right path." This shows her obedience to her husband's decision and her impartiality.

However, her elder son, Datu, did not accept this decision. She admonished him and reminded him that the decision was based on merit, without any favoritism or partiality. She reminded him that even his father had become Guru based on merit, and this was the tradition in Sikhism. She never supported Datu's arrogance and respected her husband's decision.

Mata Khivi created a loving atmosphere for all those she came into contact with, and helped her husband establish the infant Sikh community.

Encouraged by some of his friends, Datu attempted to declare himself as the rightful heir to the Guru's throne. He and his followers began to sing hymns on their own. Mata Khivi was deeply upset by this turn of events. When Datu began to suffer from headaches, she convinced him that his responsibility was too great for him to bear alone. She advised him that the only way to cure his ailment was to return to the rightful Guru and beg for his forgiveness. Mata Khivi accompanied her son back to Guru Amar Das, who came out to meet them halfway upon hearing of their approach. All was forgiven, and Datu's headaches disappeared. Thanks to Mata Khivi's intervention, Sikhism was spared another schism. Mata Khivi continued to manage Guru Amar Das's kitchen, and she remained proud of her children until the day she passed away and returned to God. Her daughter Amro had married Bhai Jasoo of Basarke village, who was the son of Bhai Manak Chand and the nephew of Guru Amar Das Ji.

Bibi Amro became a prominent preacher of Sikhism and played a significant role in the life of Guru Amar Das Ji. She introduced him to the teachings she had learned from her father, Guru Angad Dev Ji, and transformed his life. When Guru Amar Das Ji divided the teaching of Sikhism into specific districts, he appointed Bibi Amro as head of a diocese, or Manji. This appointment was equivalent to being a bishop in the Christian Church. As head of the Manji, Bibi Amro was responsible for the quality of the preaching, as well as collecting revenues and making decisions for the welfare of her Sangat. Her Manji included Basarke, her husband's village, and an old man-made pond close to modern-day Basarke is named Bibi Amro Da Talab (Tank of Bibi Amro) in her memory.

FIVE GURUS

Mata Khivi had the honor of meeting five Gurus and lived until the age of 75 before passing away in 1582 and being reunited with God. Her contributions to the Sikh cause can be divided into three periods. The first period was the twenty years of marriage before Guru Angad Dev Ji succeeded Guru Nanak, which was a test of her dedication and devotion as any decisions made by Guru Angad Dev Ji affected her and vice versa. However, she never complained and always supported him in achieving his objectives.

The second period of her life was marked by her extraordinary devotion and dedication to the Sikh cause as the wife of the Guru. In the third and final period of her life, after her husband's passing, she continued to nourish the Sikh community and work tirelessly for its betterment with all her heart.

Mata Khivi lived a long and fruitful life, working hard and earning the love of all those around her. Her good humour and amiable personality played a significant role in shaping the spirit of hospitality that is now considered an essential part of Sikh culture. She was likely one of the first women of her time to work outside of her immediate family and household responsibilities, all while raising young children. Mata Khivi handled both roles with remarkable skill and grace, and it is time for Sikhs to acknowledge her important contributions. In fact, Mata Khivi is highly praised in one of the hymns composed by Balwand and included in the Guru Granth Sahib at Ang 967.
