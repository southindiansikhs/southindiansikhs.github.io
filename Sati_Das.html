BHAI SATI DAS JI

Bhai Sati Das, along with his elder brother Bhai Mati Das, is considered to be one of the greatest martyrs in Sikh history. Both brothers were devoted followers of the ninth Sikh Guru, Guru Tegh Bahadur Singh Ji. Bhai Sati Das, Bhai Mati Das, and Bhai Dyal Das were executed at the Kotwali (police station) near the Sunehri Masjid in the Chandni Chowk area of Old Delhi under the orders of Emperor Aurangzeb.

EARLY LIFE

Bhai Sati Das was a Mohyal Brahmin from the Chhibber clan, hailing from the village of Karyala in the Jhelum District of Punjab, which is situated approximately ten kilometers from Chakwal on the road leading to the Katas Raj Temple Complex. Karyala is a stronghold of the Chhibbers and is located on the top of the Surla hills in an area known as Dhani, which means rich. The Salt Mines and coal mines of Dandot are just a few kilometers away from this region.

The Katas lake, located in the vicinity, is a beautiful attraction. It is believed to be connected to the Mahabharata, as legend has it that this was the very pool where Yudhishtira, the Pandava, was tested by his father, Lord Yama/Dharma in the form of a Yaksha. In the past, a popular Hindu fair was held at this location, which continued until 1947.

DEVOTION TO THE GURUS

After Guru Har Krishan Singh Ji's passing in Delhi, two brothers, Mati Das and Sati Das, were part of a deputation of five men that included Dyal Das and Gurditta. This group was responsible for declaring the nomination of Teg Bahadur as the ninth Guru at the village of Bakala, where the new Guru was residing at that time. Guru Teg Bahadur Singh Ji was pleased to offer the two important portfolios of finance and home departments to Mati Das and Sati Das respectively. Both brothers were proficient in Persian language and were familiar with the workings of the Guru's durbar. Dyal Das was entrusted with the department of household affairs.

Bhai Mati Das served as Dewan in the Guru's court, while Bhai Sati Das was a skilled writer and translator. He was an accomplished scholar of the Persian language and used to transcribe the words of the Guru in Persian. Later, he would translate these utterings and write them in Gurmukhi script.

GURU TEGH BAHADUR SINGH JI

Sati Das was the son of Hira Nand, who was a disciple of Guru Har Gobind Ji. Sati Das had fought in many battles under Guru Har Gobind Ji and had survived him. Before his death, Hira Nand entrusted his two sons, Mati Das and Sati Das, to the care of Guru Har Rai, who promised to take care of them. Both brothers remained attached to the Guru's family at Kiratpur. When Guru Har Krishan was summoned to Delhi by Aurangzeb, both Mati Das and Sati Das were part of his entourage, along with Dyal Das and Gurditta, who was a descendant of Bhai Budha (not to be confused with Baba Gurditta, the son of Guru Hargobind and father of Guru Har Rai).

The two brothers, Mati Das and Sati Das, accompanied Guru Tegh Bahadur Singh Ji on his journey to Assam. Guru Tegh Bahadur Singh Ji purchased a hillock near the village of Makhowal, five miles north of Kiratpur, and established a new town called Anandpur (the abode of bliss). Mati Das and Sati Das were present at the foundation ceremony of Anandpur. The Guru's council of administration consisted of Mati Das, Sati Das, Dyal Das, and Gurditta at that time. When the Guru was arrested and taken to Delhi, these four individuals followed him.

MARTYRDOM

In Delhi, the Guru and his four companions were summoned to the council chamber of the Red Fort. The Guru was asked numerous questions about religion, including Hinduism, Sikhism, and Islam. The officials suggested that the Guru should convert to Islam. However, the Guru firmly refused to abjure his faith. The officials then questioned why he was called Teg Bahadur (gladiator or Knight of the Sword); before this, his name had been Tyag Mal. In response, Bhai Mati Das immediately replied that the Guru had won the title by inflicting a heavy blow on the imperial forces at the young age of fourteen. The officials reprimanded him for his breach of etiquette and outspokenness. The Guru explained that as a spiritual leader, it was his duty to raise his voice against cruelty and injustice, including the plight of the Kashmiri Pandits. As a result, the Guru and his companions were imprisoned and tortured until they agreed to embrace Islam.

After a few days, Guru Teg Bahadur Singh Ji and three of his companions were brought before the Qazi of the city. Gurditta managed to escape and remained in hiding within the city, evading all efforts by the authorities to locate him. The Qazi asked Mati Das to convert to Islam, but he refused to do so and was promptly sentenced to death.

The executioners were called, and the Guru and his three companions were brought to the execution site. Bhai Sati Das approached the Guru with folded hands, seeking his blessings. He expressed his happiness at being the first to achieve martyrdom and prepared himself for the ultimate sacrifice.

The Guru blessed Mati Das and advised him to accept the will of the Lord with happiness. He commended Mati Das for his unwavering devotion to him and his mission. With tears in his eyes, the Guru bid him farewell, acknowledging that his sacrifice would be remembered forever in history. Mati Das then touched the Guru's feet, embraced him as a friend and brother, and left for his designated place.

DAYAL DAS AND MATI DAS

Dyal Das vehemently condemned the Emperor and his courtiers for their barbaric act. As punishment, he was tied up and thrown into a large cauldron of boiling oil, where he was roasted alive until he became a block of charcoal. Meanwhile, Mati Das also spoke out against the cruel treatment, and as a result, he was brutally hacked to pieces, limb by limb.

Throughout this savagery, the Guru maintained a divine calm, bearing witness to the brutality being inflicted upon his beloved followers.

GURU TEG BAHADUR SINGH JI'S MARTYRDOM

Mati Das, Dyal Das, and Sati Das were subjected to torture and executed on three consecutive days. The execution of Bhai Sati Das left the Kazis of Emperor Aurangzeb disheartened and dismayed. They had hoped that at least one of the Guru's Sikhs would be tempted by the allure of a luxurious life and agree to convert to Islam. However, with the steadfastness of the Sikh martyrs, they realized that they could not persuade the 9th Guru of the Sikhs to abandon his faith and adopt Islam. The disciples of the Sikh Gurus continue to draw inspiration from these three martyrs and strive to emulate their example, that no sacrifice is too great for one's religion and faith.

Guru Teg Bahadur Ji was executed by a man named Jalal-ud-din Jallad, who was from the town of Samana in present-day Haryana. The execution took place on November 11, 1675, under a banyan tree located opposite the Sunheri Masjid near the Kotwali in Chandni Chowk, where the Guru had been imprisoned. The site of his execution is still preserved, along with a well nearby where he took a bath.

Bhai Jaita, a disciple of the Guru, carried his head to Anandpur where the nine-year-old Guru Gobind Singh Ji cremated it at a gurdwara that is now known as Gurdwara Sis Ganj Sahib. The Guru's body was stolen before it could be quartered, under the cover of darkness by Lakhi Shah Vanjara, another disciple. He carried it in a cart of hay and cremated it by burning his hut. Today, the Gurdwara Rakab Ganj Sahib stands at the site of the cremation. Later on, the Gurdwara Sis Ganj Sahib was built at the site of the Guru's martyrdom in Chandni Chowk.

LEGACY

To acknowledge the unwavering devotion and ultimate sacrifice of Mati Das, Guru Teg Bahadur Singh Ji honored him with the title of Bhai. Eventually, all Chhibbers from the village of Karyala adopted this title in his memory.

ACHIEVEMENTS

As a historical figure and martyr in Sikhism, Bhai Sati Das is recognized for his unwavering commitment to his faith and his ultimate sacrifice for it. Here are some points that could be considered as his achievements:

 Bhai Sati Das was a devout follower of the ninth Sikh Guru, Guru Tegh Bahadur, and accompanied him on his journey to Assam.

 He helped establish the town of Anandpur with the Guru and played an important role in the Guru's council of administration.

 When the Guru was arrested and taken to Delhi, Bhai Sati Das and three other companions followed him.

 In Delhi, when the Guru was asked to embrace Islam, Bhai Sati Das refused to abjure his faith and remained steadfast in his beliefs.

 Along with his companions Bhai Mati Das and Bhai Dyal Das, he endured torture and ultimately gave his life for his faith rather than betray it.

Bhai Sati Das's sacrifice has become an inspiration to Sikhs and others around the world, serving as a testament to the power of unwavering faith and devotion.
