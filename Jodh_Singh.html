SARDAR JODH SINGH JI

Jassa Singh Ramgarhia had two sons named Jodh Singh and Bir Singh. After Jassa Singh's death, Jodh Singh succeeded him as the leader. He formed an alliance with Raja Sansar Chand of Kangra and with his support, Jodh Singh was able to capture the territories of Batala, Bhunga, Hoshiarpur, and the surrounding areas.

In 1805, when Maharaja Ranjit Singh demanded the Zamzama gun from Mai Sukhan, the widow of Gulab Singh Bhangi, she flatly refused to hand it over and prepared to fight against the Maharaja. Jodh Singh secretly sent three hundred soldiers to reinforce Sukhan and advised her to either give the gun to Ranjit Singh or destroy it, but she rejected both suggestions. The Maharaja, accompanied by his allies Sada Kaur and Fateh Singh Ahluwalia, besieged Amritsar, and the opposing forces were on the brink of a severe clash. However, Jodh Singh and Akali Phula Singh intervened and persuaded Sukhan to surrender, thereby avoiding bloodshed. Mai Sukhan and Gurdit Singh accepted Jodh Singh's hospitality and stayed with him for some time.

Earlier, Jodh Singh was friendly towards Sansar Chand Katoch, but their relationship soured due to Jodh Singh's inability to assist Katoch against the Gurkhas.

Maharaja Ranjit Singh believed that befriending the Ramgarhias was essential to occupy the whole of Punjab. Thus, he wrote a letter to Jodh Singh, soliciting his friendship and cooperation. After settling matters, the Maharaja sent Bishan Singh Munshi, Mehar Singh Lamba, and Fateh Singh Kalianwala to escort Jodh Singh to Lahore. Jodh Singh agreed to join Maharaja Ranjit Singh on two conditions: first, that Batala, Kalanaur, Bajwara, and Sangowal, previously belonging to them but now in the hands of their opponents, should be restored to them; and second, that Gurdit Singh Bhangi, who was lying at his door, should be provided with a Cager for his subsistence. The Maharaja accepted both conditions. Jodh Singh, accompanied by his close associates, went to Amritsar, met Ranjit Singh at Harmandir Sahib, and was honored by the latter.

SADAR JASSA SINGH WITH HIS TWO SONS JODH SINGH & BIR SINGH (THIS PORTRAIT WAS PROBABLY COMMISSIONED BY MAHARAJA JASSA SINGH HIMSELF DURING 1780'S)

The demanded territories were returned to Jodh Singh, and Mai Sukhan and her son, Gurdit Singh, were given Panjore and five or six villages in jagir. Jodh Singh was renowned for his generosity and magnanimity of heart. Any defeated chief or impoverished person could seek his hospitality. He always sympathized with those who faced misfortune. In his Misal, he introduced strict discipline, and anyone found guilty of theft or any other crime was strictly dealt with. He never sold justice but administered it with utmost honesty. Providing clean administration to his people was a priority for him, and there was nothing closer to his heart than the welfare of his subjects.

Jodh Singh fought alongside Maharaja Ranjit Singh in the Battle of Kasur and received an elephant as a gift from the Maharaja after the occupation of Kasur. From then on, Jodh Singh always supported Ranjit Singh in his expeditions against Multan and other adversaries. In 1811, Ranjit Singh gave Jodh Singh eleven villages from the pargana of Sikhowala, which belonged to the sons of Fateh Singh Kanaihya, and had an annual revenue of twelve thousand rupees. Ranjit Singh held Jodh Singh Ramgarhia in the highest regard among all Sikh Sardars and addressed him as "Baba Ji." When Jodh Singh came to visit the Maharaja, Ranjit Singh would personally receive him and seat him by his side. Jodh Singh mostly lived in Lahore or Amritsar and always mobilized his forces according to the Maharaja's instructions. Due to his unwavering loyalty to the Maharaja, Jodh Singh retained his possessions until his death on August 23, 1815.

Jodh Singh remained hostile to the Ahluwalias and Rani Nawab Bhamboo Khan. He gave five villages in Jagir to Sardars Muhar Singh, Hazura Singh, and Thakar Singh, who were dismissed by Sardar Fateh Singh Ahluwalia. In response, Sardar Fateh Singh Ahluwalia and Sardarni Baghel Singh gathered a force of twenty thousand men and marched against Jodh Singh. However, aided by Nidhan Singh, Jodh Singh emerged victorious in the great battle at Banola.

A story erroneously attributed to Sardar Jodh Singh Wazirabadia by Sir Lepel Griffin is actually about Sardar Jodh Singh Ramgarhia, as found in family records compiled by the late Sardar Mangal Singh Ramgarhia C.S.I. According to the story, the Maharaja invited Jodh Singh to Lahore for a friendly visit, but Jodh Singh, suspecting the Maharaja's intentions, brought a large force with him. When asked to send his followers back, he refused out of pride. At the Darbar the next day, Jodh Singh left his followers outside and was received cordially by the Maharaja. However, Ranjit Singh suddenly made a sign to his people to seize Jodh Singh. Drawing his sword, Jodh Singh called on them to come on as he would not be taken alive and had never learned to turn his back on an enemy. Impressed by his courage, the Maharaja dismissed Jodh Singh with honor, rich presents, and an addition to his estates. However, when Jodh Singh died shortly afterward, leaving only infant sons, the Maharaja seized all his possessions.

Sardar Jassa Singh, aided by hill chieftain Sansar Chand, took back important towns such as Garh, Depalpur, Bhunga, and Hoshiarpur from the Ahluwalia Misl by force. Later, Jassa Singh gave Hoshiarpur in Jagir to Nawab Bhamboo Khan.

Jodh Singh Ramgarhia showed his loyalty to Maharaja Ranjit Singh by participating in the battle of Kasur and siding with him in expeditions against Multan and other adversaries. As a reward, Ranjit Singh gifted an elephant to Jodh Singh after the occupation of Kasur, and later gave him the pargana of Ghuman in Sager which had an annual revenue of twenty-five thousand rupees. Ranjit Singh also gave Jodh Singh eleven villages from the pargana of Sikhowala which had an annual revenue of twelve thousand rupees. The Maharaja held Jodh Singh in high regard and addressed him as 'Baba Ji,' always receiving him with great respect and seating him by his side during their meetings.

Jodh Singh remained hostile towards the Ahluwalias, Rani Nawab Bhamboo Khan, Muhar Singh, Hazura Singh, and Thakar Singh, whom he gave five villages in Jagir after they were dismissed by Sardar Fateh Singh Ahluwalia. When Sardar Fateh Singh and Sardarni Baghel Singh collected a force of twenty thousand men and marched against Jodh Singh, he, aided by Nidhan Singh, came forth to meet the enemy. A great battle was fought at Banola, in which Sardar Fateh Singh was worsted.

Sir Lepel Griffin mistakenly attributed a story to Sardar Jodh Singh Wazirabadia, but it was actually about Jodh Singh Ramgarhia. According to family records put together by Sardar Mangal Singh Ramgarhia, the Maharaja invited Jodh Singh to Lahore on a friendly visit but then attempted to seize him. Jodh Singh, suspecting the Maharaja's intention, had brought a large force with him but was asked to send them back, which he was too proud to do. When the Maharaja made a sign to seize him, Jodh Singh drew his sword and called on them to come on, showing his courage and impressing the Maharaja. As a result, Ranjit Singh dismissed Jodh Singh with honor, rich presents, and an addition to his estates. However, when Jodh Singh died, leaving only infant sons, the Maharaja seized all of his possessions.

SADAR JASSA SINGH TRAINING HIS SON JODH SINGH ON THE USE OF THE RIFLE

When Holkar, a Maratha leader, arrived in the Punjab at the invitation of Sardar Fateh Singh Ahluwalia, he plundered and destroyed nearly 50 villages belonging to Sardar Jodh Singh. In response, Sardar Jodh Singh sent two messengers, named Ral Singh and Lakha Singh, to Major Lake, who was pursuing Holkar. The British commander received them with formalities and honored them with robes of honor. He also sent a rifle and a field glass to Sardar Jodh Singh, which are still preserved in the family as heirlooms. After this, Sardar Jodh Singh formed a friendship with the Afghans of Kassur. Although Ranjit Singh attacked his territory several times, the Sardar, with the help of Afghans, always defeated him.

A pact of eternal friendship between Ranjit Singh and the Ramgarhia family was made before the Granth in the Holy Darbar Sahib at Amritsar. Ranjit Singh stamped the paper with his open palm dyed with saffron in his royal and illiterate way. To further attract Sardar Jodh Singh, he went to the Ramgarh fort almost unattended and expressed his admiration for the fortifications. He then ordered the foundation of a fort, later called Gobindgarh, to be laid down according to the same plan.

The simple-hearted Sardar Jodh Singh was won over and accompanied Maharaja Ranjit Singh with all his forces in the last successful expedition against Kutb-ud-din Khan. Mr. Prinsep, in his life of Maharaja Ranjit Singh, wrote, "Ranjit Singh, who was himself free-spoken and allowed great latitude in conversation to his courtiers, received a rebuke for the grasping disposition he displayed in his treatment of the old Sikh Sardars from Jodh Singh Ramgarhia, himself a reduced chief of the class. When taking his leave to join Muhkam Chand in the operations against the Faizulapurian Sardars, Ranjit Singh offered him presents as a mark of a favor. However, with characteristic frankness, Sardar Jodh Singh begged that such an honor might be dispensed with in his case, for he would consider himself fortunate in these times if allowed to keep his own turban on his head. Ranjit Singh took no offense at his freedom, but smiled and told him to be faithful and of good cheer."

Sardar Jodh Singh Ramgarhia was a brave and generous man who helped Maharaja Ranjit Singh in taking Kangra and subduing Raja Sansar Chand. He also assisted the Maharaja in the Multan expedition, and in return, the Maharaja gave him many villages in jagir that were previously owned by Sardar Golab Singh Kharara. These villages had an annual revenue of 25 thousand Rs. In 1811 AD, the Maharaja gave Sardar Jodh Singh 11 villages in Sheikhupura in jagir, along with an elephant. The annual revenue from these villages was Rs. 12,000.

MAHARAJA JASSA SINGH TEACHING HIS SONS THE ART OF SWORD FIGHTING

Sardar Jodh Singh was highly respected by the Maharaja, who called him Baba-Ji and gave him a seat next to him in the Darbar. He was also known for his generosity and offered protection to anyone who sought it, including Sardar Gulab Singh Bhangi and Bibi Lashmi of Phagwara, who were defeated by Ranjit Singh.

Sardar Jodh Singh was also a great builder and contributed significantly to the construction of the Golden Temple. He brought pieces of perforated marble from the four minarets of Jahangir's maqbara near Shahdara on the bank of Ravi, as well as finely tessellated and chequered pieces of marble from Muhammaden monuments situated as far as Delhi and Agra. He built the first two storeys of Baba Atal and the great Ramgarhia Bunga, with its two lofty towers. The family still possesses a Khanda and a Katar of this brave man as an heirloom.

Sardar Jodh Singh Ramgarhia passed away in 1816, leaving behind a legacy of bravery, generosity, and architectural contributions to the city of Amritsar.

The death of Sardar Jodh Singh triggered a family dispute, with Sardar Diwan Singh, Bir Singh, Mehtab Singh, and the widow of Sardar Jodh Singh all making claims for the estate. Upon hearing of this, the Maharaja summoned them to Nadaun where he was located, promising to resolve the conflict through arbitration. They were initially received with courtesy by the Maharaja, but soon left the reception tent, which was then surrounded by troops, and the three Ramgarhias were arrested. Ranjit Singh then marched towards Amritsar and, after some intense fighting, captured the fort of Ramgarh. He subsequently seized the vast Ramgarhia jagirs and conquered all their forts. Thus, the saffron bond was fulfilled.

There was a saying in the country that it would take a year to conquer the Ramgarhia family if they lost one fort per day, as the family possessed 360 forts. However, when fate was against them, a few hours were enough to change the family's fortunes. Sardar Wir Singh and Mahtab Singh were eventually released and placed under Sardar Lehna Singh of Majitha. A jagir worth 45,000 was settled on the family. Sardar Diwan Singh initially refused to accept his share of Rs. 12,000 and remained imprisoned, but eventually pretended to agree. After regaining his freedom, he fled to Patiala, where Maharaja Karam Singh welcomed him and he stayed for 10 months. Many old retainers of the Ramgarhia clan gathered around him. When their numbers swelled to a thousand, the Rajas of Patiala, Nabha, and Jind suspected that the Sardar was a secret agent sent by the Maharaja to seize their territories. Therefore, they asked him to keep only four or five hundred men and dismiss the others. The Sardar did not agree to the proposal and instead took friendly leave of them and wandered around for some time.

Later, at the intercession of some Sardars, the Maharaja gave him a jagir worth Rs. 97,000 per annum and sent him in command of 1,000 men to Baramoola cantonment in Kashmir. This was a challenging hill post on the road to Srinagar and located on the frontier of the Maharaja's territory. Additionally, as a perquisite, he was allowed to obtain shawls worth Rs. 7,000 annually from the Governor of Kashmir. In 1828, the Maharaja fell ill, and upon hearing of his illness, the Sardar came to Lahore with all his forces without any permission or information. Fortunately, the Maharaja quickly recovered, and he did not take offense at the Sardar's sudden arrival. Instead, he pleased him with many lavish gifts. The Sardar then returned to Baramoola, where he remained for 14 years until his death in 1834. His Sumadh, together with a large Dharmsala, is located on the banks of the River Jhelum. The house where the Sardar used to live was situated a few miles from Srinager, but all traces of it have disappeared. However, the place is still marked by some tall poplar trees and is known as the cantonment of Sardar Diwan Singh Ramgarhia. Sardar Bir Singh had died six years earlier in 1828, and 2/3 of his jagirs were resumed.

JODH SINGH'S SUCCESSORS

After Jodh Singh's death, his family members began to quarrel over the division of the Misal's possessions. Diwan Singh, who was the son of Tara Singh, Vir Singh (brother of Jodh Singh), and the widow of Jodh Singh all claimed the principality. Maharaja Ranjit Singh, upon hearing of their dispute, called the three claimants - Vir Singh, Diwan Singh, and Mehtab Singh (son of Khushal Singh and cousin brother of Jodh Singh) to him at Nadaun with the intention of settling their dispute through arbitration.

The Maharaja received them with courtesy, but they behaved so rudely towards each other that Ranjit Singh was obliged to keep them in detention. The Maharaja then marched on Amritsar and after some fighting, took the fort of Ramgarh. He seized all the Ramgarhia lagers and, in a short time, reduced all their forts, which numbered more than 150. These forts contained abundant provisions. Almost all of them were pulled down.

On the intercession of Sardar Chanda Singh Kanaihya, the Ramgarhia Sardars were released from jail and granted an annual jagir of 35,003 rupees. However, Diwan Singh refused to accept his share and fled to Patiala, where he was well received. He then moved around for some time. Maharaja Ranjit Singh sent word to Diwan Kalowala, Tibrah, and Kundilah worth 9,000 rupees, of which 3,600 rupees were personal, and 5,400 rupees were for services.

After his father's death, Mangal Singh was sent to Peshawar in command of 400 foot soldiers and 110 swars. There, he did commendable service under Hari Singh Nalwa and Tej Singh and fought in the famous battle of Jamrud in April 1837, where the brave Hari Singh Nalwa laid down his life.

In 1839, Mangal Singh was recalled and sent to the hill territories between the Beas and the Satluj under the orders of Lehna Singh Majithia. During the absence of that chief at Peshawar, he was placed in charge of the hill forts and was active in the suppression of the insurrection of 1840. During the reign of Maharaja Sher Singh, he was employed under Lehna Singh in Suket, Mandi, and Kulu, and he remained there till the close of the Satluj Anglo-Sikh war in 1846. During the Second Sikh War, Mangal Singh remained loyal to the British and served them in guarding the roads and maintaining order in the Amritsar and Gurdaspur districts. Later, he worked as a manager of the affairs of Harmandir Sahib, Amritsar.

Mangal Singh was a man of education and liberal ideas. It was mostly owing to his influence that the cause of female education was systematically taken up in Amritsar. Mangal Singh's two sons, Gurdit Singh and Mitt Singh, served the British government in the police and civil departments, respectively.

He was respectfully received by the Maharaja at Lahore and was given command of 700 men in the expedition then setting out for Kashmir. There, he remained in charge of Baramula, a difficult hill post, till his death in 1834.

The widows of Jodh Singh were given jagirs of four villages for their maintenance. Vir Singh was given Dharmkot Randhawa in jagir.
