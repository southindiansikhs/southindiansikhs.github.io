BHAGAT KABIR DAS JI

Bhagat Kabir Das Ji was a devoted spiritual poet who lived in Uttar Pradesh, India. He was a strict monotheist and likely the founder of Gurmat. In Guru Granth Sahib Ji, his writings consist of 227 Padas in 17 ragas and 237 slokas. Although born into a Muslim family, Kabir is revered by Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs alike. He served as a teacher and social reformer through his writings, and like other devotees, he did not believe in ritualism, the worship of deities, Brahmanism, the caste system, or the illusionary concepts of Hindu and Muslim priests. The Kabirpanthi sect follows his teachings and refers to him as their guru. Sikhs also follow Kabir's teachings, as in Gurmat, Kabir, Nanak, Ravidas, and the Bhatts are all considered the same and are revered as gurus. Sikhs show their respect by bowing before the Guru Granth Sahib Ji, which contains the teachings of many who shared similar thoughts about God.



EARLY LIFE

BIRTH

There are different opinions on the life history of Kabir. Some historians believe that he lived from 1398 to 1448, while others suggest the period from 1440 to 1518. It is widely accepted that he was born in 1398 AD, which is 71 years prior to Guru Nanak Dev Ji. Kabirpanthis, the followers of Kabir, claim that he lived up to the age of 120 years and died in 1518. However, Charlotte Vaudenville, a British scholar who relied on the research of Hazari Prasad Trivedi, leans towards the dates of 1448 as the probable year of Kabir's death.

Kabir was born in Banaras and was adopted by Neeru and his wife Neema, who named him Kabir, meaning "the Highest". He had a son named Kamal and a daughter named Kamali with Mata Loi. Though born into a Hindu family, he was brought up as a Muslim. Kabir worked as a weaver by profession and claimed that he was sent by the Lord himself.

BRAHMANISM

During the fifteenth century, Banaras was a hub of Brahmin orthodoxy and their center of learning. Brahmins held significant power over all aspects of life in the city. As a member of the low caste of Julaha, Kabir faced great difficulty in spreading his ideology. Despite this, he and his followers would congregate at a particular location in the city for meditation. Brahmins mocked him for preaching to prostitutes and other low-caste individuals, but Kabir used satire to denounce them and win the hearts of those around him. Today, there is no doubt that Saint Kabir is the most renowned and significant figure from the city of Banaras.

Despite being in Banaras (Shivpuri), Kabir could not find true spiritual knowledge. Instead, he encountered many hypocrites there, which he lamented in his writing "Sagal Janam Shivpuri Gavaya." Seeking genuine spiritual guidance, Kabir then traveled to Maghar where he found Gurmat, meaning "the company of holy men," and wrote more Bani which is now included in the Guru Granth Sahib Ji. When he returned to Banaras, he preached the same teachings. Kabir passed away in Magahar.

Although Ramanand Ji was Kabir's external Guru, Kabir ultimately received true knowledge from within himself. In fact, in the previous three ages, Kabir revealed himself under different names: Sat Sukrit, Munindra, and Karunamaya.

MUSLIMS INVADED BENARAS

During Kabir's time, the city of Banaras was devastated by an attack from a Muslim invader named Tamur Lang, also known as "Tamur the Lame." Kabir spoke out against the mullahs and their rituals of bowing towards the kaba five times a day, which made him a target of anger from both Hindus and Muslims in and around Benaras. Despite this opposition, Kabir traveled in and around Benaras to share his beliefs.

Kabir was the first Perfect Master to reveal the secrets of the entire creation to the world at large. This is evident in his two verses titled "Kar Nainon Deedar." However, he faced opposition from both pandits and maulvis (Muslim religious scholars).

Sultan Sikandar Lodi made various attempts to punish Kabir, including trying to drown him, burning him alive, and having him trampled by an elephant. Despite these efforts, Kabir's verses, which contain the highest secrets of Sant-Mat, continue to resonate with common people even today. He used strong language to denounce prevalent ritualism.

Kabir's works, including "Bijak," "Granthavali," "Shabdavali," and "Anurag Saagar," remain highly regarded. Among his disciples was the King of Benaras, and he had a host of other renowned followers such as Dharamdas Ji, Mir Taqi, Ganaka Ji, Pipa Ji, Dhanna Ji, and Sadana Ji.

DEATH

When Kabir passed away, there was a dispute between the Hindus and Muslims over who would perform his last rites. In honour of the great spiritual leader, a tomb and Samadhi Mandir were eventually built in Maghar. These structures still stand side by side as a testament to Kabir's legacy.

HISTORICAL FACTS OF KABIR'S LIFE IN GURU GRANTH SAHIB JI

KABIR'S MEMORIAL AT MAGHAR

AUTOBIOGRAPHY

WASTING OF LIFE IN KASHI AND RECIEVED GURMAT FROM MAGHAR

According to Gurbani, Bhagat Kabir spent his entire life in Shivpuri (Kashi, Banaras). In his old age, he moved to Maghar in Uttar Pradesh, where he finally found Gurmat, likely in the company of Saints. Kabir wrote Bijak during his time in Benaras, where he felt he had wasted his life and was unable to find Gurmat.

Gurbani also reveals that Kabir spent his time in Kashi in penance and meditation, which he later deemed as useless. He criticized the Saints of Benaras, calling them hypocrites who were caught up in Maya (temporal world) and had forgotten the name of God. It was only after coming to Maghar that he found what he was looking for.

Kabir also stated that after obtaining Gurmat from Maghar, he returned to Kashi and found God either in the company of Saints or within himself.

QUOTE FROM GURBANI

Here are the three Bani (verses) by Bhagat Kabir ji from Sri Guru Granth Sahib, along with their translations:

1 "The mother thinks that her son is growing up; she does not understand that, day by day, his life is diminishing. Calling him, 'Mine, mine', she fondles him lovingly, while the Messenger of Death looks on and laughs." (SGGS p91) In this verse, Kabir ji uses the example of a mother who loves and dotes on her son, but does not realize that his life is gradually coming to an end. The personification of Death as a "Messenger" who looks on and laughs suggests that death is inevitable and cannot be escaped by anyone.

2 "I was on fire, but now I have found the Water of the Lord's Name. This Water of the Lord's Name has cooled my burning body." (SGGS p323) In this verse, Kabir ji metaphorically compares his state of mind to a burning fire, which has been extinguished by the "Water of the Lord's Name". This suggests that Kabir ji has found spiritual solace and peace through devotion to the Lord's Name.

3 "Like the monkey with a handful of grain, who will not let go because of greed - just so, all the deeds committed in greed ultimately become a noose around one's neck. Without devotional worship, human life passes away in vain. Without the Saadh Sangat, the Company of the Holy, without vibrating and meditating on the Lord God, one does not abide in Truth." (SGGS p336) In this verse, Kabir ji compares the attachment to worldly possessions and desires to a monkey who holds onto a handful of grain out of greed, even though it may lead to its own destruction. Kabir ji emphasizes the importance of devotional worship and the company of the holy (Saadh Sangat) in order to live a life of truth and avoid the pitfalls of greed and attachment.

















KABIR BANI

Some of Bhagat Kabir's bani was excluded from the final compilation of Sri Guru Granth Sahib Ji by the 5th Guru, Guru Arjan Dev ji. The selections that were included were passed down to him from the 1st Guru, Guru Nanak Dev ji, through his janamsakhis.
