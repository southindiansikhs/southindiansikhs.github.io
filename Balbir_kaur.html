BIBI BALBIR KAUR JI

The Akali movement brought new life to the Gursikh community. For the first time since the Sikh Raj period, the Gursikhs asserted their religious independence and initiated non-violent efforts to gain control of their Gurdwaras. The tragic events at Nankana Sahib and Guru-Kae-Bagh only served to strengthen the movement, and as a result, the Sikhs raised slogans for India's freedom and for the independence of their Gurdwaras.

However, the level of commitment and self-sacrifice of the Sikhs deeply disturbed the British, who sensed a potential threat to their control from this small but determined community. The British directed their terror machinery against the Sikhs and arrested and confined all Akali sympathizers in jails.

The Maharaja of Nabha, Ripudaman Singh, was an independent-minded ruler who participated in Panthic observances and visited Akali leaders outside of the jails, deeply troubling the British. They saw this as a potential rebellion and swiftly initiated legal steps to seize control of Nabha rule and expel Maharaja Ripudaman Singh.

News of his expulsion spread quickly through the Sikh community, shaking the very core of Sikh psyche. The Shiromani Gurdwara Prabhandhak Committee (SGPC) and the Shiromani Akali Dal conducted Akhand Paths at various places to express their outrage at this injustice and demanded the reinstatement of the Maharaja. However, their efforts were met with further resistance as agents of the British empire, acting on British instructions, forcibly interrupted an Akhand Paath at Jaito Gurdwara by arresting the Granthi who was reciting the Paath.

This incident was a deep blow to the Sikhs' religious sentiments and poured salt over open wounds. The expulsion of Maharaja Ripudaman Singh was a political affair that the Panth was still struggling to grapple with, and the forced interruption of the Akhand Paath only added to their troubles.

The interruption of the Akhand Paath was seen as a direct challenge to the freedom and honor of the Guru Khalsa Panth, and the Akali leaders decided to respond with a non-violent protest. Thousands of Gursikhs, including men, women, children, and elders, gathered in Amritsar to show their support for the cause and were eager to reach Jaito to complete the interrupted Akhand Paath. However, the Akali committee decided to send a Jatha of only 500 Gursikhs and asked the rest to await the schedule for the next Jatha. Despite their eagerness, the Gursikhs accepted their Jathedar's decision and left for Jaito after seeking the Hukam from Sri Akal Takhat and pledging to remain non-violent.

The Jatha was accompanied by thousands of supporters who cheered them on with the Jaekara, "Jo Bolay So Nihal, Sat Sri Akal." The Singhnias were not allowed to join the Jatha, but they successfully obtained permission to accompany them for organizing langar along the way.

The non-violent march of the Jatha was a remarkable event for the entire world. The Saint-Soldiers walked barefoot in rows of four from Amritsar while reciting "Satnaam VaahGuru." When they reached their first rest-stop, the local Sikhs demonstrated their dedication and volunteer sewa, which testified to the world that the Sikh nation not only understood non-violence and martyrdom but also knew how to honor its martyrs.

It became apparent at the first rest-stop that the services of the Singhnia who had accompanied the Jatha for organizing langar were not needed. The Jathedar asked them to return, and while many did, several wanted to continue with their brave brothers and did not return.

One such woman was Balbir Kaur. When the Jathedar asked her to return, tears filled her eyes. She said, "Veer! Do not stop me from serving the living martyrs of Guru Gobind Singh. Sewa is the only essence of this life. Besides, we never know when death will come upon us. I plead for permission to continue for Guru's sake. Let me proceed." The Jathedar could not break her heart and reluctantly gave permission, especially when faced with the display of self-sacrifice.

Balbir Kaur was 22 years old, full of youth and beauty. Guru's faith and feelings of selfless service for humanity had generated a glow on her face that made her seem like a goddess of purity or an angel. She was not alone; she was accompanied by her one-year-old son, whose playful and happy face brought joy and amusement to the entire Jatha.

As the journey neared completion, the Jatha prepared to depart from its final rest-stop. The Jathedar stood on a high spot and pleaded for the return of the accompanying congregation. British forces had dug in with machine guns, and this information had previously reached the Jatha. The Jathedar did not hide this information from anyone and said, "With Guru's blessing, a martyr's mela is being organized. However, only those Gursikhs who have Sri Akal Takhat's Hukam should proceed further. Others should return and await their turn."

The congregation stopped and allowed the Jatha to proceed, but not everyone followed the Jathedar's orders. Some Gursikhs, eager for martyrdom, found hidden routes parallel to the established route of the Jatha. They advanced in hiding, with the intention of joining the martyrdom program when it was unveiled. However, Bibi Balbir Kaur did not take any hidden route. She continued marching with her brothers while her son enjoyed the view, watching people on either side.

When the Jathedar learned that Balbir Kaur was still marching with the Jatha, he left his leading position and caught up with her. "Bibi, there is potential for firing ahead. You should not proceed any further," the Jathedar pleaded.

"My Veer! Do not stop me. My quest for sewa has not been fulfilled yet. Let me enjoy this sewa. You tell me about the dangers of potential firing ahead? Five hundred Veers are with me. Since they are going towards certain death, why shouldn't they be accompanied by a Bahan (sister)? I too have partaken Gurus Amrit. I will consider myself blessed if I can accept martyrdom along with my brothers and reach Guru Gobind Singh's court. Here, my quest has not been fulfilled by serving my Veers," Balbir Kaur pleaded with tears in her eyes. "But..." the Jathedar was about to say something, when he was interrupted by Balbir Kaur saying, "My child, this is what you wanted to point out. He too is Guru's blessing. If he too serves the Panth, what greater deeds could be beyond this?" Saying this, Balbir Kaur hugged her child who burst out laughing.

Despite the pressure from Jathedar and others, Balbir Kaur remained steadfast in her decision to march to her death with her brothers. She argued that the absence of a female participant in the impending martyrdom was an insult to the courageous daughters of the Tenth Guru. She couldn't bear the thought of such sacrilege in a faith that promotes gender equality and had transformed women into Singhnia through Guru's amrit. Her persuasive arguments eventually convinced her fellow brothers, even the Jathedar, who had to concede to her spirit of sacrifice and bravery. They resumed their march towards the Jaito Gurdwara with Guru Khalsa's Kesri flag fluttering freely in the wind, and the band accompanying them added an innocent charm to their presence. The Sant-Sipahi force marched towards their destination under the guidance of their faith in Sri Guru Granth Sahib and the command of their Jathedar, chanting "Satnaam VaahGuru" with a sense of calmness projected by every GurSikh in the Jatha.

The Jatha received warm welcomes from people of all religions along the way from Amritsar to Jaito, who provided them with milk, kheer, and other refreshments. They were greeted with flowers and thousands of rupees were donated in their honor. Even those who served the British welcomed them, but not with open arms. Instead, they showered the brave Gursikhs with bullets from their rifles and guns. The non-violent force of the Gurus was prepared for this and continued on their peaceful walk towards their goal, chanting "Satnaam Sri VaahGuru" in obedience to their Guru's command.

Despite the violent welcome, the Jatha continued on without any interruption, with the scene appearing as though they were playing the festival of Holi. However, instead of colored powder, blood was shed, with some of the Gursikhs being hit and falling to the ground, only to rise up again and continue their march towards martyrdom. Balbir Kaur was among them, hugging her child as she continued her march with a glowing face, eagerly awaiting the rain of bullets that she believed would lead her to the ultimate goal of accepting martyrdom and reaching the Kalgidhar father's lap. Her spirit of sacrifice and courage had inspired her brothers to accept her position and continue on this path towards their holy war.

Suddenly, Balbir Kaur was hit by a bullet in her forehead and blood gushed out. Her face and eyes were covered in blood, but she did not stop her march. She continued chanting "Satnaam VaahGuru" while her child played with the blood on her face. Another bullet then hit her child through his ear and then struck Balbir Kaur's chest, killing the child instantly. Balbir Kaur kissed her child's forehead and placed his body on a nearby platform, saying "VaahGuru, look after your amanat (temporarily entrusted to me for safe custody)." However, she did not stop her march. Her face turned yellow from blood loss, and she was weak and unsteady, but her heart's quest had not been quenched. She continued chanting "Satnaam VaahGuru" and kept pace with others, while bullets continued to rain down on them.

Suddenly, another bullet struck Balbir Kaur's chest, piercing her body and exiting from the other side. This bullet was the message of death that she had eagerly awaited. With this bullet, her beautiful body fell to the ground, but her soul left to join her child in Kalgidhar Father's protection. Her deepest quest was finally fulfilled. Her blood-filled face still exhibited peace and bravery. This account is taken from "Adarshak Singhnia" by Karam Singh and translated by Baldev Singh in "Daughters of the Khalsa."

The Movement for the liberation of Gurdwaras from corrupt priests (Mahants) who were protected by the British, had awakened the Sikhs to assert their religious independence through non-violent efforts. However, the British rulers responded with violence by gunning down many participants and arresting thousands to suppress the movement. Bibi Balbir Kaur's life story is an example of how Sikh women were equally involved in the movement and willingly sacrificed themselves and their children in a non-violent way. Their joint struggle eventually forced the British to yield.

Ripudaman Singh, the ruler of Nabha state (now part of Punjab), had a soft corner for the movement and was an independent-minded ruler, which deeply troubled the British. He was not on good terms with the ruler of the Patiala state, but instead of settling the quarrel through imperial arbitration, the British officials forced Ripudaman Singh to abdicate. This forced abdication led to a strong agitation among the Sikhs. British officials in the Nabha state forcibly dispersed a meeting of the Sikhs who had gathered in the Gurdwara Gangsar at Jaito, a city in the Nabha state, and interrupted the Akhand Path, a continued recitation of the Sri Guru Granth Sahib for forty-eight hours, which had been started by the Sikhs to pray for the restoration of Ripudaman Singh's rights. This interference in their right to worship freely led to a strong agitation by the Sikhs.

The Shromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee (SGPC), the supreme body for the management of the Gurdwaras with its headquarters at Amritsar, accepted the challenge and declared a state of war. The SGPC had already liberated Gurdwaras at Nankana Sahib (the birthplace of Guru Nanak) and the Golden Temple at Amritsar, and had won the struggle against the government at Guru Ka Bagh near Amritsar. A band of martyrs started from Akal Takht, Amritsar to Jaito to start the Akandh Path there. Every member of the band took a pledge to remain non-violent at any cost and to sacrifice their life if attacked. At first, a band of 25 volunteers left Amritsar daily for Jaito, but they were forcibly stopped by the Nabha administration from entering the Gurdwara. Many of them lost their lives, but the result was not satisfactory.

The SGPC decided to send five hundred people daily, and the first batch of martyrs left the Akal Takhat on February 9, 1924. Every one of them took a pledge to remain non-violent if fired upon or tortured, and to start the Akhand Path at the Gurdwara in Jaito on February 21, 1924. The number of volunteers was much more than required, and thousands were asked to wait for their turn. Women were initially not allowed to proceed with the band, but they insisted on participating in such a holy endeavor. They succeeded in accompanying the band on the plea that they would cook food along the way. Bibi Balbir Kaur, with her two-year-old son, was the leader of the women volunteers.

The first group of five hundred individuals left Amritsar amidst the echoes of "Jo Bole, So Nihal, Sat Sri Akal." They were all garlanded and in high spirits, as thousands gathered to wish them success. This unarmed, non-violent march was a unique event being watched by the whole world. The group, organized in rows of four, proceeded on foot from Amritsar reciting "Sat Nam Waheguru." At their first rest stop, the local Sikhs honored them, served them, and took care of their food and other necessities. After the first stop, it became clear that the services of the women for cooking food would not be required. The group leader asked them to return, and while others agreed, Balbir Kaur wanted to continue the march with her brave brothers and refused to return. When the leader asked her to go back, she tearfully said, "Brother, don't stop me from participating in this noble struggle. I am not afraid of death. I request you to let me proceed with my brothers." The leader reluctantly permitted her, keeping in view her display of self-sacrifice. Balbir Kaur was a beautiful 22-year-old woman, full of youth, determination, and feelings of selfless service. Her innocent two-year-old child was a source of amusement for every member of the party.

When the group reached near their destination, the leaders stopped and announced, "The British forces are ready to face us with machine guns. I request the accompanying congregation to return. Only those permitted by the Akal Takhat should proceed further, and others should return and wait for their turn." Some returned, while others found hidden routes to join the band to seek martyrdom when the firing would start. Bibi Balbir Kaur, with her son, did not adopt any hidden route and continued to march with the other members of the party. When the leader came to know that Bibi Balbir Kaur was marching with the band, he stopped and approached her, saying, "Sister, there is a real danger of firing ahead. You should not continue further." She replied with folded hands, "Dear brother, don't stop me. I am also a baptized one and shall deem myself fortunate if I am martyred along with 500 brothers. Our Gurus have bestowed equality to women. It is sacrilegious that I should not be allowed to proceed further. If you are worried about my child, let him serve the community. He cannot get a better chance in life." Saying these words, she hugged her child and could not talk further due to tears in her eyes. The leader persuaded her to return, and other members of the party also pressured her to go back. She did not budge from her decision to march to death with the others. Her persuasive arguments and burning desire for sacrifice forced everybody to accept her plea. Even the leader was forced to accept her decision. The saint-soldiers, with their yellow flag fluttering in the air, once again started their march under the command of their leader, with faith in their mission. Their brave faces projected calmness, and everyone seemed fully prepared to sacrifice their lives. People of every faith welcomed them on their way and served them with sweets and milk. Flowers were showered upon these living martyrs.

As soon as the party entered the boundaries of the Nabha state, the state police and British forces warned them not to proceed. However, the saint-soldiers continued their march despite the warning. Upon reaching the city of Jaito, they were welcomed with a shower of bullets. Despite the intense gunfire, they continued to meditate and chant "Sat Nam Waheguru", never showing their backs. Blood was shed everywhere, resembling a Holi festival of blood. Although many fell to the ground, they courageously rose and continued their march. Some died on the spot and became martyrs. Bibi Balbir Kaur continued marching while hugging her child, waiting eagerly for the bullet that would end her life. Finally, a bullet hit her forehead, covering her face with blood. Despite the injury, she continued marching with a smile on her face, while her child played with the flowing blood on her face. Sadly, a stray bullet struck and killed the child. Kissing her child's forehead and placing the body with the wounded and killed, she rushed forward to encourage her fellow band members to face the bullets bravely. Though her face turned yellow for the loss of blood and her steps were unsteady, she kept pace with the others. Upon reaching the Gurdwara, she thanked God for giving her a chance to reach there. The bullets continued to rain, and she was hit by a bullet that pierced her chest and took her life. Her soul left to join her child, and her cherished desire was fulfilled. Balbir Kaur became immortal, and her heroic deed is mentioned in many books. Such sacrifices created a new sense of life among the Sikhs in those days. The agitation continued for twenty-one months, and many groups of five hundred followed one another. In the end, the British government yielded, and the Sikhs completed not one but one hundred and one Akhand Paths in that Gurdwara. Balbir Kaur has become immortalized and her heroic actions have been documented in many books. In fact, such sacrifices brought about a new sense of vitality and purpose amongst the Sikh community during that time.
