MATA GUJRI JI

Mata Gujri was the daughter of Bhai Lal Chand Subulikka and Bishan Kaur, a devout couple from Kartarpur, located in present-day Kapurthala district of Punjab. Lal Chand had migrated from his ancestral village, Lakhnaur, in Ambala district, to settle at Kartarpur, where his daughter Gujri was married to (Guru) Tegh Bahadur on February 4, 1633. The betrothal had taken place four years earlier when Tegh Bahadur had come to Kartarpur as part of his elder brother, Suraj Mall's marriage party. Bishan Kaur, the mother, had been struck by Tegh Bahadur's handsome face and she and her husband had pledged their daughter's hand to him. After the wedding, the couple settled in Amritsar. Bride Gujri was admired by everyone, as recorded in Gurbilas Chhevi Patshahi, which states "Gujri is destined to be worthy of Tegh Bahadur in every way, just like the bridegroom himself."

In 1635, Mata Gujri left Amritsar with her family and went to live in Kartarpur, located in the foothills of the Sivalik mountains. After Guru Hargobind Singh Ji passed away in 1644, Mata Gujri, along with her husband and mother-in-law, Mata Nanaki, moved to Bakala, now in the Amritsar district of Punjab. There, they lived a peaceful life, with Guru Tegh Bahadur dedicating his time to meditation while Mata Gujri took care of the household duties. When Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji was installed as the Guru in 1664, he and Mata Gujri visited Amritsar and later founded a new habitation called Chakk Nanaki (later known as Anandpur) in 1665.

Shortly after, Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji, along with his mother and wife, embarked on a journey to the east, leaving the family at Patna. He went on to travel to Bengal and Assam, while Mata Gujri gave birth to their son, Gobind Rai (later Guru Gobind Singh) on December 22, 1666, in Patna. Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji returned to Patna for a short stay in 1670 before leaving for Delhi, instructing the family to proceed to Lakhnaur, now in Haryana.

In 1635, Mata Gujri left Amritsar with her family and settled in Kartarpur. Later, they moved to Bakala where she lived as a devoted housewife while her husband, Guru Tegh Bahadur, focused on his spiritual practice. After becoming the Guru in 1664, they traveled to Amritsar and then founded a new habitation named Chakk Nanaki. In 1666, Mata Gujri gave birth to their son, Gobind Rai, who later became the tenth Sikh Guru, Guru Gobind Singh. After traveling to the east and returning to Patna, they went to Lakhnaur and then settled in Chakk Nanaki. When Guru Tegh Bahadur was executed in Delhi in 1675, the responsibility of managing the affairs at Chakk Nanaki initially fell on Mata Gujri. She showed courage during this difficult time and was assisted by her younger brother, Kirpal Chand.

During a prolonged siege by hostile hill rajas and Mughal troops, Guru Gobind Singh Ji was forced to evacuate Chakk Nanaki (Anandpur) on the night of December 5-6, 1705. At that time, Mata Gujri was with her younger grandsons, Zorawar Singh Ji and Fateh Singh Ji, who were nine and seven years old respectively. While crossing the rivulet Sarsa, they were separated from the main body, and their servant, Gangu, led them to his village, Saheri, near Morinda in present-day Ropar district. However, Gangu betrayed them to the local Muslim officer, and on December 8, Mata Gujri and her grandsons were arrested and confined in Sirhind Fort, where they were held in what Sikh chronicles refer to as Thanda Burj, the cold tower.

As the young children were repeatedly summoned to appear in court, their grandmother urged them to remain steadfast in their faith. On December 11, they were ordered to be bricked up alive in a wall, but the masonry crumbled before it could cover their heads, and they were executed the next day. Meanwhile, Mata Gujri ji was imprisoned on top of a tower that was open to all sides and exposed to the cold weather without any warm clothes, in the very cold month of December. Mata Gujri ji attained martyrdom on the same day as her grandsons. It is worth noting that Guru Nanak Sahib ji had said, "Why isn't a woman equal to a man when she is the one who gave birth to kings and protectors of Dharma?" Mata Gujri ji played such an important role in Sikhism through her upbringing of her grandsons that as Sikhs, we owe our existence to her. It was due to her teachings that the 6-year-old and 9-year-old did not waver from their Dharma and attained martyrdom, continuing and emphasizing the institution of martyrdom in Sikhism. Seth Todar Mall, a kind-hearted wealthy man from Sirhind, cremated the three dead bodies the next day.

The shrine of Gurdwara Mata Gujri (Thanda Burj) is located in Fatehgarh Sahib, near Sirhind, where Mata Gujri spent the last four days of her life. About one kilometer southeast of it is Gurdwara Joti Sarup, which marks the site of her cremation. A small domed pavilion made of white marble on the ground floor is dedicated to Mata Gujri. Sikhs from near and far visit this place to pay their respects and honor her memory, especially during the three-day fair held from 11-13 Poh, which falls in the last week of December according to the Bikrami calendar.

Sources: Trilochan Singh's "Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji" (1967) and Harbans Singh's "Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji" (1982).

Mata Gujri holds a unique place in Sikh history as she was the first Sikh woman to be martyred. She was also the wife of a martyr (Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji), mother of a martyr (Guru Gobind Singh Ji), grandmother of four martyr Sahibzadas (Ajit Singh Ji, Jujhar Singh Ji, Zorawar Singh Ji, and Fateh Singh Ji), sister of a martyr (Kirpal Chand), and aunt of five martyr sons of Bibi Viro, sister of Guru Tegh Bahadar Ji.

Mata Gujri was born to Bhai Lal Chand and Bibi Bishan Kaur, who were a devout couple living in Kartarpur, near Jalandhar in Punjab. Tyag Mal, who later became known as Guru Tegh Bahadur, visited Kartarpur with the marriage party of his elder brother, Suraj Mal. Gujri's parents were impressed by Tyag Mal and arranged for their daughter to marry him. The wedding was held in Kartarpur in 1633, as it was customary to have child marriages in those days.

Mata Gujri was highly respected by Tyag Mal's family after their marriage. A historian wrote that "Gujri was destined to be worthy of Tyag Mal in every way." She was a noble, beautiful, cultured, sweet-tongued, humble, and tender-hearted woman, as well as a devoted wife and daughter-in-law who was always happy. In 1635, while they were living in Kartarpur, she witnessed her husband's bravery during a battle against Mughal forces that had attacked Guru Hargobind Singh Ji. The Guru was so impressed with Tyag Mal's bravery that he gave him the title of Tegh Bahadur Singh Ji (Lord of the Sword), by which he would be known in history.

After Guru Hargobind Singh Ji's death in 1644, Tegh Bahadur, along with his mother and wife, moved from Kiratpur near Anandpur to the village of Bakala near Amritsar, where they lived for twenty-one years. The family owned some land there and lived comfortably on its income. They even had a cellar dug into the floor to avoid the oppressive heat of summer. When free, Tegh Bahadur meditated in solitude in the cellar (called the Bhaura), while Mata Gujri dutifully served her husband and mother-in-law as a devoted housewife.

In 1664, when Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji became the ninth Guru, he, his mother, wife, and some followers went to Hari Mandar in Amritsar to pay their respects, and from there they went to Kiratpur. In 1665, Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji purchased a large tract of land and founded a new town, Chakk Nanki, named after his mother. It is now known as Anandpur Sahib (Abode of Bliss). The family left for East India to spread Sikhism and avoid the intrigues of selfish and frustrated relatives.

In the course of their travels, the group stopped in the city of Gaya where Ram Singh, a Rajput prince, asked Guru Tegh Bahadur for his help in negotiating a settlement with the king of Assam. The guru agreed to accompany him, leaving his family in the care of his brother-in-law and devotees in Patna, Bihar. Before departing, Guru Tegh Bahadur wrote letters to his followers in Patna instructing them to take good care of Mata Gujri and the family, which demonstrated his affection for her. While Guru Tegh Bahadur Singh Ji was on tour, Mata Gujri gave birth to Gobind Rai, later known as Guru Gobind Singh Ji, in Patna in 1666. Everyone was overjoyed, and Mata Gujri devoted herself to caring for her newborn son and distributing alms to the poor. When Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji returned to Patna in 1670, he stayed for a few days before returning to Punjab, leaving his family in Patna due to unsettled conditions in Punjab and the young age of his son. The family eventually left Patna and stayed for a few days at Lakhnaur near Ambala with Mata Gujri's brother in 1671, where a well still commemorates her visit. The whole family finally arrived at Chakk Nanki in 1671 and was warmly welcomed by the people.

In 1675, a group of Pandits from Kashmir visited Guru Tegh Bahadur and expressed their grievances against the Governor of Kashmir who was forcing them to convert to Islam on the orders of Aurangzeb. At that moment, Gobind Rai, who was only 8 years old, appeared and inquired about the visitors' sadness. The Guru explained the situation to his son and the young boy suggested that his father should sacrifice his life to save the Pandits. The Guru was moved by his son's words and told the Pandits to inform the governor that they would only convert if Guru Tegh Bahadur himself did so first.

Knowing that his end was near, Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji nominated Gobind Rai as his successor. In July 1675, the Guru was arrested for supporting the Pandits and taken to Delhi with three companions for the ultimate sacrifice. Mata Gujri showed remarkable courage during the difficult time of their parting. During her husband's absence, she slept on the floor, ate only once a day, and prayed with great devotion. Eventually, Guru Tegh Bahadur was executed in Delhi in November 1675 along with his companions who also refused to renounce their faith. Mata Gujri bore this tremendous trial with remarkable fortitude.

After the execution of Guru Tegh Bahadur, a Sikh named Jaita secretly brought his head to Guru Gobind Rai at Chakk Nanki. Mata Gujri accepted the situation as God's will and took on the responsibility of managing the family affairs and raising Gobind Rai as a prince, providing him with education and military training for the future mission. From 1675-1684, the family stayed in Chakk Nanki, during which time Mata Gujri wrote letters to Masands, showing her determination to correct the dishonest ones while remaining kind-hearted.

Mata Gujri enthusiastically celebrated the marriage of Gobind Rai in 1677 to Jito, whom she named Sundri. When Gobind Rai decided to create a nation of Saint Soldiers in 1699 and asked for five Sikhs willing to sacrifice their lives, Mata Gujri, now a grandmother, encouraged her grandson to proceed with the baptism but consoled those who were afraid to participate. Later, when Guru Gobind Singh abolished the Masands and punished the corrupt ones, some approached Mata Gujri to intercede on their behalf, but she refused, knowing their true nature.

In December 1704, Guru Gobind Singh Ji was forced to leave the fort at Anandpur due to a long siege by Mughal troops. During their escape, Mata Gujri and her two younger grandsons, Zorawar Singh and Fateh Singh, aged 8 and 5 respectively, were separated from the rest of the family while crossing the flooded rivulet Sirsa. They were led by their cook, Gangu, to his village, Saher, near Ropar. However, during the night, Gangu stole their bag containing money. When confronted, he feigned ignorance and protested against the accusations, informing the village headman. As a result, Mata Gujri and her grandsons were arrested and taken to the governor of Sirhind, who confined them to the fort known as Thanda Burj, or the "cold tower". Although it was a summer resort for officers, it was most uncomfortable for an eighty-year-old lady and two children without any warm clothes in the month of December. Mata Gujri knew what was going to happen to them and advised her grandsons to never give up their faith under any circumstances. She reminded them of the sacrifice made by their grandfather, Guru Tegh Bahadur, who preferred death over conversion, and also recounted the sacrifice of Guru Arjan Sahib. She prepared them to face the governor boldly and not to bow before him.

Governor Wazir Khan was the Guru's bitter enemy and was furious for failing to capture him. He was determined to exert his power over the young children. Zorawar Singh and Fateh Singh were summoned to court and when they refused to bow before the governor, they uttered "Waheguru ji ki Khalsa, Waheguru ji ki Fateh." The governor was angered and threatened to torture them if they did not convert to Islam. However, the young boys stood firm in their faith and refused to give it up. The governor gave them twenty-four hours to consider his offer and promised them gifts and comfortable lives if they agreed. He also warned them that they would be buried alive if they refused. The boys told Mata Gujri about this and she kissed them and advised them to remain steadfast in their faith.

The next day, the governor made the same tempting offers, but the boys rejected them again. They were then ordered to be buried alive. The chief of the Malerkotla state, Sher Muhammad Khan, who was present, pleaded for their lives but to no avail. When the boys were being buried alive, they pushed down the temporary structure built of mud and bricks. As a result, they were beheaded on December 27, 1704. When Mata Gujri was informed of her grandsons' martyrdom, she thanked God for giving them a dignified death. She closed her eyes and meditated while breathing her last breath. She accepted her fate without complaint, singing Gurbani, and thus attained martyrdom on the same day as her grandsons. Today, there is a Gurdwara called Fateh Garh Sahib at the site in their memory.

Todar Mal, a wealthy merchant, purchased land at a high cost to cremate the three deceased bodies. Later, a Gurdwara named Joti Sarup was erected at the site where the Sahibzadas were beheaded. Two tombs made of white marble were built within the four walls to honor the Sahibzadas, while the one dedicated to Mata Gujri is located on the ground floor, symbolizing that the grandmother is still watching over her grandsons. At Fateh Garh Sahib near Sirhind, there is a shrine called Gurdwara Mata Gujri (Thanda Burj), where she spent her last three days. Every year, devotees from near and far come to pay homage to her memory during the last week of December.

Mata Gujri's life story is an inspiration for generations. Her name is uttered with respect as she chose faith over a comfortable life. Through her teachings and guidance, her young grandsons set an example for others. We Sikhs owe our existence to her, and she continued to emphasize the importance of martyrdom in Sikhism.
