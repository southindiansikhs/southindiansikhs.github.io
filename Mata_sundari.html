MATA SUNDARI JI

Mata Sundari Ji, the wife of Guru Gobind Singh Ji Singh , was the daughter of Bhai Ram Saran, a Kumaran Khatri from Bijvara in present-day Hoshiarpur district of Punjab. She married Guru Gobind Singh on 4 April 1684 in Anandpur. On 26 January 1687, she gave birth to Sahibzada Ajit Singh, the eldest son of Guru Gobind Singh. When Anandpur was evacuated on the night of 16 December 1705, Mata Sundari, along with Mata Sahib Devan, was escorted by Bhai Mani Singh to Delhi. In 1706, she rejoined Guru Gobind Singh Ji at Talvandi Sabo, where she received the news of her son's martyrdom and the deaths of her aged mother-in-law, Mata Gujari, and the other Sahibzada’s.

After Guru Gobind Singh Ji's departure from Talvandi Sabo to the South, Mata Sundari stayed in Delhi. There, she adopted a young boy who bore a striking resemblance to her late son and named him Ajit Singh Ji. Following Guru Gobind Singh Ji's passing in October 1708, the Sikh community looked to her for guidance. Mata Sundari appointed Bhai Mani Singh to manage the sacred shrines at Amritsar and tasked him with collecting the writings of Guru Gobind Singh Ji. She also issued hukamnamas to sangats under her own seal and authority, with the discovered hukamnamas bearing dates between 12 October 1717 and 10 August 1730.

Mata Sundari was disappointed in her adopted son, Ajit Singh Ji, who was treated by Emperor Bahadur Shah as Guru Gobind Singh Ji's successor. The emperor invited him to his court and bestowed upon him a robe of honour in September 1710. This newfound status went to Ajit Singh's head, and he began living extravagantly as a courtier, growing arrogant and even haughty towards Mata Sundari. She disowned him, and he later migrated to Mathura. However, Ajit Singh's behaviour worsened, and he was convicted for murder and executed on 18 January 1725.

Mata Sundari returned to live in Delhi, where she passed away in 1747. A memorial in her honour stands in the compound of Gurdwara Bala Sahib in New Delhi.

The misconception that Guru Gobind Singh Ji had multiple wives was propagated by writers who were not familiar with Punjabi culture. Later, other authors accepted these incorrect writings as fact and portrayed it as a sign of royalty. In those days, kings, chiefs, and other important people often had multiple wives as a symbol of their elevated status. However, this was not the case for Guru Gobind Singh Ji.

In Punjab, there are typically two or three major ceremonies associated with a wedding, including the engagement, wedding ceremony, and Muklawa. Large gatherings and music are common at all three events. In many cases, the engagement ceremony would occur as soon as the individual had passed infancy. Even today, it is not uncommon in some parts of India for engagements to take place between the ages of 8 and 12. The wedding ceremony would take place a couple of years after the engagement. After the wedding, it would take another few years for the bride to move in with her in-laws and begin living with them. This final stage is called Muklawa. During the Muklawa ceremony, a dowry and other gifts would be given to the bride to help her establish a new home. Nowadays, weddings and Muklawa are often combined and only take place once the partners are adults.

A grand engagement ceremony was held at Anandpur as per the customs during the time of Guru Gobind Singh's engagement to Mata Jito Ji. However, the bride resided in Lahore, which was the capital of the Mughal rulers who were not on good terms with the Gurus. Due to the security risks and the inconvenience to the Sangat, it was decided that the Guru would not travel to Lahore for the wedding ceremony along with the armed Sikhs in large numbers. Moreover, it would involve a lot of traveling, huge expenses, and logistical issues for the Sangat, including the elderly and young, who wished to attend the ceremony.

As mentioned in the Sikh chronicles, a beautiful camp was built at a scenic place located a couple of miles north of Anandpur for the wedding, which was named Guru Ka Lahore. Thus, instead of the Guru traveling to Lahore, Lahore was brought to Anandpur Sahib for the marriage ceremony. Today, this place is visited by people who go to Anandpur. The bride was brought to Guru Ka Lahore by her parents, and the marriage was celebrated with a grand gathering attending the ceremony.

The two grand ceremonies, one during the engagement and the other during the wedding of the Guru, led outsiders to believe that the Guru had two marriages. This misconception arose because the Guru's wife, Mata Jito Ji, was given the affectionate name of Sundari (beautiful) by the Guru's mother after their marriage, as per the custom in Punjab. This, along with the two elaborate functions, gave outsiders the impression that the Guru had two wives, when in reality, he had only one wife with two names, as previously explained.

There are some historians who suggest that Guru Gobind Singh Ji had a third wife, Mata Sahib Kaur. In 1699, during the founding of the Khalsa Panth, the Guru asked Mata Sahib Kaur to put puffed sugar in the water for preparing Amrit. While Guru Gobind Singh Ji is considered the spiritual father of the Khalsa, Mata Sahib Kaur is recognized as the spiritual mother of the Khalsa. However, some people who are not familiar with the Amrit ceremony mistakenly assume that Mata Sahib Kaur was the wife of Guru Gobind Singh. It is important to note that Guru Gobind Singh Ji is the spiritual but not the biological father of the Khalsa, and while Mata Sahib Devan is recognized as the spiritual mother of the Khalsa, she was not the wife of Guru Gobind Singh Ji.

Some writers have mistakenly assumed that Guru Gobind Singh Ji had three wives due to their lack of knowledge of Punjabi culture and the Amrit ceremony. However, the truth is that the Guru had only one wife with two names, and the third woman was not his wife. It is believed that the parents of Mata Sahib Devan had proposed her marriage to the Guru, but as he was already married, he could not accept the proposal. However, he allowed her to stay at Anandpur without accepting her as his wife. To address the dilemma of her desire to have children without being married, the Guru made a statement that she would be the "mother" of a great son who would live forever and be known all over the world. This statement was only understood after the Guru associated Mata Sahib Devan with the preparation of Amrit by bringing patasas. Therefore, it is due to a lack of understanding that some writers consider Mata Sahib Devan as the worldly wife of Guru Gobind Singh Ji.

Mata Sundari, the respected consort of Guru Gobind Singh Ji, was a prominent leader of the Sikhs for 40 years after her husband's passing. Her life story exemplifies her commitment to truth and high values, and she fearlessly led her husband's followers during a critical time. Among Indian women leaders, she holds a preeminent rank. Originally named Jito, brides in those times were given new names by their in-laws after marriage, and she was named Sundari after her wedding. Born in Lahore in 1670, her father Harjas Subhikhi was a wealthy and respected man in the capital of Punjab. Out of respect, she is referred to as Mata (mother) Sundari. Growing up, she lived a happy and luxurious life under the care of her father, who was head of a clan. While there is no information available about her schooling, her literary tastes suggest that she was well-educated.

As was customary in those days, her father began searching for a suitable match for her at a young age. He was a devotee of Guru Tegh Bahadur and had encountered Gobind Rai during his visits to Anandpur. He selected the young Gobind Rai as his prospective son-in-law and approached Mata Gujri, Gobind Rai's mother, and his maternal uncle, Kirpal Chand, with the proposal. They accepted the proposal, but Harjas wanted the marriage to take place in Lahore. Gobind Rai was not in favor of this plan due to the prevailing circumstances and instead promised to establish a new city located 10 miles away from Anandpur before the wedding. Thus, Guru Gobind Rai founded a new city called Guru Ka Lahore or New Lahore.

Historians record that the new city of Guru Ka Lahore, built by Guru Gobind Rai for his marriage with Mata Sundari, was as busy and lively as the old Lahore. While the city still exists under the same name, it is now just a village. The wedding of Mata Sundari was celebrated with great pomp and show and was said to have been grander than any other wedding in the families of the Gurus. This new city was a lovely gift to welcome the bride.

The marriage took place in 1677, but the 'Muklawa' ceremony was performed after a few years. In 1685, the whole family was invited to Nahan, a nearby hill state, by the king of Nahan, and they left for there.

Mata Sundari became a beloved member of the Guru's family and witnessed her husband giving military training to his followers. It was a new experience for her. After a few months, Guru Gobind Rai moved to Paonta, located on the bank of the River Yamuna, and built a fortress there. It was at Paonta where their son Sahibzada Ajit Singh was born in 1689. The Guru had a court of fifty-two poets and created some of his literary works here, with Mata Sundari assisting him in every possible way.

It was here that Guru Ji fought the battle of Bhangani and defeated the hill chiefs. Mata Sundari was deeply impressed by the bravery of her husband and his soldiers, and these events prepared her for the challenges that were to come.

The family then moved to Anandpur, where Mata Sundari took care of her children, including the Sahibzada’s - Jujhar Singh in 1690, Zoravar Singh in 1696, and Fateh Singh in 1699. During this time, Guru Gobind Rai created saint-soldiers by baptizing his followers and even underwent baptism himself, taking on the name Gobind Singh. Mata Sundari was by his side during this important event, and she also sweetened the water used for the creation of the Khalsa. She was impressed and happy to see the Khalsa army undergoing military training.

From 1701 to 1704, Anandpur was repeatedly attacked by the hill chiefs and the Mughal forces, but Guru Gobind Singh Ji and his Sikhs were able to repel every attack and emerge victorious. Although Mata Gujri sometimes yielded to the requests of some weak-hearted Sikhs and asked Guru Ji to give up the conflict and vacate the fort, Mata Sundari never agreed to this proposal. She was a bold and inspiring lady who showed no sign of weakness.

In 1704, Guru Gobind Singh Ji agreed to vacate the fort on the assurance by the Mughals that no harm would come to them, but it turned out to be a false assurance. As soon as the Sikhs left the fort, they were attacked. Mata Sundari, along with Jawahar Singh and Dhana Singh, was sent to Delhi in disguise. They covered a distance of two hundred miles. Meanwhile, Mata Gujri, with her two younger grandsons, left for Sarhind with a servant. Guru Ji, with his two elder sons and forty Sikhs, left for Chamkaur. This was a period of great anxiety for Mata Sundari, but she faced it bravely.

After arriving in Delhi, Mata Sundari stayed at Jawahar Singh's house in Ajmeri Gate. It was here that she received the heart-breaking news of the martyrdom of her four Sahibzadas and her mother-in-law. The elder two were martyred while bravely fighting against the Mughal forces, and the younger two were bricked alive by the chief of Sarhind for refusing to convert to Islam. Mata Gujri also passed away in Sarhind. During this difficult time, Guru Gobind Singh Ji and his small group of Sikhs faced many dangers and eventually made it to Damdama Sahib in District Bhatinda.

Mata Sundari travelled a long distance from Delhi to meet Guru Gobind Singh Ji at Damdama Sahib, where she sought confirmation of the news of the Sahibzadas' martyrdom. Guru Gobind Singh Ji consoled her by saying, "I have sacrificed four sons for the Sikhs. It does not matter if four have died as we have been blessed with thousands of sons." Despite the loss of her comforts and Sahibzadas, Mata Sundari did not complain and remained devoted to serving her husband during his literary pursuits.

However, their stay at Damdama Sahib was brief as Guru Gobind Singh Ji received an invitation from Aurangzeb to travel to South India. Mata Sundari, along with Bhai Mani Singh, returned to Delhi.

Later on, when Aurangzeb passed away, his son, Bahadur Shah, sought Guru Gobind Singh Ji's assistance in his fight against his younger brother, Azam Shah. After a fierce battle, Bahadur Shah emerged victorious and expressed his gratitude towards the Guru for his help. Subsequently, Bahadur Shah invited the Guru to stay in Delhi as a royal guest. Mata Sundari took the opportunity to bring her adopted son, Ajit Singh, who was five years old at the time, to meet the Guru. However, the Guru warned her that the boy would cause her troubles, but Mata Sundari could not let go of her affection for him.

As Ajit Singh grew older, he became a nuisance and created many enemies. He even adorned himself with the Guru's weapons, which were highly respected by Mata Sundari. Despite her attempts to discipline him, Ajit Singh attacked her with his dagger, and his ill-mannered behaviour led to trouble with the Mughal government. He eventually gave up his faith and cut his long hair in a cowardly manner. Mata Sundari was deeply upset by his behaviour and refused to see him ever again.

Heartbroken by these events, Mata Sundari left for Mathura, where she knew the ruler. She stayed there for a brief period before returning to Delhi. On her return, she took up residence in a new house that was built for her by her followers.

After Guru Gobind Singh Ji's passing in 1707, Mata Sundari took on the responsibility of guiding the Sikhs and contributed in many ways to fulfil her duty until her death. During a difficult time of division among the Sikhs, she worked to maintain unity, preventing potential bloodshed between the Bandai Khalsa and Tat Khalsa groups in Amritsar. She sent Bhai Mani Singh, a respected figure to both parties, to settle the dispute and preserve peace. The community held a deep respect for Mata Sundari, recognizing her impartiality in such matters.

Mata Sundari also worked to advance the teachings of Guru Gobind Singh, encouraging Bhai Mani Singh to teach critical appreciation and the meaning of Gurbani to the Sikhs. This resulted in the establishment of the Gurmat Institute in Delhi and later at Amritsar, which still operates today on Sattowali Street.

Despite adverse circumstances, Mata Sundari oversaw the preparation of more copies of the handwritten Guru Granth Sahib. In addition, she became a nurturing caregiver to Jassa Singh Ahluwalia, who grew up under her care and guidance, receiving blessings and some of Guru Gobind Singh's arms. With her training and support, Jassa Singh Ahluwalia became a respected leader of the Sikhs and was known as Badshah.

Mata Sundari's home was a refuge for those in need, as demonstrated by her care for Jassa Singh Ahluwalia and many others who sought her guidance and support.

The edicts or hukumnamas written by Mata Sundari to the Sikhs in various parts of India provide valuable evidence about her life. In these edicts, she often addressed the Sikhs as her beloved sons and her language was filled with love. In one edict addressed to the Sikhs in Patna, she requested twenty-five rupees to be donated for the marriage of a needy Sikh's daughter, demonstrating her concern for those in need. In another edict, she advised her followers to bridge their differences, showing her dedication to the unity of the Sikh community. Many of the edicts also requested donations for the communal kitchen, indicating that she continued the tradition of providing food for all.

These edicts also demonstrated the love and respect that the Sikhs had for Mata Sundari. In one edict, the Sikhs sent gold to her adopted son's wife. In her old age, she remained devoted to meditation and often sat in front of her husband's weapons. Before her passing in 1747 in Delhi, she sent Guru Gobind Singh's weapons to the Akal Takht in Amritsar, as per her wish. Her body was cremated near the cremation site of Guru Harkrishan Sahib, the eighth Guru, in accordance with her desire. Her small room has been preserved and a Gurdwara was constructed in her honour by S. Baghel Singh when he conquered the city. Today, the Gurdwara is managed by the Delhi Gurdwara management committee.

Mata Sundari, the serene mother, completed many of the unfinished works left by the Tenth Guru. She courageously took on the leadership of the Khalsa when the need arose and was a symbol of selflessness and austerity. She remained steadfast in the face of adversity and was a champion of truth and high values. She disowned her faithless adopted son and did not save him from the death punishment, despite her respected position among the Mughals. Her contribution to the Sikh community surpasses that of any other Sikh woman. She will always shine as a guiding star, inspiring people to follow the path of righteousness. She was truly an ideal woman, and I bow my head in reverence to the greatness of her soul.
