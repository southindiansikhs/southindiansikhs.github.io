HARDIT SINGH MALIK

Sardar Hardit Singh Malik CIE OBE was a notable Indian civil servant and diplomat who made significant contributions in his field. He was a pioneer in several domains, including aviation and sports. He served as the first Indian High Commissioner to Canada and later as the Indian Ambassador to France.

In addition to his diplomatic career, Sardar Hardit Singh Malik achieved a notable feat as the first Indian to fly as a pilot with the Royal Flying Corps during the First World War. He also pursued his passion for cricket and played first-class cricket between 1914 and 1930.






EARLY LIFE

Sardar Hardit Singh Malik was the second son of Sardar Bahadur Mohan Singh and Sardarni Lajvanti, and he was born in Rawalpindi, Punjab, British India (now in Punjab, Pakistan). The title "Malik" was bestowed to his grandfather, Sardar Khazan Singh. At the age of 14, he traveled to England where he attended a preparatory school and then Eastbourne College. He studied history at Balliol College, Oxford, from October 1912 and graduated in 1915, achieving an Oxford blue in golf.

Apart from his academic pursuits, Malik was also a cricketer, having played in 18 first-class cricket matches. He played in five County Championship matches for Sussex in 1914, and then returned to play for Sussex in 1921. He also played one match for Oxford University in 1921. Malik later played for Sikhs and then Hindus in the Lahore Tournament in India between 1923 and 1930. As a right-handed batsman, he scored 636 runs with a highest score of 106, and as a bowler, he took four wickets with a best performance of two for 92. He captained the team while at Eastbourne College and also represented Oxford University in golf.

FIRST WORLD WAR

Sardar Hardit Singh Malik was a young man from Rawalpindi, Punjab, British India (now in Punjab, Pakistan). He volunteered at the American Hospital in Neuilly-sur-Seine during university vacations. After graduating from Balliol College, Oxford, he tried to join the Royal Flying Corps (RFC) with some university friends, but was denied a commission, possibly due to his race. He served as an ambulance driver with the French Red Cross in 1916 before offering his services to the French air force. His Oxford tutor, "Sligger" Urquhart, wrote to General David Henderson, head of the RFC, and secured Malik a cadetship. On April 6, 1917, Malik received an honorary temporary commission as a second lieutenant in the RFC (substantive from April 13).

Malik trained at the No.1 Armament School from April 1917 and was appointed a Flying Officer in No. 26 Squadron on July 13, 1917. He wore a turban instead of a helmet due to his Sikh faith and later wore a specially designed flying helmet that fitted over his turban, earning him the nickname "Flying Hobgoblin." He flew a Sopwith Camel with No. 28 Squadron RFC on the Western Front from October 1917, where he was under the command of Canadian Major William Barker, who later won the Victoria Cross. Malik secured several kills and was credited with his first victory on October 18, 1917, after shooting down a German aircraft. However, during a dogfight on October 26, 1917, he was wounded in his right leg and unconscious after his plane received over 450 hits. He was hospitalized through November.

After recovering and receiving a month's leave in London, Malik rejoined his squadron in northern Italy in early 1918, where it had been assigned to support the Italian military. However, he was diagnosed with an allergy to the Sopwith Camel's castor oil lubricant and was reassigned to England, where he flew the Bristol F.2 Fighter with No. 141 Squadron RAF based at Biggin Hill. In May 1918, he was promoted to the substantive rank of lieutenant. He was posted back to France with No. 11 Squadron RAF, where he was stationed at Aulnoye-Aymeries when the Armistice was signed on November 11. By the end of the war, Malik had been credited with two aerial victories, though he claimed six victories, which would have made him a flying ace and the only other Indian flying ace of the First World War besides Indra Lal Roy. Malik was one of two Indians who survived the war out of the four who flew with the RFC and RAF during the First World War, the other being Erroll Chunder Sen, who had been a German prisoner of war during 1917–18.

After the war, Malik served as an Indian Civil Service officer and was appointed as the first Indian High Commissioner to Canada and then the Indian Ambassador to France. He also played first-class cricket between 1914 and 1930.

LATER LIFE AND CAREER

Malik returned to India after the end of World War I and married Prakash Kaur in April 1919. She was the daughter of a lawyer from Lahore named Bhagatishvar Das. The couple had three children, including two daughters named Harsimran and Veena (Raghavan) and a son named Harmala Singh Malik. Veena later got married and settled in Queens, New York. Initially, Malik considered staying in the Royal Air Force (RAF), but he gave up on the idea since it was unlikely for an Indian to get a permanent commission. The first Indian cadets were not accepted for RAF pilot training until a decade later. Malik resigned from his RAF commission on 16 August 1919. He then decided to join the Indian Civil Service and returned to England to pass the examinations in 1921. He joined the service as an assistant commissioner in Sheikhupura District in January 1922, and was promoted to deputy commissioner (officiating) in April 1926 and to deputy commissioner (provisional) in November 1927.

In the late 1920s, the Indian Sandhurst Committee was established to select Indians to become the first Indian officers in the proposed Indian Air Force. Malik was one of only two surviving Indians who had seen combat with the RAF during World War I. He played a significant role in the committee's decision to send six Indian officer cadets to England for pilot training in 1930. These men, including the future IAF Chief of the Air Staff Subroto Mukherjee, became the first Indian officers in the Indian Air Force in 1932.

Malik served as a deputy trade commissioner in London from 1930 to May 1934, briefly serving as the acting Trade Commissioner from May to September 1932. He then became the Trade Commissioner in Hamburg from 1933. In June 1934, he was appointed as a deputy secretary in the Indian commerce department and spent the next four years in India. He was then appointed as the Trade Commissioner to Canada and the United States in July 1938, serving in New York, Washington, and Ottawa until 1943. Malik was appointed as an Officer of the Order of the British Empire (OBE) in January 1938 and as a Companion of the Order of the Indian Empire (CIE) in June 1941. In April 1944, he became the Prime Minister (dewan) of the powerful salute principality of Patiala under Maharaja Yadavindra Singh, serving until Indian independence in 1947 and the dissolution of the Indian Civil Service. In 1949, he joined the new Indian Foreign Service and became the first Indian High Commissioner to Canada. He then served as the Indian Ambassador to France during the period when France was decolonising its Indian possessions in French India, including Pondicherry. He was also the leader of the Indian delegation when the United Nations General Assembly was held in Paris. In April 1956, he was decorated as a Grand Officer of the Legion of Honour by the President of France, Rene Coty.

Malik retired in 1957 and moved to Delhi. In January 1975, he was promoted to the honorary rank of Group Captain in the Indian Air Force. He continued to lead an active life until the age of 88. After a long period of illness, Malik died in Delhi on 31 October 1985, three weeks before his 91st birthday. He was survived by his wife and children. His autobiography, "A Little Work, A Little Play," was published posthumously in 2011.

ACHIEVEMENTS

 Sardar Hardit Singh Malik was the first Indian pilot to see action in World War I and was also the sole Indian survivor of the first day of the Battle of the Somme.

 He was awarded the Distinguished Flying Cross (DFC) by the British government for his service in World War I.

 Malik played a significant role in the selection of the first Indian officers for the proposed Indian Air Force in the late 1920s.

 He was appointed Trade Commissioner to Canada and the United States in 1938, serving in New York, Washington, and Ottawa until 1943.

 Malik was appointed as the Prime Minister (dewan) of the salute principality of Patiala in 1944, serving until Indian independence in 1947.

 He joined the Indian Foreign Service in 1949 and was appointed as the first Indian High Commissioner to Canada. He also served as the Indian Ambassador to France.

 In April 1956, he was decorated as a Grand Officer of the Legion of Honour by the President of France, Rene Coty.

 Malik continued to lead an active life until the age of 88 and was promoted to the honorary rank of Group Captain in the Indian Air Force in January 1975.

 His autobiography, A Little Work, A Little Play, was published posthumously in 2011.
