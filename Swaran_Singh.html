SWARAN SINGH

Sardar Swaran Singh was an Indian politician and the longest-serving union cabinet minister in India.









EARLY LIFE

Swaran Singh Purewal, born on 19 August 1907 in the village of Shankar in Jalandhar district of Punjab, India, came from a Jat Sikh family. He completed his intermediate (high school) education at Randhir College in Kapurthala and then enrolled at Government College in Lahore, where he obtained a degree in Physics with honors. Afterward, he served as a lecturer in Physics at Lyallpur Khalsa College. Later on, he studied law at the Government Law College in Lahore and completed his L.L.B degree in 1932. He then began his career as a lawyer, specializing in criminal law, and opened his practice in the nearby town of Jalandhar.

POLITICAL CAREER

THE EARLY DAYS

In the 1930s, Swaran Singh joined the Akali Dal political party and became a prominent leader by the mid-1940s. He played a crucial role in mediating a compromise between the Indian National Congress party and the Akali Dal in the early 1940s.

Just before the 1946 elections, the Panthic Party was formed with Baldev Singh as its leader and Swaran Singh as its deputy leader. In 1946, he was elected as a member of the Punjab legislative assembly and then appointed as the parliamentary secretary to the Punjab Coalition government.

Swaran Singh was a member of the Punjab Partition Committee and played an important role there. On 15 August 1947, the day of Indian Independence, he was sworn in as the Home Minister in the cabinet of the state of Punjab, which had shifted its capital from Shimla to Jalandhar.

On 13 May 1952, he resigned from his position as Home Minister when Jawaharlal Nehru included him in the central cabinet.

IN THE CENTRAL GOVERNMENT

Swaran Singh served as a prominent Cabinet Minister in the Indian government for 23 years and was the last surviving member of Jawaharlal Nehru's first cabinet. He was known for his effective debating and negotiating skills, and was well-versed in several languages. He assisted Nehru in talks with Chinese leader Chou-En-Lai on the Indo-China border issue in 1960 and was a member of the Indian delegation during six rounds of talks with Pakistan in 1962-63.

Singh's debates at the UN Security Council on the Bangladesh cause during the East Pakistan liberation war in 1971 were notable, and he proved to be a worthy match for his Pakistani counterpart Zulfikar Ali Bhutto. Singh even countered Bhutto's allegation that his hands were stained with blood by showing his clean, spotless hands. He was elected to the Lok Sabha, the lower house of the Indian parliament, in 1957, 1962, 1967, and 1972. However, he resigned from the government in November 1975.

CABINET POSITIONS

Swaran Singh holds the record for being the longest-serving union cabinet minister in India. Although Babu Jajgivan Ram holds the record for the maximum duration as a cabinet minister, which is approximately 30 years, Swaran Singh holds the record for consistent and uninterrupted membership of the cabinet in continuation.

Swaran Singh is particularly renowned for his tenure as India's external affairs minister. Additionally, he served as the president of the National Congress party twice, first in 1969 and then in 1978.

EXTERNAL AFFAIRS MINISTER

Swaran Singh is most notably recognized for his position as India's external affairs minister. He accompanied then Prime Minister Indira Gandhi on a visit to the USSR in July 1966. Later, on 9 August 1971, he signed "The Treaty of Peace, Friendship and Cooperation between the USSR and the Republic of India." This treaty provided for closer relations between the two nations in areas such as economics, politics, culture, and defense. Both nations agreed to support each other in case of a conflict with a third party. The treaty, signed by Andrei Gromyko, was binding for 20 years.

During the war between India and Pakistan in 1971, Swaran Singh led the Indian delegation to the United Nations General Assembly to explain India's stance on the conflict. George H.W. Bush, then the US ambassador to the UN, demanded an unconditional cease-fire by India. Swaran Singh responded by stating that the United States' one-sided and biased stance was surprising and shocking. He emphasized that India had been seeking a political resolution that was acceptable to the elected and recognized representatives of the people of Bangladesh.

On 16 December 1971, the East Pakistan troops surrendered to the joint forces of Bangladesh and India, who had taken control of Dacca (now Dhaka), the capital city.

SWARAN SINGH COMMITTEE

During the national emergency in India, Sardar Swaran Singh was appointed as the chairperson of a committee responsible for studying the Constitution of India in 1976. Upon the declaration of the national emergency, Indira Gandhi formed a committee headed by Sardar Swaran Singh to examine the issue of amending the constitution in light of previous experiences. Based on the recommendations of this committee, the government made several amendments to the Constitution, including to the Preamble. These amendments were made through the Forty-second Amendment to the Constitution of India, which was passed in 1976 and came into effect on 3 January 1977.

AWARDS

In 1992, Sardar Swaran Singh was conferred with the Padma Vibhushan, which is the second-highest civilian award in India.

THE EMINENT PERSONS GROUP ON SOUTH AFRICA

Sardar Swaran Singh had three meetings with Nelson Mandela while the latter was imprisoned in the mid-1980s. He was a part of the eminent persons group on South Africa, which was sponsored by the Commonwealth Institute. The group included other notable individuals such as Malcolm Fraser, former Prime Minister of Australia, General Obasanjo of Nigeria, Lord Barber who had served as Edward Heath's Chancellor of the Exchequer and was also the chairman of the Standard Chartered Bank, Dame Nita Barrow, Reverend Scott, and John Malecela, a Tanzanian former government minister. The group visited South Africa for a period of five weeks, during which they gathered information, interacted with the locals, and met with Nelson Mandela and the African National Congress (ANC). Their findings were compiled in a report titled "Mission to South Africa: The Commonwealth Report" and were published by the Commonwealth Institute.

UNESCO BOARDS OF DIRECTORS

Sardar Swaran Singh served as a member of the Board of Directors for sessions 123 to 132, from 1985 to 1989.

ACHIEVEMENTS

Sardar Swaran Singh had many notable achievements throughout his career. Some of them are:

 Longest-serving union cabinet minister in India, with a career spanning over 23 years.

 Successfully negotiated with the Pakistani counterpart, Zulfikar Ali Bhutto, at the UN Security Council during the East Pakistan liberation war in 1971.

 Signed "The Treaty of Peace, Friendship and Cooperation between the USSR and the Republic of India" in 1971, which provided for closer contacts between the two countries in various fields, including defense.

 Led the Indian delegation to the UN general assembly in 1971 to explain India's position in the ongoing war with Pakistan.

 Chairperson of the committee entrusted with the responsibility of studying the Constitution of India in 1976 during the national emergency, which led to the incorporation of several changes to the Constitution.

 Met with Nelson Mandela in prison three times in the mid-1980s and was a member of the eminent persons group on South Africa sponsored by the Commonwealth Institute.

 Awarded the Padma Vibhushan award, the second highest civil award by the Republic of India, in 1992.
