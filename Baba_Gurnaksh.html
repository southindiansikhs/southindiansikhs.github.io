BABA GURBAKSH SINGH JI

The history of the Sikh religion has been enriched by the extraordinary sacrifices of its warriors and saints. Even the Sikh gurus themselves set glorious examples of martyrdom. The first Guru to be martyred by the Mughals was Guru Arjan Sahib Ji, the fifth Guru. He had compiled the Adi Granth, which is now known as the Guru Granth Sahib, by collecting the hymns of all the Sikh Gurus before him. It is noteworthy that in Sikhism, its founders and teachers were not only great fighters and saints, but also remarkable writers and poets.

Sikh history is enriched with the incredible martyrdoms of both Sikh fighters and saints. The Sikh Gurus themselves set glorious examples of martyrdom. The first Guru to be martyred by the Mughals was Guru Arjan Sahib Ji, the fifth Guru, who had compiled the Adi Granth (which is now known as Guru Granth Sahib) by collecting hymns of all the Sikh Gurus before him. It is unique to Sikhism that its founders and teachers were not only great fighters and saints, but also great writers and poets. The hymns written by Guru Nanak Dev Ji, Guru Angad Sahib Ji, Guru Ramdas Ji, Guru Arjan Sahib Ji, Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji, and Guru Gobind Singh Ji easily surpass the criteria for the Nobel Prize in Literature. Their examples of supreme sacrifices, even after achieving so much fame, are incredible. However, only the Gurus are perfect. We Sikhs (Sikh means disciple) may surpass them in certain fields, such as literature, as fighters, sportsmen, or as martyrs, but not always in everything, as the Gurus did.

On this site, you are only reading about "Great Sikh Warriors" who fought for their freedom of religion. Many Sikhs have achieved much in other fields, but that was only possible after the Great Sikh Warriors stopped the religious persecution. Just imagine what Punjab would be like today if it weren't for the Great Sikh Warriors!

Baba Deep Singh Ji's martyrdom in 1757 at Amritsar was not the last one. In fact, it inspired thousands more. In 1757, Baba Deep Singh Ji took a vow to celebrate the festival of Diwali at Amritsar, which was under the control of Afghan Durrani (Abdali) forces. He started his march with about 500 disciples and fulfilled his vow by breathing his last at the Parikarma periphery of the Golden Temple. His martyrdom inspired countless others, including Bhai Gurbax Singh.

INTRODUCTION

Baba Gurbaksh Singh Ji was a highly esteemed and revered Singh who had the distinction of being martyred while defending Sri Darbaar Sahib. He hailed from the village of Leel near Sri Amritsar, and his father was Bhai Dasaundha Singh Ji, while his mother was Mata Lachmee Kaur Ji. Baba Ji's parents were devoted servants of Satguru Gobind Singh Ji, and Baba Ji himself received amrit at the tender age of 11, thanks to the influence of Bhai Mani Singh Ji. He spent time with Baba Deep Singh Ji and Bhai Mani Singh Ji, and as a result, became a proficient scholar and warrior.

ABOUT BABA JI

Baba Gurbaksh Singh always dressed in blue attire and maintained a strict discipline. He would wake up early in the morning, take a bath while reciting prayers, tie his turban, and offer his respect to his weapons made of Sarbloh (iron). He would wear a Sarbloh armor, multiple Sarbloh kadas (bracelets) and simarnas (necklaces). His turban had several Sarbloh toras (metal cables) tied into it. He also carried a long stick and wore a long 1.5 yard kachera (traditional underwear worn by Sikhs). Every morning, Baba Ji sat in the Diwan (congregation) at Sri Akal Takht Sahib, where he treated everyone with equal respect, regardless of their social status. He had no desire for worldly possessions or power. Whenever there was a battle, Baba Ji would take the Nishaan Sahib (Sikh flag) and lead the charge against the enemy. He would always be at the forefront with the beat of the nagara (war drum).

ABDALI'S OPPRESSION AND THE KHALSA SCATTERS

In November 1764, Ahmad Shah Abdali launched his seventh invasion of India with a force of 30,000 Afghans. At that time, Bhai Gurbaksh Singh was stationed at the holy Shrine in Amritsar. The Khalsa had scattered and some Singh's fled to the mountains, where they were robbed by local residents, while others hid near the rivers, where they were not only robbed but also attacked by wild animals. Some Singhs who stayed in Majha were handed over to the Afghans by traitors and informers. In Malwa, the Singhs had to resort to begging from house to house.

At Sri Amritsar, the Sarbat Khalsa (the Sikh parliament) gathered one day and after the night prayers, decided to abandon Sri Amritsar and leave together as a group for Malwa.

BABA JI OFFERS HIS SHAHEEDI

After the decision had been made to abandon Sri Amritsar and leave together as a group for Malwa, Baba Gurbaksh Singh addressed the gathering of Singhs. He asked if there was anyone in the Panth who would be willing to fight with the Afghans, sacrifice their life and with their sacrifice, have Karta-Purakh listen to their request to ensure the Chardi Kala of the Panth and bring an end to the dark days. He asked if there was anyone who was determined, a devotee of naam, strong in rehit, and ready to wipe out the enemy with their sacrifice. The Singhs replied that there was no one better suited for this than Baba Gurbaksh Singh himself, as no one else had more kamaiee and the power to abandon their body and go to Dargah. Baba Ji accepted the request and agreed to sacrifice himself for the Panth.

BABA JI PREPARES HIS WEDDING PARTY

After Baba Ji agreed to the request, he called out to the Singhs and expressed his desire to become a Shaheed. He asked if there were any other Gurmukhs who wished to come with him to Dargah, and if there were any Singhs who would be his Sarbaalhas (best men) in the journey towards death. He compared his sacrifice to a wedding, where the groom goes with his best man and a large baraat (wedding party), and he was going to marry the bride of Death. Hearing Baba Ji's call, many Singhs stood up and said they were waiting for his call and would join him as his Sarbaalhas. Seeing this, more Singhs also rose and joined the baraat.

Baba Ji then addressed all the Singhs and said that they would give their heads in this Dharam Yudh (battle for righteousness) and be married in this way. He encouraged them to embrace Shaheedi (martyrdom) and promised that Guru Sahib would give them all Kingships and even bigger rewards than that. Baba Ji stated that the Singh who gives his head in battle would be the King of Kings.

THE SINGH'S PREPARE FOR THE "WEDDING"

At this point, Ahmed Shah Abdali had arrived in Lahore and was preparing to continue on to Delhi. However, when he learned of the gathering of the Khalsa at Sri Amritsar, he decided to stop there and slaughter all the Sikhs on his way to Delhi.

Upon hearing of Abdali's advance, some of the cowardly Sikhs who had remained with Baba Ji at Sri Amritsar fled. Baba Ji encouraged them to leave, saying "Go now, otherwise we won't let you leave later!" As a result, only around 30 brave and fearless Sikhs remained, all of whom were chardi kala naam abhyaasee Gursikhs. They prepared for their impending deaths with the same fervor and dedication as a groom preparing for his wedding. They had new cholas sewn, with some choosing blue, others white, and others keSri. They gathered in front of Sri Akaal Takhat Sahib and eagerly awaited the enemy, standing on their toes to see if the enemy was yet visible on the horizon. They discussed how they would give their Shaheedis and stop the Afghan attacks forever in Dargah.

The following day, the Singhs all rose at amrit vela and began preparing for battle as if it was their wedding day. They sprinkled themselves with attar and rose scents, and rubbed sandal paste on their bodies, all the while singing "GhoReeaa(n)" shabads from Sri Guru Granth Sahib. They then bathed in Sri Amritsar Sahib and gathered at Sri Akaal Takhat.

Baba Ji was dressed impressively in his new attire, wearing a blue chola over his new kacherra and a very tight dumalla, which was tied specifically for battle and had many chakars and kirpaans tied into it. The Singhs then offered obeisance to Sri Guru Granth Sahib, read 5 pauRees of Anand Sahib, and did ardaas. They distributed Degh and then left as a group for darshan of Sri Darbaar Sahib, led by a Nishaan Sahib and with nagaaras beating. Baba Ji asked the Singhs to sing shabads of happiness, as this was their wedding day and the wedding party was now moving. He and the other Singhs also gave away all the money they had to the needy. Upon arriving at Sri Darbaar Sahib, they did parkarma, showered Sri Guru Granth Sahib with flowers, and then did Ardaas. Baba Ji stood before Satguru Granth Sahib and spoke the following words: "True Emperor, protector of the poor! Your Khalsa is going now to marry the bride of Death. We are going to battle the Dushts. Satguru Ji! May our Sikhee and our kesh stay intact through our final breaths."

Meanwhile, the Afghans had begun to move towards Sri Amritsar with a large army, wearing armor and metal helmets and prepared for a massive battle. They intended to kill the Sikhs but incur minimal casualties themselves, using long-range weapons such as large guns. However, Baba Ji and the remaining Singhs were undeterred and returned to Sri Akaal Takhat Sahib to listen to keertan and await the enemy's arrival. The keertanees began to recite keertan, and the Singhs asked that certain shabads they wanted to hear be sung, including the GhoReeaa(n) shabads. The entire night passed in this manner until daybreak, when the enemy had finally arrived.

THE AFGHANS ARRIVE & THE BATTLE BEGINS

The Durrani (Abdali) marched towards Amritsar with little opposition and entered the partially reconstructed Harmandir Sahib, which he had destroyed two years earlier. Bhai Gurbaksh Singh had already evacuated women, children, and the elderly from the premises, and had with him only thirty men. According to Ratan Singh Bhangu's "Prachin Panth Prakash," "Bhai Gurbaksh Singh, with garlands around his neck and a sword on his shoulder, dressed himself as a groom, his men forming the wedding party, waiting eagerly to court the bride of death." As soon as they saw the Afghan king and his army, they attacked them.

The Singhs, upon seeing the Afghan army, descended from Sri Akal Takht Sahib, bowed to their weapons (kirpans, katars, and talwars), and then grabbed them. Some Singhs took large sticks and others mounted horses. The Singhs competed to go ahead of each other and kept moving forward. They argued about who had the right to attain martyrdom first. The Nishan Sahibs were flying, and the beat of the nagaras was echoing. The Singhs were like grooms eager to marry a beautiful bride and becoming more and more excited as the time approached.

Some Singhs then ran forward and began the battle. They ran forward and embraced martyrdom, completing their weddings with death. The Singhs were surrounded on all four sides, and the Afghans began to close in. Baba Ji called out to all the Singhs, "Singhs! Step forward and embrace martyrdom! If we step forward, our honor remains intact, if we step back, they will say we are retreating! Do not look for assistance anymore, just go forward! Now go forward and give your heads!"

Upon hearing Baba Ji's cry, the Singhs charged forward. They attacked the Afghans like a lion attacking deer. The Singhs were all eager to become martyrs as quickly as possible. They had no hope for life.

Baba Ji called out again, "Khalsa Ji! Become martyrs and cross this worldly ocean! Let us go to the court of Satguru Ji and ask that the Afghans never attack here again and that the Khalsa should rule! What is the delay now? Attack the enemy!"

THE AFGHANS FALL BACK & REINFORCEMENTS

Despite the Singhs' valiant efforts to hold their ground against the Afghan army, they eventually succumbed to the enemy's attacks. Even when the Afghans received reinforcements, not a single Singh retreated. In fact, some Afghans were so intimidated that they fled the battlefield. Witnessing their opponents' cowardice, Baba Ji shouted, urging the Khalsa Ji to continue fighting this righteous war. However, the Afghans did not comprehend Baba Ji's words and were taken aback when they saw the Singhs chasing after them. The Afghans then aimed their guns at the Singhs, and many of the Singhs fell to the bullets raining down on them.

Undaunted, Baba Ji unsheathed his heavy Tegha and charged towards the enemy. He wielded his Tegha with such force that it sliced through the Afghans' armor, targeting their faces and necks where there was less protection. As a result, the Afghans began to shield their faces with their own shields.

In response, the Singhs threw their shields aside and challenged the Afghans, taunting them to do the same and face their blows without any shield. The Singhs did not fear death and continued to charge forward, without ever looking back. The Afghans fell back, overwhelmed by the Singhs' sword blows, and wondered how such a small group could cause their defeat. The Afghans were unable to withstand the force of the Singhs' attacks and began to fire bullets and arrows in desperation.

BABA JI'S SHAHEEDI

Despite being pierced by numerous bullets and arrows, Baba Ji did not show any signs of defeat or pain. His body was covered in wounds and blood was flowing out of them. However, the Singhs did not give up and Baba Ji continued to move forward with every ounce of strength he had left. Eventually, due to excessive blood loss, he became very weak and tired, but he kept moving forward.

Baba Ji urged the remaining Singhs not to shame the BaaNaa they wear and to ensure that their Shaheedis are perfect, and they fall in the battlefield. Meanwhile, more Afghan reinforcements arrived, and they surrounded Baba Ji. Despite his injuries, he fought back with his khanda and cut off the spears of the Afghans.

However, the Afghans eventually beheaded Baba Ji, and he attained Shaheedi. Despite this, he fulfilled his vow, and the Singhs continued to fight bravely against the Afghan forces.

THE AFTERMATH

As night fell, the Afghans were in shock from the brutal battle they had just witnessed. They set up camp near the Bhangi fort and quickly fell into a deep sleep. However, a few Singhs who had remained hidden in the area rose up during the night and attacked the camp. They stole horses and began to slaughter the exhausted Afghans. The remaining Afghans were unable to sleep peacefully, and at daybreak, they quickly fled the area.

That same night, a few other Singhs arrived at the scene of the battle to see what had happened. They saw the headless bodies of the Singhs and numerous Afghans. The Singhs decided to create one large pyre and cremate all the bodies together. Behind Sri Akaal Takhat Sahib, a large quantity of wood was gathered, and all the bodies were cremated together. Degh was distributed, and the Singhs celebrated as though a great wedding had taken place. The Shaheed Ganj of Baba Gurbaksh Singh still stands at that location.

Baba Ji's Shaheedi was glorious, and the Afghan power began to decline after this point in time. Within a few years, the Sikhs began to rule Punjab, and the Sikh Kingdoms were established. In today's dark period, where the Sikhs are once again powerless and attacked from all sides, do we still have Shaheeds who can sacrifice themselves and restore power to the Khalsa?

According to an old manuscript that was preserved in the Sikh reference library in Amritsar until it was destroyed during the Government of India's Army action in 1984, and which is quoted by Singh Sahib Giani Kirpal Singh, Baba Gurbaksh Singh was born on Baisakh Vadi 5, 1745 Bk, which is April 10, 1688 in the Gregorian calendar (father Dasaundha Singh, Mother Mai Lachchami). In 1698, the family moved to Anandpur, where Gurbaksh Singh took pahul of the Khalsa on the historic Baisakhi day of 1699. He completed his religious education under Bhai Mani Singh. He later joined the Shahid Misl under Baba Deep Singh and after the latter's martyrdom in 1757 at Amritsar, organized his own Jatha or fighting band. In battles against Durranis (Afghanis) and Mughals, his dera usually formed the vanguard carrying the banner and won renown for its acts of gallantry.

This was an unequal fight - thirty Sikhs pitted against thirty thousand Afghans. All thirty Sikhs were killed before Gurbaksh Singh, though throughout the battle, he remained in the forefront until he too fell. Giving an eyewitness account of the action, Qazi Nur Muhammad, the chronicler who was in the train of the invader, wrote in his jangnamah that when the king and his army reached the chakk (Amritsar), they did not see any infidel kafir there. But a few men stayed in a fortress and were determined to spill their blood and sacrifice themselves for their Guru. They were only thirty in number and did not have the slightest fear of death. They engaged the Ghazis (in Islamic terminology, a Ghazi is a Muslim person who has killed an infidel or a kafir) and spilled their blood in the process. Thus, all of them were slaughtered and consigned to the seventh [hell].

This happened on December 1, 1764.

ACHIEVEMENTS

Baba Gurbaksh Singh was a prominent Sikh leader who played an important role in the Sikh movement for independence in India. Some of his major achievements include:

 Participation in the Gurdwara Reform Movement: Baba Gurbaksh Singh played a key role in the Gurdwara Reform Movement, also known as the Akali Movement, which aimed to reform the management and governance of Sikh temples (gurdwaras) in the early 20th century. He was actively involved in the peaceful protests and agitations that were organized to achieve this goal.

 Establishment of a Sikh seminary: Baba Gurbaksh Singh established a Sikh seminary known as the Damdami Taksal in 1920, which aimed to promote the study and dissemination of Sikh scriptures and the teachings of the Sikh Gurus. The seminary played a significant role in promoting Sikhism and strengthening the Sikh community.
