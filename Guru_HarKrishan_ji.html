GURU HAR KRISHAN SAHIB JI

Sri Guru Har Krishan Sahib Ji was born on Wednesday, July 23, 1656, and was the eighth of the ten divine messengers (human form Gurus) of Sikhism. He was the second son of the seventh Guru, Sri Guru Har Rai Sahib Ji and Mata Krishan Kaur Ji (Sulakhni Ji). Guru Har Rai Sahib Ji had declared before his passing in 1661 that his younger son, Har Krishan, would be the next Sikh Guru. Thus, Har Krishan Sahib Ji became the Guru at the tender age of five on Sunday, October 20, 1661.

In human history, only a few devotees of God have achieved such a supreme level of spirituality at such a young age as Guru Har Krishan Sahib Ji did. Despite his youth, Guru Sahib delighted his disciples by commenting on passages from the holy scripture, the Guru Granth Sahib Ji. He reminded people to cherish the One God alone, asking them to discard their passions and learn the virtues of patience, charity, and love.

In 1663, when Guru Har Krishan Sahib Ji was in Delhi, a severe epidemic of cholera and smallpox broke out. The seven-year-old Guru attended to and served the suffering people with complete devotion. By his grace, the lake at Bangla Sahib provided a cure for thousands of people. While serving the sick, the Guru was himself seized by high fever and an attack of smallpox, eventually passing away on March 30, 1664, at the age of only eight years. The Guru wished that no one mourn his death, instead instructing the Sikhs to sing hymns from the Gurbani. The Tenth Nanak, Guru Gobind Singh Sahib Ji, later paid tribute to Guru Har Krishan Sahib by stating “Let us think of the holy Har Krishan, whose sight dispels all sorrow,” which is part of the daily Sikh prayer Ardas.

Before passing away, when Guru Har Krishan Ji was asked about his successor, he merely exclaimed "Baba Bakala ji," thus guiding the Sikhs to the village Bakala near the Beas river in the Punjab province, the location of the next Guru, Sri Guru Teg Bahadur Sahib Ji.

BIOGRAPHY

Har Krishan Ji was born in Kiratpur, located in the Shivalik Hills of the northwest Indian subcontinent, to Krishen Devi (also known as Mata Sulakhni) and Guru Har Rai Ji. His father supported Dara Shikoh, who was influenced by moderate Sufi beliefs, over Aurangzeb, who was influenced by conservative Sunni beliefs, in a war of succession to the Mughal Empire throne. However, Aurangzeb won the succession war in 1658 and summoned Guru Har Rai Ji in 1660 to explain his support for the executed Dara Shikoh. Instead of going himself, Guru Har Rai Ji sent his elder son Baba Ram Rai Ji to represent him. Aurangzeb kept Ram Rai Ji, who was only 13 years old at the time, as a hostage and questioned him about a verse in the Adi Granth, the holy text of Sikhs, claiming that it disparaged Muslims. Ram Rai changed the verse to appease Aurangzeb, rather than standing by the Sikh scripture. As a result, Guru Har Rai Ji excommunicated his elder son and nominated the younger Har Krishan Ji as the next Guru of Sikhism.

There is a scarcity of authentic literature providing detailed information about the life and times of Guru Har Krishan Ji. Furthermore, the available biographies about him, particularly those regarding his mother, were written in the 18th and 19th centuries and are highly inconsistent. It is known that Guru Har Krishan Ji was born in Kiratpur, in the northwest region of the Indian subcontinent, to Mata Sulakhni Ji and Guru Har Rai Ji. When Aurangzeb came to power after winning the succession war in 1658, he summoned Guru Har Rai Ji to explain his support for the executed Dara Shikoh. However, Guru Har Rai Ji sent his elder son Baba Ram Rai to represent him, and when Ram Rai changed a verse in the Adi Granth to appease Aurangzeb, Guru Har Rai excommunicated him and nominated Har Krishan Ji as his successor. Later, Aurangzeb summoned Har Krishan to his court with a plan to replace him with Ram Rai, but Har Krishan Ji contracted smallpox and died before the meeting could take place. Before his death, Har Krishan Ji uttered the words "Baba Bakale," which the Sikh religious organization interpreted to mean that the next Guru would be found in Bakale village, who was later identified as Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji, the ninth Guru of Sikhism.

LEGACY

The key points about Guru Harkrishan Ji's life:

* Guru Harkrishan Ji became the leader of the Sikh people at a very young age, which is a rare accomplishment in human history. Other Sikh Gurus became leaders at the age of 12 or older, but Guru Harkrishan was only 5 years old when he assumed the Guruship.

* During his stay in Delhi, there was a deadly smallpox epidemic that claimed many lives. Guru Ji blessed the lake at Bangla Sahib, which became a source of cure for thousands of people. Despite exposing himself to his devotees, he contracted smallpox and died, serving others selflessly and without fear for his own safety. This act of serving the sick is known as "sewa".

* Gurudwara Bangla Sahib Ji was built in Guru Ji's honor. It was the palace of Raja Jai Singh, a powerful Sikh and devotee of the Guru.

* Guru Harkrishan Ji challenged an illiterate water-carrier, Chhaju Ram, to expound on the philosophy of the holy Gita. To everyone's surprise, Chhaju Ram delivered a profound interpretation. Pandit Lal Chand, who had challenged the Guru, was deeply humbled by Chhaju Ram's wisdom and became a Sikh himself. He later escorted the Guru Sahib to Kurukshetra.

EARLY LIFE

Guru Harkrishan Sahib Ji was born on July 23, 1656, which was the 10th day of Sawan Vadi, 1713 according to Bikrami Samvat. He was born in Kiratpur Sahib to Mata Krishan Kaur Ji (Sulakhni Ji) and Guru Har Rai Sahib Ji. Ram Rai, his elder brother, was ex-communicated and disinherited for his anti-GuruGhar activities, as previously mentioned. Before his death in 1661, Guru Har Rai Sahib Ji declared Sri Harkrishan Sahib Ji as the Eighth Nanak Guru when he was approximately five years old. This decision enraged Ram Rai Ji with jealousy, and he complained to Emperor Aurangzeb against his father's decision. The Emperor favored Ram Rai's complaint and issued orders, through Raja Jai Singh, for the young Guru to appear before him. Raja Jai Singh sent his emissary to Kiratpur Sahib to bring the Guru to Delhi. Initially, the Guru was reluctant to travel to Delhi, but he agreed to the trip after repeated requests from his followers and Raja Jai Singh.

After the passing of Guru Har Rai Ji on 20 October 1661, Guru Harkrishan Ji comforted the disciples, urging them to accept the Will of the Almighty and sing God's praises instead of succumbing to despair and lamentation. As time passed, disciples from far and wide came to see the young Guru, who sat on the throne radiating a mature wisdom beyond his years. Bhai Santokh Singh likened Guru Harkrishan Ji's fame to the small yet far-reaching rays of the early morning sun. Those who visited him were imparted with true knowledge and had their heart's desires fulfilled while their sins were erased. The Sikhs recognized Guru Harkrishan Ji as the embodiment of Guru Nanak Dev Ji, seeing the same divine light on his handsome face as that which must have shone on Guru Nanak Ji's. Guru Harkrishan Ji's teachings focused on cherishing the One God and cultivating virtues such as patience, charity, and love, thereby carrying on the legacy of the Gurus.

Guru Harkrishan Ji possessed a remarkable talent for interpreting passages from the Holy Granth. His disciples were thrilled with his commentaries, as he reminded them to cherish the One God and to abandon their passions while learning the virtues of patience, charity, and love. In this way, Guru Harkrishan Ji continued the teachings of the Gurus and maintained the legacy he had inherited from them.

On Baisakhi day of 1662, which fell on March 29, vast numbers of followers gathered in Kiratpur. The festival lasted for three days, and the Guru's grandmother, Mata Bassi, and mother, Mata Sulakkhni, looked after the sangat. Among the attendees was Pair Mall of Pasrur, along with his family. His son, Khem Karan, was a promising youth. Mata Bassi betrothed her granddaughter, Bibi Rup Kaur, to him. The nuptials took place on December 3, 1662. According to the Guru kian Sakhian, Mata Bassi offered presents that included a pothi of stories from Guru Har Rai Ji's mouth and a dagger belonging to Guru Hargobind Singh Ji.

GURU JI TO DELHI

Emperor Aurangzeb was displeased by the increasing popularity of Guru Harkrishan Ji and summoned the young Guru to his Darbar (Court) in Delhi, as he had done with his father, Guru Har Rai Ji. Instead of going to Delhi himself at the beck and call of Aurangzeb, Guru Har Rai Ji had sent his elder son, Ram Rai, to the emperor's court.

Before his passing, Guru Har Rai Ji cautioned his son not to meet with Aurangzeb, who preferred Ram Rai Ji as the next Guru of the Sikhs. When a servant of Raja Jai Singh of Amber arrived with the emperor's request for a meeting, Guru Harkrishan Ji sought counsel with his leading Sikhs. With clasped hands, they replied, "We are thy servants, Lord. With thy knowledge of 'the three worlds', thou knowest best." Having made his decision, Guru Harkrishan Ji called for the messenger and informed him that he would accompany him to Delhi. On his journey to Delhi, Guru Harkrishan Ji passed through Ropar, Banur, and Ambala, often stopping to meet with crowds of his disciples who were eager to see their new Guru.

ILLITERATE RECITES SALOKS

While near Panjokhara, a humble Sikh asked Guru Harkrishan Ji to stay for a day so that followers from Peshawar, Kabul, and Kashmir could see him. The Guru Ji agreed and, while in the village, he met a proud pandit named Lal Chand who boasted of his caste and learning. He derided the Guru Ji, questioning his knowledge of the old religious books. At that moment, an illiterate water-carrier of a low caste named Chhajju Ram happened to pass by. The Guru Ji asked for him to be called by Dargah Mall.

When Chhajju Ram arrived, Guru Harkrishan Ji asked him to explain the essence of the Bhagavad Gita to the pandit. To everyone's surprise, the illiterate water-carrier eloquently explained the sacred book. This humbled Lal Chand, who then became a disciple of the Guru, along with Chhajju Ram. They both accompanied the Guru Ji on his journey to Kurukshetra. Lal Chand took Pahul and became Lal Singh, who later fought alongside Guru Gobind Singh Ji in the battle of Chamkaur on December 7, 1705, where he died as a Martyr.

GURU JI AND RANI

To test the intelligence of the highly esteemed Guru, Raja Jai Singh requested him to identify the real queen amongst a group of equally well-dressed ladies surrounding him. Without hesitation, the Guru went straight to a lady dressed as a maidservant and sat in her lap, revealing that she was indeed the true queen. This is just one example of many stories found in Sikh accounts that showcase the mental prowess of the Guru.

In another instance, the Rani herself had devised a test to challenge the Guru. She invited him to the ladies' dwelling-house, and he graciously accepted. Upon arriving, the Raja's servants greeted him with respect at the entrance to the inner apartments of the palace. As he walked past the ladies, who were adorned in expensive jewels and clothing, they bowed in reverence to him. However, when he approached a woman dressed humbly in a coarse homespun maid's outfit, he stopped and said, "You are the Rani. Why have you chosen to dress as a maid?" The Rani humbly acknowledged his perception and wisdom.

Through his interactions with common people, Guru Harkrishan Sahib Ji quickly gained more and more followers in the capital.



GURU JI AND SMALL POX

During this time, a devastating epidemic of cholera and smallpox was spreading through Delhi. Despite their caste or religion, the young Guru selflessly tended to the sick. The local Muslim population was deeply moved by his humanitarian actions and began referring to him as Bala Pir (child prophet). Even Aurangzeb, sensing the gravity of the situation, did not attempt to interfere with Guru Harkrishan Sahib Ji's efforts. However, he also did not dismiss the claims of Ram Rai.

While tirelessly serving the people afflicted by the epidemic, Guru Harkrishan Sahib Ji himself fell ill with a high fever. Soon after, he was diagnosed with smallpox and was confined to bed for several days. The disease had a severe impact on the Guru's tender body.

Mata Sulakhani, the Guru Ji's mother, was deeply saddened by the turn of events and questioned why her son, who held the seat of Guru Nanak Dev Ji and was known as the alleviator of the world's sorrows and sufferings, was now lying sick. In response, Guru Harkrishan Sahib Ji explained that sickness and disease are part of the human experience, and both happiness and suffering are a natural part of life. He emphasized the teachings of Guru Nanak Dev Ji, that everything that happens is ordained by a higher power and must be accepted. He reminded Mata Sulakkhani that one must follow the command of the divine.

Guru Harkrishan Sahib Ji was moved from Mirza Raja Jai Singh's house to a camp located on the bank of the Jamuna River. The Sikh community was confused and saddened by the Guru' Jis suffering and wondered who would succeed him on the throne. However, the Sri Gur Pratap Suraj Granth describes how Guru Harkrishan Sahib Ji comforted his followers with the following teachings:

The Gurgagaddi, or Guru Nanak Dev Ji's throne, is eternal and will continue to command increasing honor. The Granth, or Sikh holy scripture, is the Lord of all. Those who wish to see the Guru Ji should look upon the Granth Sahib Ji with faith and love, and by doing so, they will be freed from sin. Those who wish to speak with the Guru Ji should read the Granth Sahib Ji with devotion. Those who practice its teachings will attain the four most cherished objects of human life. Those who have faith will receive all blessings, while those who lack faith will acquire little. No one in this world lives forever, as the body is mortal. However, the spirit of the Guru Ji lives on within the Granth Sahib Ji. Therefore, it is important to bow one's head to the Granth sahib Ji daily, in order to overcome passions and attain liberation.

The Sikhs were moved to tears as they listened to what seemed to be the final words of their Guru. Then, mother Sulakkhani stepped forward, her eyes also filled with tears. She expressed her deep grief, saying, "How will I go on living without you, my son? I was blessed when I joined this family through marriage to the late Guru, and even more blessed when you were born. But now, I am lost in an endless sea of sorrow. Who will come to my rescue? How can a fish survive without water?"

Guru Harkrishan Ji replied, "The body is mortal, but by having faith in God's Will, you will find everlasting peace and be free from sorrow." Today, the Gurdwara Bangla Sahib in Delhi stands where Sardar Baghel Singh, the Sikh General who took control of Delhi, built it around the Princely Haveli of Raja Jai Singh, where Guru Harkrishan Ji had stayed during his time in Delhi.

THE GURU JIS PHYSICAL DEPARTURE

Guru Harkrishan Ji passed away from this world on Saturday, April 16, 1664, at the young age of 7. His body was cremated at the site where he had set up his camp to care for the sick and suffering people of Delhi, which is now known as Bala Sahib Gurdwara. He was fondly remembered by the Muslims as "Bala Pir" and by the Hindus as "Balmukand". In the daily invocation prayer (ardas) written by Guru Gobind Singh ji, Sikhs remember Guru Harkrishan Ji as the one whose divine presence can alleviate all sorrow and suffering.

As per the Guru kian Sakhian, Mata Bassi, the grandmother, requested Bhai Gurdas, from the family of Bhai Bahilo, to start a reading of the holy Granth in Guru Harkrishan's memory. Dargah Mall and Munshi Kalyan Das were sent to Punjab to convey the mournful news. They first went to Kiratpur to inform Guru Harkrishan's sister, Bibi Rup Kaur, and then proceeded to Bakala to inform Tegh Bahadur, who would later become Guru Tegh Bahadur Ji.

Diwan Dargah Mall and Munshi Kalyan Das stayed in Bakala for three days before returning to Delhi. According to an entry in the Bhatt Vahi Talauda Parganah Jind, the ashes of Guru Harkrishan Ji were taken from Delhi to Kiratpur where they were mixed with the waters of the Sutlej. The original entry is translated as follows:

"Sangat, the son of Binna Uppal from Amb Mari, parganah Miyen ka Maur, Nanu Ram, the son of Bagha, a calico-printer from Mohalla Dilwali, Delhi, Jaggu, the son of Padma, from Duburji, parganah Sodhara, and Dariya, the son of Mula, from Alipur Shamali, parganah Multan, carried the ashes of Guru Harkrishan Ji from Delhi and arrived at Kiratpur, parganah Kahlur, on the 11th day of the dark half of the month of Bhadon of 1721 Bk/ Saturday, 16th April 1664. The ashes were then immersed in the River Sutlej, and Karahprasad was distributed."
