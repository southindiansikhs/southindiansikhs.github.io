BASIC ARTICLES

The teachings of Sikhism are based on the following principles:

I. Unity of God: Sikhism believes in the existence of only One God who is omnipotent, infinite, formless, all-knowing and all-pervading, and is not born.
II. Equality: According to Sikhism, all human beings are equal and there is no distinction between them based on birth, gender, position or wealth. They are all children of God.
III. Faith: A Sikh must have complete faith in the Guru and follow his teachings. The word Sikhism literally means the way of the disciple.
IV. Love of God: In Sikhism, one can only love God by getting rid of their ego and self-love.
V. Character-building: Sikhism emphasizes the importance of developing a strong moral character and conquering the five deadly sins - lust, anger, greed, attachment and pride. Only then can a disciple progress on the path of spirituality.
VI. Nam Simran: The practice of meditation and remembrance of God's name is essential in Sikhism. It is through meditation that one can attain union with the Divine and realize the glory of the Almighty. As we think, so we become, and thus, the practice of meditation is crucial for spiritual growth in Sikhism.





ARTICLES OF 5K”S

The Panj Kakar (five K's) are five articles of faith that Khalsa Sikhs wear at all times, as commanded by the tenth Sikh Guru, Sri Guru Gobind Singh, at the Vaisakhi Amrit Sanskar in 1699. The Five K's are not just symbols but articles of faith that collectively form the external identity and the Khalsa devotee's commitment to the Sikh way of life. They were made mandatory for all Sikhs. This act created an external uniform for the spirit of the Khalsa that was easily identifiable in public.

A Sikh who has taken Amrit and wears all five K's is known as Khalsa (pure) or Amritdhari (Amrit Sanskar participant). The five Ks collectively symbolize the dedication of a Sikh who wears them to a life of devotion and submission to the Guru.

1. Kesh
2. Kangha
3. Kara
4. Kirpan
5. Kachera

KESH - UNCUT HAIR

There are several reasons why Sikhs keep their hair uncut. Historically, hair (Kesh) has been regarded as a symbol of both holiness and strength. A person's hair is considered a gift from God and should be accepted as such. Keeping hair uncut signifies the acceptance of God's gift in its natural form. Uncut hair is also seen as a symbol of leading a simple life and rejecting pride in one's appearance. It represents the desire to move beyond bodily concerns and attain spiritual growth. For a Sikh, bowing one's head to a barber would be inappropriate. Hair is a highly visible symbol of Khalsa membership and follows the appearance of the Gurus. Sikh women are also forbidden to cut their hair, just as Sikh men are forbidden to trim their beards.






KANGHA – COMB
A Kangha is a small wooden comb that is used by Sikhs twice a day to keep their hair clean and untangled. It is considered a symbol of cleanliness and is supposed to be kept with the hair at all times. The act of combing their hair reminds Sikhs that their lives should be well-organized and tidy. According to Sikh tradition, Guru Gobind Singh Ji commanded all Sikhs to keep a Kangha with them at all times. The comb serves as a reminder to maintain their hair with grace and to accept God's gift of hair as it is. Men are not supposed to shave their hair as natural growth is encouraged. During the time of Guru Gobind Singh Ji, some holy men let their hair become dirty and tangled. The Guru Ji taught that while hair should be allowed to grow naturally, it should be kept clean and combed at least twice a day.





KARA
The Kara is a powerful reminder for Sikhs to always act in accordance with the Guru's teachings. It is an iron bracelet that symbolizes the continuity of life and serves as a permanent bond to the community of Khalsa Sikhs. The Kara represents self-restraint and gentleness, reminding Sikhs to conduct themselves with grace and honor. By wearing the Kara, a Sikh is connected to the Guru and reminded not to engage in actions that would be disapproved by the Guru. Additionally, the iron material of the Kara represents God's eternal nature, with no beginning or end, and emphasizes the importance of unity and continuity within the Khalsa community. It is not worn as an ornament, which is why it is made of iron instead of gold or silver.





KIRPAN – SWORD
The Kirpan is a sword that represents the duty of a Sikh to come to the defense of those in danger. As a defensive side-arm, just like how a police officer is expected to carry a public-defensive weapon while on duty, all Sikhs are expected to wear a Kirpan on their body at all times. Its use is only allowed in the act of self-defense and the protection of others. The Kirpan symbolizes bravery and the protection of the weak and innocent. The reason for this mandate was primarily to protect the weak from tyranny and slavery, to maintain a state of harmony and security, to allow for the free development of trade, craftsmanship, arts, and literature, and to safeguard and protect the universal right of all beings to live their lives in a peaceful, stable, and sheltered environment. The word 'Kirpan' is derived from two roots: 'Kirpa', meaning "Mercy, grace, compassion, kindness" and 'Aan', meaning "Honor, grace, dignity". Thus, the word symbolizes "the dignity and honor of compassion, kindness, and mercy". In modern times, there has been a debate about allowing Sikhs to carry a Kirpan, which is considered a bladed weapon and prohibited in some countries. However, some countries allow Sikhs a dispensation to carry a shortened Kirpan that is at least 6 inches in length. Sikhs in Punjab are expected to wear full-sized Kirpans whenever practical, as mandated by Guru Gobind Singh Ji.




KACHERA


The Kachera, also known as Kaccha, was originally included as one of the five Ks to symbolize a Sikh soldier's readiness to be prepared for battle or defense at a moment's notice. It allowed for quick movement during combat. A baptized Sikh, who has taken Amrit, wears a Kachera every day. Some even wear it while bathing to ensure they are always prepared, changing into a new one one leg at a time to avoid any moment of unpreparedness. The Kachera was originally included as one of the five Ks to symbolize a Sikh soldier's readiness to quickly engage in battle or defense. The garment allowed the soldier to move freely without hindrance, and was easier to maintain and wash compared to other traditional undergarments of the time, such as the dhoti. Wearing the Kachera is a symbol of self-respect, and serves as a reminder to the wearer to exercise mental control over lust, which is considered one of the five evils in Sikh philosophy. The Kachera is designed to be practical and roomy, with an embedded string that circles the waist and can be tightened or loosened as needed, and then securely knotted. It can be considered as something between underwear and an outer garment, as it does not reveal private anatomy and looks and wears like shorts.
