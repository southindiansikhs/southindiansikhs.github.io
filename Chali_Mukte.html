CHALI (40) MUKTE

Chali Mukte is a term used to describe two distinct groups of soldiers who sacrificed their lives for the Sikh community. Literally translated, it means "forty liberated ones."





CHALI MUKTE OF MUKTSAR

In 1704, the town of Anandpur was subjected to a prolonged siege by the combined forces of the Mughals and the hill chiefs. The alliance consisted of both Muslim and Hindu troops, and their total numbers were estimated to be around one million. The objective of the siege was to eliminate the Sikh community.

The Khalsa, during their eight-month-long siege in Anandpur, had completely run out of provisions and were forced to survive on leaves and tree bark. The Mughals and hill chiefs who surrounded the city demanded that it be evacuated. They offered a deal that anyone who renounced Sikhism and declared themselves "not a Sikh of Guru Gobind Ji" would be left unharmed and allowed to leave the siege.

The Jats of Majha, led by Mahan Singh Brar, decided to take up this offer and leave to return home. They informed Guru Gobind Singh Ji that they were no longer his Sikhs and wished to leave. The Guru accepted their decision and requested that they write and sign a disclaimer stating they were no longer his Sikhs. Only forty Sikhs out of the hundreds present signed the disrespectful document and were allowed to leave Anandpur. It is worth noting that in the history of warfare, deserters have never been treated with such leniency and forgiveness.

In the district of Amritsar, in the Majha region, there lived forty deserters from Anandpur. Among the residents of the village of Chabal, there was a brave woman named Mai Bhago, who was renowned for her unwavering faith, courage, and zeal to serve the Guru. Mai Bhago was outraged by the timidity of those who, after being defeated by the prolonged siege of Anandpur, disowned Sri Guru Gobind Singh Ji and returned to their homes. She was disgusted by the shame brought upon Majha Singhs by these forty deserters and accused them of cowardice and lack of faith. Mai Bhago was determined to erase this stain of infamy from the reputation of Majha Singhs. She visited nearby villages and urged the women not to offer hospitality to the deserters who had disavowed the Guru.

Upon returning home, the forty Sikhs did not receive a warm welcome from their wives as they had expected. Instead, their wives were appalled that they had deserted Guru Gobind Singh Ji. The women of the Khalsa had already made a conscious choice to embrace widowhood, rather than see their husbands betray their Guru and walk away from their destinies.

These women had willingly chosen to bear the burden of losing their husbands, of raising their children alone, and of having their personal and economic futures compromised by their husband's absence, rather than witness them betray their faith and their Guru. The women of the Khalsa displayed remarkable courage and devotion to their faith and their leader, even in the face of immense hardship and tragedy.

Mai Bhago reprimanded and shamed the Singhs for their cowardice, and through her inspiration, she guided them back onto the path of devotion and sacrifice. She even dressed in men's clothing to rally them to return to the fold of Sri Guru Gobind Singh Ji. Overwhelmed with shame for their previous cowardly act, the Singhs followed her banner and joined in the famous battle of Sri Muktsar Sahib, which took place against the Mughal forces at Khidrana, located in the district of Ferozepur.

Anticipating an attack from Wazir Khan, Mai Bhago, along with forty Sikhs and others, positioned themselves at Mukatsar. Meanwhile, Guru Gobind Singh Ji was forced to evacuate the fort of Anandpur. Amidst the confusion, the Guru's children became separated. His two youngest sons, Zorawar Singh and Fateh Singh, were with their grandmother (the mother of Guru Gobind Singh Ji), while his two elder sons, Ajit Singh and Jhujhar Singh, were with their father.

During the Battle of Chamkaur, Guru Gobind Singh Ji's elder sons were martyred. The Guru himself was saved by a group of five Sikhs, and they evacuated Chamkaur, traveling through the Malva region while being pursued by the Mughal forces of Aurangzeb. They traveled day and night through the dense jungles, with the imperial forces constantly hot on their trail.

Eventually, they reached the village of Khidrana, where Mai Bhago and her group of men stopped near a pool or dhab. The pursuing Mughal army was almost upon them, but the forty Sikhs with Guru Gobind Singh Ji boldly challenged the host of ten thousand soldiers and fought fiercely. In this pitched battle, Guru himself supported his men by showering arrows from a nearby hill.

Despite their valiant efforts, all forty Sikhs attained martyrdom in the battle. Their sacrifice demonstrated the unwavering dedication and courage of the Khalsa, even in the face of overwhelming odds.

During the Battle of Chamkaur, Guru Gobind Singh Ji's two elder sons were martyred and he was saved by five Sikhs. After evacuating Chamkaur, he traveled through the Malva region, pursued by the Mughal forces of Aurangzeb. Guru Gobind Singh Ji and his followers reached the village of Khidrana, where Mai Bhago and the men she was leading stopped near a pool. The imperial army was almost upon them, but the 40 Sikhs fought fiercely, and all of them attained martyrdom in the pitched battle. Guru Gobind Singh Ji supported them with a shower of arrows from a nearby hill.

After the battle, Guru Gobind Singh Ji found one of the 40 Sikhs, Mahan Singh, still alive, and held him as he passed away. Before dying, Mahan Singh asked the Guru to forgive the 40 deserters and destroy the beadava letter disclaiming they were not Sikhs of the Guru. The Guru tore up the document and named the 40 brave fighters the Chali Mukte (40 liberated ones). Khidrana was then named Muktsar, the Pool of Liberation.

The names of the Chali Mukte are as follows: Bhai Bhag Singh, Bhai Dilbag Singh, Bhai Mann Singh, Bhai Nidhan Singh, Bhai Kharbara Singh, Bhai Darbara Singh, Bhai Dyal Singh, Bhai Nihal Singh, Bhai Khushal Singh, Bhai Ganda Singh, Bhai Ishmer Singh, Bhai Singha, Bhai Bhalla Singh, Bhai Suhel Singh, Bhai Chamba Singh, Bhai Ganga Singh, Bhai Sumer Singh, Bhai Sultan Singh, Bhai Maya Singh, Bhai Massa Singh, Bhai Sarja Singh, Bhai Sadhu Singh, Bhai Gulab Singh, Bhai Harsa Singh, Bhai Sangat Singh, Bhai Hari Singh, Bhai Dhana Singh, Bhai Karam Singh, Bhai Kirt Singh, Bhai Lachman Singh, Bhai Buddha Singh, Bhai Kesho Singh, Bhai Jado Singh, Bhai Sobha Singh, Bhai Bhanga Singh, Bhai Joga Singh, Bhai Dharam Singh, Bhai Karam Singh, Bhai Kala Singh, and Bhai Mahan Singh.

Etymologically, the term 'mukta' is derived from the Sanskrit word 'mukht', which means 'liberated' or 'delivered' from the cycle of birth and death. In Sikhism, achieving 'mukti' or liberation is considered the ultimate spiritual goal of human existence, and a 'mukta' or 'mukti' is a person who has attained this state of final beatitude. 'Mukta' also means 'pearl', and therefore signifies a title or epithet of distinction. It was likely in this sense that the five Sikhs who received the vows of the Khalsa immediately after the first five Panj Piare (q.v.) on March 30, 1699, were blessed with the title 'mukta', while the plural form is 'mukte'.

It is noteworthy that the forty martyrs of Muktsar, who had previously deserted the Guru out of desperation during the prolonged siege of Anandpur, earned the title of 'mukta' by sacrificing their lives for the Guru. They redeemed their past apostasy of having disowned the Guru and were inspired by the moral consciousness of their Khalsa wives.

The 40 liberated ones would not have returned to their Guru without the spiritual understanding of their wives, and they would have had to suffer for many lifetimes to repay their mistake. These women understood that it was their duty as Khalsa wives to serve the soul of their husbands and guide them to their destiny with God and Guru, no matter what the circumstances. It was the grace, wisdom, and blessings of their wives that allowed these men to be liberated, not their own actions alone. These women had the meditative discipline, trust in the divine, and strength of character to look their husbands in the eye and declare, "You are dead to us, no matter what! Go back and stand with your Guru."

CHALI MUKTE OF CHAMKAUR

The term "Chali Mukte" is also used to refer to the martyrs who fought in the Battle of Chamkaur. After evacuating Anandpur in December 1704, Guru Gobind Singh Ji and a small group of Sikhs were pursued by a large Mughal army, which eventually caught up with and encircled them at Chamkaur. The Chali Mukte were a group of forty Sikhs who were trapped with no access to food. Despite being outnumbered, they held off the Mughal soldiers and prevented them from capturing or killing the Guru. One of the Muslim generals of the Mughal army showed cowardice by hiding behind a wall instead of facing the Guru. During the battle, the Guru lost his two elder sons. Eventually, the Guru was convinced to leave with only three other Sikhs by fighting their way through the Mughal forces.
