SIKHISM

Sikhism, which is one of the youngest major world religions, originated and primarily developed in 15th-17th century sub-continental India (South Asia). The word "Sikhi" comes from "Sikh," which is based on the Sanskrit root "śiṣya," meaning "disciple" or "learner." Guru Nanak Dev Ji, who was born in 1469 in the village of Nankana Sahib near Lahore (present-day Pakistan), was the founder of Sikhism. The faith system is based on the teachings of Guru Nanak Dev Ji and ten successive Sikh Gurus, with the last one being the revealed sacred scripture, Guru Granth Sahib. This system of religious philosophy and expression is traditionally known as Gurmat (literally the counsel of the Gurus) or the Sikh Dharma. With around 30 million followers worldwide, Sikhism is the fifth-largest organized religion in the world.

The religion emphasizes salvation through disciplined, personal meditation on God's name and message. One of the significant features of Sikhism is its non-anthropomorphic concept of God, where God can be interpreted as the entire creation itself.

Sikhs are ordained to follow the teachings of the ten Sikh Gurus, which are enshrined in the holy scripture, the Guru Granth Sahib. Guru Arjan Dev Ji compiled the scripture, incorporating not only the writings of the Sikh Gurus but also selected works of many devotees and saints from diverse socio-economic and religious backgrounds. The scripture denounces caste and superstition and upholds the unity of God. Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the tenth and last human guru, decreed the text as the final guru of the Khalsa Panth. Sikhism has around 30 million followers worldwide.

Sikhism's traditions and teachings have a unique connection to the history, society, and culture of the Punjab region, which is the traditional homeland of the Sikhs and the location of the former Sikh Empire. Today, the majority of Sikhs reside in Punjab, India, and before the partition of India in 1947, millions of Sikhs lived in what is now Pakistani Punjab. In addition to being known as Sikhi and Gurmat, Sikhism is also sometimes referred to as Aad-Matt, which means the primal counsel.

HISTORY OF SIKHISM

Guru Nanak Dev Ji, the founder of Sikhism, was born in the village of Talwandi (now known as Nankana Sahib), near Lahore in present-day Pakistan, in 1469. His father, Mehta Kalu, was an accountant of land revenue in the government, and his mother was Mata Tripta. Nanak had one older sister, Bibi Nanki Ji, who recognized the Light of God in him from an early age but kept it a secret. Bibi Nanki Ji is considered the first disciple of Guru Nanak. Even as a boy, Nanak was deeply interested in religion and spirituality, and his quest for knowledge eventually led him to leave home and embark on a spiritual journey. He traveled throughout India in the manner of Hindu saints, and during this time, he met Kabir, a saint revered by both Hindus and Muslims. Nanak undertook four major journeys, known as Udasis, which took him thousands of miles across the country.

In 1538, Guru Nanak Ji chose his disciple Bhai Lehna as his successor to the Guruship, instead of his own son. Bhai Lehna was renamed Guru Angad and became the second Guru of the Sikhs. He carried on the work initiated by Guru Nanak. In 1552, Guru Amar Das became the third Sikh Guru at the age of 73. During his Guruship, Goindwal became a significant centre for Sikhism. He continued to preach the principle of gender equality, the prohibition of Sati, and the practice of Langar. In 1567, Emperor Akbar sat with ordinary and impoverished people of Punjab to have Langar. Guru Amar Das also trained 140 apostles, out of which 52 were women, to manage the rapid expansion of the religion. Before his death in 1574 at the age of 95, he appointed his son-in-law, Jetha, as the fourth Sikh Guru.

After Jetha became Guru Ram Das Ji, he founded the city of Ramdaspur which was later renamed as Amritsar. In 1581, Guru Arjan, the youngest son of the fourth guru, became the fifth guru of the Sikhs. He is credited with constructing the Golden Temple and compiling the Sikh Sacred text, adding more than 2,000 hymns to the Guru Granth Sahib. In 1604, he installed the Adi Granth as the Holy Book of the Sikhs for the first time. However, in 1606, he was arrested and fined by the new Mughal Emperor Jahangir, which he refused to pay as he believed he had done nothing wrong. His followers and a respected Sufi Saint Hazrat Mian Mir tried to intervene on his behalf, but he refused any interference and underwent severe torture. Jahangir also asked him to modify the writings of the former Gurus in the Adi Granth, which he refused to do. Eventually, he was allowed to bathe in a nearby river, where he disappeared under the water, becoming the first martyr of the Sikhs.

Guru Hargobind Singh Ji was chosen as the sixth guru of the Sikhs. He wore two swords, one for spiritual reasons and one for temporal or worldly reasons. This marked a significant change for the Sikhs as they began to build a military force and always kept a trained fighting force to defend their independence. Guru Har Rai became the next guru in 1644, followed by Guru Har Krishan, who became the guru at a very young age in 1661. Guru Teg Bahadur became the Guru in 1665 and led the Sikhs until 1675 when he sacrificed his life defending the Kashmiri Hindus who had sought his help.

Just before his death, Guru Gobind Singh Ji issued an order stating that the Sikh Holy Scripture, Guru Granth Sahib Ji, would replace the line of human Gurus and become the spiritual authority for the Sikhs. He also declared that the temporal authority would be vested in the Khalsa Panth, that is, the Sikh community.

The Sikh Holy Scripture, also known as Guru Granth Sahib, was first compiled and edited by the Fifth Guru, Guru Arjan, in 1604, although earlier gurus are also known to have documented their revelations. This is a rare example of a sacred text that was compiled by the founders of a faith during their own lifetime. What makes the Guru Granth Sahib particularly unique among holy scriptures is that it is written in the Gurmukhi script but includes many languages, such as Punjabi, Hindi-Urdu, Sanskrit, Bhojpuri, and Persian. Sikhs believe that the Guru Granth Sahib is the last and perpetual living guru.

Vaisakhi holds great significance in Sikh history, as it was on this day in 1699 that the tenth Sikh Guru, Sri Guru Gobind Singh Ji, founded the Khalsa Panth at Takht Sri Kesgarh Sahib in Anandpur Sahib, Punjab. This event is known as the Khalsa Sajna Diwas and is celebrated by Sikhs around the world. During a congregation of approximately 80,000 followers of Sri Guru Nanak Sahib Ji, Sri Guru Gobind Singh Ji, the tenth soul of Sri Guru Nanak Sahib Ji and the founder of Sikhism, made a significant demand. He entered the gathering brandishing a naked sword and with a thunderous voice, demanded a head. This was an unprecedented test for the Sikhs. Guru Sahib had demanded a head for the first time in Sikh history and that too from his own disciples. The congregation was stunned, but after a while, Bhai Daya Ram stood up and offered his head to the true Guru. Guru Sahib took him inside a tent, and after some time, came out and demanded another head with his sword dripping with blood. He repeated this process three more times and thus took five heads in total. Bhai Daya Ram Khatree from Lahore, Bhai Dharam Dass (Jatt from Delhi), Bhai Himmat Rai (Jheeor from Jagannath Puri Odisha), Bhai Mohkam Chand (Chheemba from Dwarka, Gujarat), and Bhai Sahib Chand (Naaee from Bidar, Karnataka) volunteered for beheading. Guru ji beheaded them and then revived them by administering amrit (nectar) and renamed them as Bhai Daya Singh, Bhai Dharam Singh, Bhai Himmat Singh, Bhai Mohkam Singh, and Bhai Sahib Singh, respectively.

After administering Amrit to the Panj Piare, Sri Guru Gobind Singh Ji granted them the authority to initiate others into the Khalsa Panth by administering Amrit in the same manner. He also requested them to initiate him in the same way, stressing the importance of equality between the Guru and his disciples. He also changed his name from Sri Guru Gobind Rai Ji to Sri Guru Gobind Singh Ji after receiving Amrit. This new initiation ceremony was named Khande ki Pahul by Guru Sahib.

During the gathering of 1699, Guru Gobind Singh Ji established the Khalsa Vani, "Waheguru ji ka Khalsa, Waheguru ji ki fateh." He bestowed the title of Singh (meaning "brave like lion") to all his male followers and Kaur (meaning "princess of Guru Sahib") to his female followers. He also founded the principles of the Khalsa, known as the Five Ks. These include Kes with Keski, which means to maintain uncut hair and cover it with a turban; Kangha, a wooden comb used to keep the hair clean and in place; Kara, an iron bracelet worn as a symbol of self-restraint; Kachhehra, knee-length cotton shorts worn for chastity and readiness for battle; and Kirpan, a sword used for self-defence and to protect the weak, regardless of religion, caste, or creed.
